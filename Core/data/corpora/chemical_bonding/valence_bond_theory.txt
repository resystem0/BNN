Valence bond theory, developed by Heitler, London, Pauling, and Slater in the late 1920s and 1930s, offers a quantum mechanical description of chemical bonding that retains much of the intuitive character of classical structural chemistry while grounding it in the formalism of quantum mechanics. The central idea is that a covalent bond forms when two singly occupied atomic orbitals on adjacent atoms overlap and their electrons become paired with opposite spins. The greater the overlap between the orbitals, the stronger the bond that results. This framework leads naturally to the concept of hybridization, which resolves apparent conflicts between the predictions of atomic orbital theory and the observed geometries of molecules.

The methane molecule provides the clearest illustration of why hybridization is necessary. Carbon in its ground state has the electron configuration 1s2 2s2 2px1 2py1, with only two unpaired electrons available for bonding. Yet carbon forms four equivalent bonds arranged symmetrically at the tetrahedral angle of 109.5 degrees, implying four equivalent bonding orbitals rather than two. The hybridization model resolves this by postulating that one electron is promoted from the 2s orbital to the empty 2pz orbital, giving four unpaired electrons, and that these four orbitals then hybridize to form four equivalent sp3 hybrid orbitals. Each sp3 hybrid orbital is a mathematically valid solution to the Schrödinger equation for the carbon atom, formed by taking equal linear combinations of the 2s and three 2p orbitals. The four resulting hybrid orbitals point toward the vertices of a regular tetrahedron, exactly matching the observed geometry.

The sp2 hybridization scheme applies to atoms forming three sigma bonds in a trigonal planar arrangement. In ethylene, each carbon atom uses three of its four valence orbitals — the 2s and two of the three 2p orbitals — to form three sp2 hybrids lying in a plane and separated by 120-degree angles. The remaining unhybridized 2p orbital on each carbon stands perpendicular to this plane. The sigma framework of the molecule is constructed from these sp2 hybrids: two carbon-hydrogen bonds and one carbon-carbon sigma bond. The two perpendicular p orbitals then overlap sideways to form the pi bond, which constitutes the second component of the carbon-carbon double bond. The pi bond differs fundamentally from the sigma bond in its geometry: electron density is concentrated above and below the internuclear axis rather than along it, and the bond is weaker because lateral overlap is less effective than head-on overlap.

Linear molecules and triple bonds arise from sp hybridization. In acetylene, each carbon uses the 2s and one 2p orbital to form two sp hybrids pointing in opposite directions along the molecular axis, separated by 180 degrees. The two remaining unhybridized p orbitals on each carbon, oriented perpendicular to the molecular axis and to each other, overlap with their counterparts on the adjacent carbon to form two pi bonds. Together with the sigma bond, these two pi bonds constitute the triple bond. The sp hybridization is also found in carbon dioxide, where each oxygen forms one sigma bond to carbon and one pi bond, and in molecular nitrogen, which has three bonds between the two nitrogen atoms through sigma and pi overlap of sp and p orbitals.

Resonance in valence bond theory arises when the actual electronic structure of a molecule cannot be adequately represented by a single arrangement of bonds and lone pairs. The theoretical justification is that the wavefunction of such a molecule is best approximated not by a single valence bond structure but by a linear combination of several resonance structures, each corresponding to a different bonding arrangement consistent with the molecular formula and geometry. The actual wavefunction, and therefore the actual energy and electron distribution, is intermediate among all contributing resonance structures, weighted by their relative stability. The benzene molecule, classically described by two equivalent Kekulé structures with alternating single and double bonds, is the canonical example: the actual molecule has all six bonds of equal length and uniform electron density, a fact that the resonance hybrid description captures correctly.

The concept of resonance has quantitative consequences through the resonance energy, which is the difference between the actual thermochemical stability of a compound and the stability calculated for a hypothetical compound with the geometry of a single resonance structure and no delocalization. For benzene, this can be estimated by comparing the measured heat of hydrogenation — the enthalpy released when all double bonds are hydrogenated — to three times the heat of hydrogenation of cyclohexene, which has one isolated double bond. The difference, approximately 150 kilojoules per mole, represents the extra stability conferred by delocalization. Systems with large resonance energies tend to be kinetically as well as thermodynamically stable toward reactions that would disrupt the delocalized system.

Valence bond theory also describes hypervalent compounds, those in which a central atom appears to exceed the octet. Phosphorus pentachloride and sulfur hexafluoride are classic examples. One traditional explanation invokes d-orbital participation in hybridization, with sp3d or sp3d2 hybrid orbitals accommodating the extra bonds. However, modern computational analysis suggests that d-orbital contributions are actually minor and that these compounds are better described using a three-center four-electron bonding model in which molecular orbital concepts are blended with valence bond language. Regardless of the precise description, the experimental geometries — trigonal bipyramidal for phosphorus pentachloride and octahedral for sulfur hexafluoride — are correctly predicted by either hybridization scheme, and the bonds are real and strong.

The overlap integral, a quantitative measure of how much two atomic orbitals share spatial volume, underlies the prediction that bond strength increases with orbital overlap. Maximum sigma overlap occurs in head-on interactions, where the lobes of two orbitals point directly at each other. This geometry places the highest electron density along the internuclear axis where electrostatic attraction is greatest. Valence bond theory captures this relationship between geometry and bond strength in a transparent way that molecular orbital theory, with its delocalized orbitals, somewhat obscures. The two theories are complementary: molecular orbital theory is more suitable for describing delocalized and spectroscopic properties, while valence bond theory preserves the intuitive picture of localized bonds that forms the conceptual foundation of organic and inorganic structural chemistry.
