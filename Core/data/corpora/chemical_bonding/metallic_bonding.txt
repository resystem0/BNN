Metallic bonding arises in pure metals and metallic alloys and is responsible for the distinctive cluster of properties — high electrical and thermal conductivity, mechanical ductility and malleability, luster, and high melting points for many metals — that distinguish metals from other materials. The nature of this bonding is fundamentally different from the localized electron-pair sharing of covalent bonds or the complete electron transfer of ionic bonds. In metals, valence electrons are not associated with any particular bond or atom pair but instead move freely throughout the entire solid, forming what is often called the electron sea or electron gas model.

The electron sea model, while qualitatively useful, is a classical approximation that captures the essential physics without the machinery of quantum mechanics. In this picture, metal atoms donate their valence electrons to a collective pool, becoming positively charged cation cores embedded in a mobile sea of delocalized electrons. The electrostatic attraction between the positive cores and the surrounding electron sea holds the solid together. Because the electrons are not localized to specific bonds or atoms, the positive cores can be displaced relative to one another without destroying the bonding, which explains why metals can be deformed without fracturing. When a shear stress is applied, layers of metal atoms slide past each other, but the electron sea reforms instantaneously around the new arrangement, maintaining cohesion. This contrasts sharply with ionic solids, where displacing layers by one lattice unit brings like charges into opposition and causes fracture.

Band theory provides the quantum mechanical replacement for the electron sea model and is necessary for explaining the full range of metallic properties and for distinguishing metals from semiconductors and insulators. The starting point is the recognition that when a large number of atoms are brought together to form a solid, the discrete energy levels of isolated atoms broaden into continuous bands of allowed energy levels. For a solid containing N atoms, each contributing one orbital of a given type, that orbital type produces a band containing N closely spaced energy levels. Because N is on the order of 10 to the 23rd power for a macroscopic solid, the spacing between levels within a band is immeasurably small and the band is effectively continuous.

The valence band contains the highest occupied states at absolute zero, and the conduction band contains the lowest unoccupied states. In metals, the valence band is either partially filled or overlaps with the conduction band, so there is no energy gap between occupied and empty states. This means that electrons near the Fermi level can be promoted to empty states by arbitrarily small energy inputs, including the thermal energy available at room temperature and the small energy of a photon of visible light or the electric field applied across a conductor. It is this ease of electronic excitation that gives metals their characteristic optical reflectivity and electrical conductivity.

Electrical conductivity in metals arises because conduction electrons can move freely in response to an applied electric field. Under an applied potential difference, electrons near the Fermi level gain net momentum in the direction opposing the field and constitute an electric current. The conductivity is limited by scattering events in which electrons are deflected by lattice vibrations, impurities, or defects, momentarily losing their directed momentum. The mean free path between scattering events determines the resistivity. At higher temperatures, more vigorous lattice vibrations scatter electrons more frequently, increasing resistivity. At very low temperatures, the lattice vibrations are suppressed and resistivity drops dramatically; in some metals, below a critical temperature, resistivity vanishes entirely as the material enters a superconducting state described by the BCS theory.

Thermal conductivity in metals is also dominated by electron transport. Conduction electrons carry thermal energy efficiently because they move quickly and are distributed throughout the entire sample. The Wiedemann-Franz law, an empirical relationship later given theoretical justification by Sommerfeld's free electron model, states that the ratio of thermal conductivity to electrical conductivity is proportional to temperature, with the proportionality constant — the Lorenz number — being nearly the same for all metals. This relationship confirms that the same electrons responsible for electrical conduction are also the primary carriers of heat.

The cohesive energy of a metal, defined as the energy required to atomize it into isolated gas-phase atoms, correlates with the number of valence electrons and the extent of d-orbital participation in bonding. Alkali metals, which contribute only one electron per atom to the conduction band, have low cohesive energies and melt at low temperatures; cesium melts at only 28.5 degrees Celsius. Transition metals, by contrast, have partially filled d orbitals that contribute to strong directional bonding in addition to the delocalized band contribution. Tungsten, with half-filled d orbitals and six valence electrons actively involved in bonding, has the highest melting point of all elements at 3422 degrees Celsius and an enormous cohesive energy. The relationship between d-orbital filling and cohesive energy follows a characteristic double-humped curve across the transition series, peaking near the middle where d orbitals are approximately half-filled.

Metallic luster originates from the interaction of electromagnetic radiation with the mobile conduction electrons. When light of a frequency below the plasma frequency strikes a metal surface, the conduction electrons respond collectively to the oscillating electric field of the light wave, re-radiating electromagnetic radiation coherently and with high efficiency. This collective oscillation of the electron gas is called a plasmon, and its characteristic frequency depends on the electron density. Metals with plasma frequencies in the ultraviolet reflect all visible light and appear silvery white, as silver and aluminum do. Gold and copper have plasma frequencies at the edge of or within the visible spectrum, causing selective absorption of blue and green light and the characteristic yellow and reddish colors.
