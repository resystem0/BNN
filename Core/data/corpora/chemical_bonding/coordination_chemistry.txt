Coordination chemistry concerns the formation, structure, and properties of coordination compounds, also called complex ions or metal complexes, in which a central metal atom or ion is surrounded by a set of ligands held in place by coordinate covalent bonds. The field was systematized by Alfred Werner in the early twentieth century, who correctly deduced the octahedral geometry of cobalt complexes based on isomer counting arguments before X-ray crystallography could confirm it. Modern coordination chemistry encompasses transition metal chemistry, bioinorganic chemistry, homogeneous catalysis, and materials science, making it one of the richest and most practically significant areas of inorganic chemistry.

A ligand is any ion or molecule that donates a pair of electrons to the central metal atom, forming a coordinate covalent bond in which both electrons in the bond originate from the ligand. Simple ligands such as chloride, water, and ammonia are monodentate, donating a single electron pair through a single donor atom. More complex ligands may be bidentate, donating through two donor atoms simultaneously and forming a five- or six-membered chelate ring with the metal. Ethylenediamine, which has two nitrogen donor atoms separated by a two-carbon bridge, is a classic bidentate ligand. Polydentate ligands with three, four, five, or six donor atoms form increasingly stable complexes because the chelate effect makes ring formation entropically favorable.

The chelate effect is the enhanced thermodynamic stability of complexes containing chelate rings compared to analogous complexes with monodentate ligands of similar donor ability. The origin is primarily entropic. When a bidentate ligand replaces two monodentate ligands, the reaction produces two free particles from every one chelate ligand consumed: the two displaced monodentate ligands. This increase in the number of independent species in solution increases the entropy of the system, and since the enthalpy changes for chelate and non-chelate complexation are similar, the more favorable entropy makes the chelate complex thermodynamically more stable. EDTA, ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid, is a hexadentate ligand that wraps around a metal ion and occupies all six coordination sites of an octahedral complex simultaneously, forming an extraordinarily stable complex used in analytical chemistry, medicine, and industrial processes.

Crystal field theory describes the electronic structure of transition metal complexes by treating the ligands as point charges or dipoles that create an electrostatic field at the metal center. The d orbitals of the metal, which are degenerate in a free ion, are split into groups of different energies by this ligand field. In an octahedral complex, the six ligands approach along the x, y, and z axes, and the d orbitals that point directly at the ligands — the dz2 and dx2-y2, collectively called the eg set — experience greater electrostatic repulsion and are raised in energy. The dxy, dxz, and dyz orbitals, which point between the ligand directions and belong to the t2g set, experience less repulsion and are lowered in energy. The energy difference between these two sets is the crystal field splitting parameter, conventionally designated delta-o for octahedral complexes.

The magnitude of the crystal field splitting depends on both the metal and the ligands. Ligands that interact strongly with the metal d orbitals produce large splittings and are called strong-field ligands; those producing small splittings are weak-field ligands. The spectrochemical series orders common ligands from weakest to strongest field: iodide, bromide, chloride, fluoride, hydroxide, water, pyridine, ammonia, ethylenediamine, cyanide, and carbon monoxide represent a progression of increasing field strength. Strong-field ligands such as cyanide produce such large splittings that electrons pair in the lower t2g set rather than occupying the higher eg orbitals, producing low-spin complexes. Weak-field ligands produce splittings small enough that electrons occupy the higher orbitals singly before pairing, producing high-spin complexes with more unpaired electrons.

The number of unpaired electrons in a complex determines its magnetic properties and is readily measured by magnetic susceptibility experiments. A complex with four d electrons in an octahedral environment can be either high-spin, with four unpaired electrons, or low-spin, with two unpaired electrons in the t2g set and zero in the eg set, depending on whether the pairing energy or the crystal field splitting is larger. This distinction in spin state also affects reactivity: high-spin complexes generally undergo substitution more readily because the occupied eg orbitals are antibonding with respect to the metal-ligand sigma bonds, weakening them and facilitating ligand exchange.

Ligand field theory extends crystal field theory by including the covalent character of metal-ligand bonding, treating the interaction using molecular orbital concepts. In ligand field theory, the metal d orbitals mix with ligand orbitals of matching symmetry to form bonding, nonbonding, and antibonding molecular orbitals delocalized over the entire complex. Pi-donor ligands such as halides donate electron density into the metal d orbitals through pi-type overlap, which raises the energy of the t2g set and reduces the effective crystal field splitting, placing them low in the spectrochemical series. Pi-acceptor ligands such as carbon monoxide and cyanide have empty pi* orbitals that accept electron density from the metal's t2g electrons in a back-donation interaction, stabilizing the t2g set and increasing the splitting. Back-donation is especially important in carbonyl complexes and explains why carbon monoxide binds so strongly to metals, including its lethal affinity for the iron center in hemoglobin.

The colors of transition metal complexes arise from d-d electronic transitions in which an electron is promoted from the t2g set to the eg set by absorbing a photon whose energy matches the crystal field splitting. Because the crystal field splitting is typically in the range corresponding to visible light, transition metal complexes are vividly colored. The complementary color of the absorbed wavelength is what the eye perceives. Aqueous copper sulfate absorbs red light due to the relatively small crystal field splitting of the Cu(H2O)6 complex and appears blue. The addition of ammonia replaces water with stronger-field ligands, increases the crystal field splitting, shifts the absorption to shorter wavelengths, and produces the deep blue-violet tetraammine copper complex familiar from qualitative analysis. The systematic study of these color changes and their relationship to ligand identity is one of the foundations of modern spectroscopy and analytical chemistry.
