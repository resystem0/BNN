Covalent bonding is the chemical union formed when two atoms share one or more pairs of electrons, rather than transferring electrons entirely from one atom to the other. This mode of bonding is characteristic of interactions between atoms of similar or identical electronegativity, and it underlies the entire edifice of organic chemistry as well as the chemistry of most molecular inorganic compounds. The shared electrons are attracted simultaneously to the nuclei of both bonded atoms, and this mutual attraction provides the stabilizing force that holds the molecule together.

The octet rule, formulated in its modern form by Gilbert Lewis in the early twentieth century, provides a useful heuristic for understanding why atoms form the number of bonds they do. The rule states that main-group atoms tend to share, gain, or lose electrons until they are surrounded by eight electrons, achieving the stable electron configuration of a noble gas. Carbon, with four valence electrons, typically forms four covalent bonds to complete its octet. Nitrogen, with five valence electrons, forms three bonds and retains one lone pair. Oxygen forms two bonds and retains two lone pairs. Hydrogen, as a special case, seeks the duet configuration of helium and thus forms only one bond.

Lewis structures are the conventional two-dimensional representation of covalent molecules, depicting shared electron pairs as lines connecting atomic symbols and lone pairs as pairs of dots. Drawing a Lewis structure requires counting total valence electrons, assembling a connectivity skeleton with single bonds, distributing remaining electrons as lone pairs to satisfy the octets of electronegative atoms, and then converting lone pairs to bonding pairs as needed to satisfy the central atom's octet. The process reveals the distribution of electron density within the molecule and provides the starting point for predicting molecular geometry through valence shell electron pair repulsion theory.

Bond order is a measure of the number of shared electron pairs between two atoms and is directly related to bond strength and bond length. A single bond, such as the carbon-carbon bond in ethane, has a bond order of one, a bond enthalpy of approximately 347 kilojoules per mole, and a bond length of about 154 picometers. A double bond, as in ethylene, has a bond order of two, a bond enthalpy of roughly 614 kilojoules per mole, and a shorter length of about 134 picometers. A triple bond, as in acetylene, has a bond order of three, a bond enthalpy approaching 839 kilojoules per mole, and a still shorter length of about 120 picometers. The trend is consistent: higher bond order means greater electron density between the nuclei, stronger attraction, and more resistance to bond stretching.

Resonance structures arise when a single Lewis structure cannot adequately represent the electron distribution in a molecule. In benzene, for example, the six carbon-carbon bonds are all experimentally equivalent in length, intermediate between single and double bonds, yet no single Lewis structure can show this symmetry without alternating single and double bonds. The actual molecule is best described as a resonance hybrid of the contributing structures, with the electron density delocalized uniformly around the ring. Resonance stabilization lowers the energy of the molecule relative to any single contributing structure, and the magnitude of this stabilization, called the resonance energy or delocalization energy, can be estimated thermochemically.

The polarity of covalent bonds introduces a spectrum between the purely covalent, where electrons are shared equally, and the purely ionic, where transfer is complete. In a bond between two identical atoms, such as the hydrogen-hydrogen bond in H2, the electron density is distributed symmetrically and the bond is nonpolar. When two different atoms form a bond, the atom with higher electronegativity attracts the shared electrons more strongly, creating a partial negative charge on that atom and a partial positive charge on the other. This bond dipole, characterized by a dipole moment, influences the physical properties of molecules and their reactivity.

Bond angle and molecular geometry emerge from the arrangement of bonding and lone pairs around each atom, and deviations from ideal tetrahedral, trigonal planar, or linear geometries arise systematically from the differential repulsions exerted by lone pairs and bonding pairs. Lone pairs, being closer to the nucleus and not constrained by a second nucleus, exert stronger repulsion than bonding pairs. In water, the two lone pairs on oxygen compress the hydrogen-oxygen-hydrogen bond angle from the ideal tetrahedral value of 109.5 degrees to approximately 104.5 degrees. In ammonia, one lone pair reduces the hydrogen-nitrogen-hydrogen angle to about 107 degrees.

Coordinate covalent bonds, also called dative bonds, are a variant in which both electrons in the shared pair are donated by a single atom. The distinction from an ordinary covalent bond is one of origin rather than of the resulting bond itself; once formed, a dative bond is indistinguishable from any other shared-pair bond. Coordinate bonds are central to the chemistry of Lewis acids and bases: a Lewis acid accepts an electron pair, while a Lewis base donates one. The reaction of boron trifluoride with ammonia, where nitrogen donates its lone pair to the electrophilic boron atom, exemplifies this mode of bonding and foreshadows the rich coordination chemistry of transition metals.
