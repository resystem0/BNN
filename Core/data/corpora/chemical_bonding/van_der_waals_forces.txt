Van der Waals forces are the collection of relatively weak, non-covalent intermolecular interactions that arise from electrostatic interactions between permanent or transient electric multipoles on neighboring molecules. Named after Johannes Diderik van der Waals, who incorporated them into his equation of state for real gases in 1873, these forces explain why real gases deviate from ideal behavior, why nonpolar substances condense into liquids and solids at sufficiently low temperatures, and why noble gases solidify at all. They are universal in the sense that they operate between every pair of molecules, though their magnitude varies enormously depending on molecular size, polarity, and polarizability.

The umbrella term van der Waals forces encompasses three physically distinct types of interaction, each with its own origin and distance dependence. Keesom forces, also called orientation interactions, arise between molecules with permanent dipole moments. When two polar molecules approach each other, they tend to orient themselves so that the positive end of one dipole faces the negative end of the other, lowering the electrostatic energy. This favorable orientation is opposed by thermal randomization, which means that Keesom forces diminish as temperature increases. The interaction energy falls off as the inverse sixth power of the intermolecular distance and as the inverse first power of temperature. The contribution of Keesom interactions to the total intermolecular attraction is largest for highly polar molecules such as water, acetone, and hydrogen chloride.

Debye forces, also called induction interactions, arise when a permanent dipole on one molecule induces a transient dipole in a neighboring polarizable molecule. The permanent dipole distorts the electron cloud of the nearby molecule, and the resulting induced dipole is always oriented so as to be attracted to the inducing dipole, regardless of the orientation of the permanent dipole. Consequently, Debye interactions do not average to zero with thermal motion as Keesom interactions partially do. The strength of the induced dipole depends on the polarizability of the target molecule, which measures how easily its electron cloud can be distorted by an external field. Debye forces also fall off as the inverse sixth power of distance and contribute most significantly when one molecule is highly polar and the other is highly polarizable but nonpolar.

London dispersion forces, named after Fritz London who derived them using quantum mechanical perturbation theory in 1930, are the most universal component of van der Waals interactions because they operate between all molecules regardless of polarity. They arise from the correlation of instantaneous electron density fluctuations between neighboring molecules. At any instant, the electrons in a molecule are not perfectly symmetrically distributed; random quantum fluctuations create a transient dipole that induces a correlated transient dipole in a neighboring molecule. Because the induced dipole always has the same orientation as the instantaneous dipole in the inducing molecule, the net interaction is always attractive. London derived that the interaction energy between two identical atoms falls as the inverse sixth power of their separation and is proportional to the square of the polarizability divided by the ionization energy. His expression captures the key physical insight that more polarizable molecules, those with larger electron clouds that can be more easily distorted, experience stronger London forces.

The polarizability of a molecule increases with molecular size because larger electron clouds are more diffuse and easier to distort. This explains why boiling points within a homologous series of nonpolar compounds increase regularly with molecular size: n-pentane boils at 36 degrees Celsius, n-hexane at 69 degrees, n-heptane at 98 degrees, and n-octane at 126 degrees. The branched isomers in each case boil at lower temperatures than the linear ones because their more compact, spherical shapes reduce the surface area available for close intermolecular contact, decreasing the cumulative London interaction. This shape dependence is why neopentane, the most compact C5H12 isomer, boils at only 9.5 degrees Celsius while n-pentane boils at 36 degrees.

Noble gases provide the cleanest test of London dispersion theory because they have no permanent dipoles and no possibility of other interaction types. Their polarizabilities and ionization energies are well known, and London's formula predicts their intermolecular interaction energies and critical temperatures with reasonable accuracy. Helium, the lightest and least polarizable noble gas, has a critical temperature of only 5.2 K and is the most difficult of all substances to liquefy. Xenon, large and highly polarizable, has a critical temperature of 289.7 K and can be liquefied with moderate cooling. The trend is completely consistent with increasing London forces down the group.

The total van der Waals interaction between two molecules at short range is modified by a repulsive term that arises from the overlap of electron clouds when molecules approach within the sum of their van der Waals radii. This electron cloud overlap violates the Pauli exclusion principle if it requires electrons to occupy the same spatial region with the same spin, creating a strong repulsive force. The Lennard-Jones potential, one of the most widely used empirical functions for modeling intermolecular interactions, combines an attractive term proportional to the inverse sixth power of distance with a repulsive term proportional to the inverse twelfth power. The twelfth-power repulsion has no fundamental physical derivation but is computationally convenient and reproduces the steep rise in repulsive energy qualitatively well.

Van der Waals forces are central to modern nanotechnology and materials science. Graphite layers are held together entirely by London dispersion forces between parallel aromatic sheets, which is why graphite is a lubricant and why graphene sheets can be exfoliated by mechanical force. The gecko's ability to walk on vertical glass surfaces exploits the cumulative London forces between millions of nanoscale setae on its feet and the glass surface, demonstrating that weak individual interactions can sum to macroscopically significant adhesion when multiplied by enormous contact areas. In supramolecular chemistry, van der Waals interactions contribute to the self-assembly of molecular machines, drug binding in hydrophobic pockets, and the stability of host-guest inclusion compounds.
