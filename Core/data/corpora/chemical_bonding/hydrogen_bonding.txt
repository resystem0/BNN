Hydrogen bonding is a specific and particularly influential form of intermolecular or intramolecular interaction that arises when a hydrogen atom covalently bonded to a highly electronegative atom is attracted to a lone pair of electrons on another electronegative atom in the same or a neighboring molecule. The interaction is stronger than ordinary van der Waals forces but weaker than covalent or ionic bonds, with typical energies ranging from about 5 to 30 kilojoules per mole depending on the atoms involved and the geometric arrangement. Despite this intermediate strength, hydrogen bonding shapes the properties of water, determines the three-dimensional structures of proteins and nucleic acids, and influences countless processes in chemistry, biology, and materials science.

The classical requirement for hydrogen bond formation involves three atoms: a hydrogen bond donor, which is the hydrogen-containing group, and a hydrogen bond acceptor, which provides the lone pair. The donor atoms most commonly encountered are nitrogen, oxygen, and fluorine, all of which are highly electronegative and form strongly polarized bonds to hydrogen. Because the hydrogen atom is so small and lacks inner-shell electrons to screen the nuclear charge, the partial positive charge on hydrogen in an N-H, O-H, or F-H bond is concentrated in a small volume, creating a local region of high electrostatic potential that interacts strongly with nearby electron donors. The acceptor atoms are similarly electronegative and lone-pair-bearing: nitrogen, oxygen, and fluorine again, though chlorine and sulfur can act as acceptors in weaker interactions.

Geometry is a crucial determinant of hydrogen bond strength. The strongest hydrogen bonds are nearly linear, with the donor atom, hydrogen, and acceptor atom arranged at an angle close to 180 degrees. This linear arrangement maximizes the electrostatic interaction and the degree of orbital overlap between the antibonding orbital of the donor bond and the lone pair of the acceptor. Deviations from linearity weaken the interaction; hydrogen bonds with donor-hydrogen-acceptor angles below 120 degrees are generally considered too strained to be significant. The donor-acceptor distance also matters: typical strong hydrogen bonds have heavy-atom separations of 2.5 to 3.2 angstroms, which places the hydrogen itself at a distance of 1.5 to 2.2 angstroms from the acceptor, much shorter than the sum of van der Waals radii.

Water exemplifies how hydrogen bonding transforms the macroscopic properties of a substance. Based on its molecular weight alone, water would be expected to be a gas at room temperature, as the analogous compound hydrogen sulfide boils at minus 60 degrees Celsius. Instead, water is liquid up to 100 degrees Celsius because each water molecule can form up to four hydrogen bonds: two as a donor through its two O-H groups and two as an acceptor through its two lone pairs. These four hydrogen bonds form a three-dimensional network that gives liquid water an unusually high boiling point, large heat capacity, high surface tension, and anomalously low density as a solid compared to the liquid. The tetrahedral arrangement of hydrogen bonds in ice creates an open lattice structure with large voids, which is why ice floats on liquid water — a property of enormous consequence for aquatic ecosystems.

The anomalously high specific heat of water, approximately 4.18 joules per gram per degree, reflects the energy required to break hydrogen bonds as temperature rises. This thermal buffering capacity stabilizes the temperature of oceans and allows organisms to use water as a temperature-regulating medium. The high heat of vaporization, about 2260 joules per gram at 100 degrees Celsius, means that evaporative cooling is highly efficient, which is why sweating is such an effective thermoregulatory mechanism. Both of these properties trace directly to the energy investment required to break the extensive hydrogen bond network.

In nucleic acids, hydrogen bonding is responsible for the complementary base pairing that underlies genetic information storage and replication. In the Watson-Crick model of double-stranded DNA, adenine pairs with thymine through two hydrogen bonds and guanine pairs with cytosine through three hydrogen bonds. The specificity of these pairings arises from the precise geometric complementarity of the donor and acceptor groups: each base presents its hydrogen bond donors and acceptors in a unique spatial arrangement that matches its complement but not the other bases. The three hydrogen bonds in a guanine-cytosine pair make it more thermally stable than an adenine-thymine pair, which is why DNA sequences rich in guanine and cytosine have higher melting temperatures. RNA uses uracil instead of thymine, which pairs with adenine through two hydrogen bonds with a slightly different geometric arrangement.

Proteins depend on hydrogen bonding for the stability of their secondary structures. The alpha helix, one of the most prevalent secondary structure elements, is maintained by hydrogen bonds between the carbonyl oxygen of one peptide bond and the N-H group of the peptide bond four residues further along the chain. These hydrogen bonds run roughly parallel to the helical axis and cooperate to stabilize the regular repeating geometry. In beta sheets, hydrogen bonds form between extended polypeptide strands running either parallel or antiparallel to each other. The cumulative effect of many hydrogen bonds, each individually modest, produces stable structural elements that resist unfolding. Proteins also form hydrogen bonds with water molecules in their aqueous environment, and the competition between intramolecular hydrogen bonds and hydrogen bonds to solvent is a key determinant of folding stability.

Intramolecular hydrogen bonds, those formed within a single molecule between groups on different parts of the molecular framework, can dramatically alter solubility, reactivity, and physical properties. Salicylaldehyde, in which a hydroxyl group on an aromatic ring forms a hydrogen bond to an adjacent carbonyl, is much less water-soluble than its isomer, hydroxybenzaldehyde, which cannot form an equivalent intramolecular interaction and instead hydrogen bonds more effectively with solvent. In enols, the intramolecular hydrogen bond between the hydroxyl hydrogen and a nearby carbonyl oxygen in beta-diketones like acetylacetone stabilizes the enol tautomer to a much greater extent than is found for simple ketones, where no such intramolecular stabilization is available.
