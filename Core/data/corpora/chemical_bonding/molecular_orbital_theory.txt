Molecular orbital theory provides a quantum mechanical framework for understanding chemical bonding that goes substantially beyond the Lewis model and offers quantitative predictions about bond strengths, magnetic properties, and electronic spectra. Where valence bond theory describes bonds in terms of the overlap of localized atomic orbitals on adjacent atoms, molecular orbital theory treats the entire molecule as a single quantum mechanical system and constructs orbitals that are delocalized over all the nuclei. Electrons in these molecular orbitals experience the potential field of every nucleus simultaneously, and the resulting description is both more rigorous and, in many cases, more physically illuminating.

The linear combination of atomic orbitals method, almost universally abbreviated as LCAO, is the mathematical procedure by which molecular orbitals are constructed from atomic orbitals. The core idea is that a molecular orbital can be approximated as a weighted sum of the atomic orbitals centered on each nucleus in the molecule. When two hydrogen atoms approach each other, their 1s atomic orbitals overlap. The in-phase combination, in which both atomic orbitals are added with the same sign, produces a bonding molecular orbital that has enhanced electron density between the nuclei. The out-of-phase combination, in which the orbitals are added with opposite signs, produces an antibonding molecular orbital that has a nodal plane between the nuclei and reduced electron density in the internuclear region.

The energetic consequences of these two orbital types are fundamental to molecular orbital theory. The bonding molecular orbital lies at lower energy than either of the contributing atomic orbitals, while the antibonding molecular orbital lies at higher energy. Electrons placed in a bonding orbital therefore stabilize the molecule relative to the separated atoms, while electrons placed in an antibonding orbital destabilize it. The bond order, in molecular orbital language, is defined as one half the difference between the number of electrons in bonding orbitals and the number in antibonding orbitals. A positive bond order indicates a stable molecule; a bond order of zero means no net bonding and the atoms are unlikely to remain associated.

The molecular orbital diagram for diatomic molecules of the second period illustrates these principles vividly and explains properties that simpler models cannot. For molecular oxygen, O2, the Lewis structure incorrectly predicts a diamagnetic molecule with all electrons paired. The molecular orbital treatment places the two highest-energy electrons in degenerate pi antibonding orbitals, one in each orbital according to Hund's rule, leaving two unpaired electrons. This explains the well-known paramagnetism of oxygen, which can be demonstrated by pouring liquid oxygen between the poles of a strong magnet and watching it cling there. The molecular orbital treatment thus makes a correct prediction about the electronic structure where the Lewis model fails.

The ordering of molecular orbitals in homonuclear diatomics changes in a systematic way across the second period. For molecules from lithium through nitrogen, the sigma 2p orbital lies above the two pi 2p orbitals in energy due to mixing between the sigma 2s and sigma 2p orbitals, an effect sometimes called s-p mixing or configuration interaction. For oxygen and fluorine, this mixing is less significant because the 2s and 2p atomic orbitals are more widely separated in energy, and the sigma 2p orbital drops below the pi 2p orbitals. Understanding this orbital ordering is essential for correctly assigning electrons and predicting properties.

Heteronuclear diatomic molecules introduce the complication that the combining atomic orbitals are no longer degenerate. In carbon monoxide, the carbon and oxygen atomic orbitals have different energies, with the oxygen 2s orbital substantially lower in energy than the carbon 2s. The molecular orbitals are still formed by combining these atomic orbitals, but the coefficients in the linear combination are unequal: the bonding orbital has a larger contribution from the lower-energy oxygen orbital, and the antibonding orbital has a larger contribution from the higher-energy carbon orbital. This asymmetry in the coefficients reflects the polarity of the bond and means that the electron density in the bonding orbital is shifted toward oxygen.

For polyatomic molecules, molecular orbital theory requires solving the Schrödinger equation for a larger set of nuclei and many electrons, which is accomplished approximately using the Hartree-Fock method or more accurately using post-Hartree-Fock methods that include electron correlation. In conjugated pi systems such as benzene or butadiene, the pi molecular orbitals extend over all the carbon atoms in the conjugated framework. The orbital energies and coefficients can be computed using the Hückel method, a simplified LCAO approach that treats only the pi electrons and neglects overlap between non-adjacent orbitals. Hückel theory correctly predicts the special stability of aromatic systems with 4n+2 pi electrons, encapsulating the essence of aromaticity in a simple mathematical framework.

Symmetry plays an organizing role in molecular orbital theory through group theory. Each molecular orbital must transform as an irreducible representation of the molecular point group. This symmetry classification allows rapid determination of which atomic orbital combinations are allowed to mix: only atomic orbitals belonging to the same irreducible representation can combine into molecular orbitals. In a molecule like water with C2v symmetry, the oxygen 2px orbital belongs to the B1 representation, and only hydrogen group orbitals of B1 symmetry can mix with it. The result is that even without detailed calculation, one can identify the symmetry species of each molecular orbital and understand qualitatively how the electronic structure is organized.
