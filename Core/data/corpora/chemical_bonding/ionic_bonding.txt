Ionic bonding represents one of the fundamental modes by which atoms combine to form stable chemical compounds. The process arises when one or more electrons are transferred from an atom of relatively low electronegativity, typically a metal, to an atom of high electronegativity, typically a nonmetal. This transfer produces oppositely charged ions that attract one another through electrostatic forces, and the resulting compound exhibits a characteristic set of physical and chemical properties that distinguish it sharply from compounds held together by other bonding mechanisms.

The concept of electronegativity difference is central to predicting whether a bond will be ionic in character. Electronegativity, as quantified by the Pauling scale, measures the tendency of an atom to attract electron density toward itself within a bond. When the difference in electronegativity between two bonding atoms exceeds roughly 1.7 on the Pauling scale, the bond is conventionally classified as predominantly ionic, though this threshold is a guideline rather than a sharp boundary. Sodium chloride, with sodium at 0.93 and chlorine at 3.16, presents a difference of about 2.23, which explains why the compound is paradigmatically ionic. The electron is not simply shared but effectively transferred, leaving sodium as a cation with a noble-gas electron configuration and chlorine as an anion with the same.

The energetics of ionic compound formation are best understood through the Born-Haber cycle, a thermodynamic accounting scheme that decomposes the overall formation enthalpy into individually measurable steps. These steps include the atomization of the metal, the ionization energy required to remove electrons from the gaseous metal atom, the electron affinity of the nonmetal that absorbs those electrons, and critically, the lattice energy released when the gaseous ions condense into a crystalline solid. Lattice energy is the dominant energetic term in the stability of ionic compounds. It is defined as the energy released when one mole of the solid ionic compound forms from its constituent gaseous ions at infinite separation. High lattice energies correspond to highly stable compounds.

The magnitude of the lattice energy is governed by the Born-Landé equation and its simpler approximation, the Kapustinskii equation. These expressions show that lattice energy scales with the product of the ionic charges and inversely with the sum of the ionic radii. Consequently, compounds formed from small, highly charged ions — such as magnesium oxide, where both ions carry a charge of two and both are relatively compact — exhibit lattice energies far exceeding those of compounds like potassium iodide, where the ions carry single charges and the iodide ion is large. This relationship explains why magnesium oxide melts at over 2800 degrees Celsius while potassium iodide melts at a comparatively modest 681 degrees.

The arrangement of ions in the solid state gives rise to crystal structures that maximize attractive electrostatic interactions while minimizing repulsive ones. The particular structure adopted by a given ionic compound depends primarily on the radius ratio of the cation to the anion and on the stoichiometry of the compound. When the radius ratio is between roughly 0.414 and 0.732, the rock salt structure, exemplified by sodium chloride itself, is commonly favored. In this arrangement, each sodium ion is surrounded by six chloride ions at the vertices of an octahedron, and each chloride ion is likewise surrounded by six sodium ions. The result is a three-dimensional lattice with an overall face-centered cubic geometry.

When the radius ratio falls below approximately 0.414, the smaller cation fits more comfortably in tetrahedral holes, and compounds often adopt the zinc blende or wurtzite structures, as seen in zinc sulfide. When the ratio approaches unity and the cation and anion are similar in size, the cesium chloride structure may be adopted, in which each ion sits at the body center of a cube formed by eight ions of opposite charge, yielding an 8-coordinate environment for each species. These structural differences have measurable consequences for properties including hardness, cleavage patterns, and optical behavior.

The physical properties of ionic compounds as a class are directly attributable to the lattice structure and the strength of interionic attractions. Ionic solids are characteristically hard and brittle, hard because significant mechanical force is needed to overcome the electrostatic lattice forces, and brittle because displacing planes of ions by even one lattice spacing brings ions of like charge into repulsive contact, causing the crystal to fracture rather than deform plastically. They have high melting points for the same reason. In the solid state, ionic compounds are poor electrical conductors because the ions are locked in fixed lattice positions and cannot migrate under an applied field, but upon melting or dissolution in a polar solvent they become excellent conductors as the ions become free to move.

Dissolution behavior deserves particular attention because it reveals the interplay between lattice energy and solvation energy. A polar solvent like water can dissolve ionic compounds when the energy released by solvating the separated ions — the sum of cation and anion hydration enthalpies — is sufficient to compensate for the lattice energy that must be overcome to separate the ions. Hydration enthalpy depends on the charge density of the ions: small, highly charged ions are solvated much more strongly than large, singly charged ones. This is why lithium fluoride, despite its modest lattice energy compared to more highly charged species, is sparingly soluble: the small lithium and fluoride ions are both strongly hydrated, but the lattice energy remains competitive. The full picture of solubility requires considering the entropy change upon dissolution as well, which typically favors dissolution for most ionic salts but can oppose it when ions impose strong ordering on surrounding solvent molecules.
