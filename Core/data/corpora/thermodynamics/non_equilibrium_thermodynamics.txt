Classical thermodynamics is fundamentally a theory of equilibrium states and reversible processes. It describes systems that have settled into their most probable macrostate and examines the relationships between their equilibrium properties. But the world around us is rarely in equilibrium: living organisms maintain their organization far from thermodynamic equilibrium by continuously consuming energy; weather systems are driven by temperature gradients maintained by solar radiation; electrical currents flow through wires sustaining concentration gradients. Non-equilibrium thermodynamics is the theoretical framework for understanding these systems, extending thermodynamic concepts to processes that involve ongoing entropy production.

The linear regime of non-equilibrium thermodynamics, developed primarily by Lars Onsager in the 1930s and Ilya Prigogine in the mid-twentieth century, deals with systems that are close to equilibrium — not so far that the linear response approximation breaks down. In this regime, the thermodynamic fluxes (such as heat current, matter current, and electrical current) are related linearly to the thermodynamic forces (temperature gradient, concentration gradient, and electric potential gradient) that drive them. Fourier's law of heat conduction, Fick's law of diffusion, and Ohm's law of electrical conduction are all examples of such linear flux-force relationships.

What Onsager contributed was a deep understanding of the couplings between different fluxes and forces. In a system where multiple irreversible processes occur simultaneously — for instance, both heat flow and matter flow — the flux of heat can be driven not only by a temperature gradient but also by a concentration gradient, and vice versa. These coupling effects have specific names: the Soret effect describes how a concentration gradient is induced by a temperature gradient, and the Dufour effect describes the converse. The Peltier effect in thermoelectric devices couples electrical current to heat flow. Onsager proved a fundamental symmetry of these coupling coefficients: the coefficient relating flux i to force j is equal to the coefficient relating flux j to force i. This is the Onsager reciprocal relation, and it follows from time-reversal symmetry at the microscopic level combined with the fluctuation-dissipation theorem. These relations are not just formal symmetries but have practical consequences and have been verified experimentally.

Entropy production is the key quantity in non-equilibrium thermodynamics. For any irreversible process, entropy is produced at a rate proportional to the product of fluxes and forces. In the linear regime, the entropy production rate is always positive, consistent with the second law. Onsager's minimum entropy production theorem states that in the linear regime with some forces held fixed and others free to adjust, the system evolves toward a stationary non-equilibrium state that minimizes the rate of entropy production. This provides a variational principle for non-equilibrium steady states, analogous to the minimization of free energy for equilibrium states, though it applies only in the linear regime.

Far from equilibrium, the behavior of thermodynamic systems becomes qualitatively different and much richer. Ilya Prigogine, who received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1977, showed that far from equilibrium, open systems that exchange energy and matter with their surroundings can spontaneously organize into ordered structures — dissipative structures — maintained by the continuous flow of energy and matter through the system. These structures are not in equilibrium; they are sustained by the dissipation of energy, which is why Prigogine called them dissipative. Their maintenance requires ongoing entropy production, but the entropy is exported to the environment while the system itself becomes more ordered locally.

The Belousov-Zhabotinsky reaction is a celebrated example of a dissipative structure in chemistry. This is a chemical oscillator in which reagents react cyclically, and when the reaction is carried out in a shallow dish, it spontaneously develops spiral waves and other spatiotemporal patterns that evolve periodically. The system is far from equilibrium and maintains its pattern only because it is continuously supplied with reactants and products are continuously removed. Convection cells in a heated fluid layer — Rayleigh-Bénard convection — are another example: above a critical temperature gradient, the quiescent fluid spontaneously organizes into regular hexagonal convection rolls, a lower-entropy state maintained by the dissipation of the temperature gradient.

Living systems are the most spectacular examples of dissipative structures. An organism maintains a highly ordered, low-entropy internal state by consuming food (an energy-rich, low-entropy source) and expelling heat and chemical waste (high-entropy products). The organism locally decreases entropy — building complex molecules, maintaining concentration gradients, sustaining temperature differences — at the cost of increasing entropy in its environment. This is entirely consistent with the second law: the total entropy of organism plus environment increases. Life is not a violation of thermodynamics but an extraordinary exploitation of the non-equilibrium framework.

The fluctuation theorem, derived in the 1990s by Evans, Cohen, and Morriss and then in more general forms by Jarzynski and Crooks, extends non-equilibrium thermodynamics to nanoscale systems where fluctuations are significant. The Jarzynski equality states that the exponential average of the work done on a system in a non-equilibrium process equals the exponential of the negative free energy difference between the initial and final equilibrium states, regardless of how far from equilibrium the process is or how fast it proceeds. This remarkable result connects non-equilibrium work measurements to equilibrium free energy differences and has been exploited to measure free energy changes in biological molecules — for instance, by unfolding a single RNA molecule under force and using the work measurements to calculate the folding free energy.

The Crooks fluctuation theorem goes further, stating the relationship between the probability distribution of forward-process work and reverse-process work. These theorems have deepened our understanding of the second law, showing that entropy decrease is not truly forbidden but exponentially unlikely for macroscopic systems — in agreement with Boltzmann's statistical interpretation — and providing quantitative tools for analyzing small systems where thermal fluctuations are significant.
