Heat transfer is the process by which thermal energy moves from one region to another due to a temperature difference. It occurs through three distinct mechanisms: conduction, convection, and radiation. Each mechanism operates by a fundamentally different physical process, applies in different circumstances, and is governed by its own set of equations. In practice, many engineering and natural situations involve all three mechanisms operating simultaneously, and understanding them individually is essential before analyzing their interactions.

Conduction is heat transfer through a stationary medium — a solid or a fluid at rest — by the direct transfer of thermal energy between neighboring molecules or by the motion of free electrons. In a solid at high temperature, atoms vibrate more vigorously, and through collisions with neighboring atoms, this vibrational energy propagates through the lattice. In metals, free electrons carry much of the thermal energy, which is why metals are generally better thermal conductors than electrical insulators. The governing law for steady-state heat conduction is Fourier's law, formulated by Jean Baptiste Joseph Fourier in the early nineteenth century. Fourier's law states that the heat flux — the rate of heat transfer per unit area — is proportional to the temperature gradient and directed opposite to it: the heat flux vector q equals negative k times the gradient of temperature, where k is the thermal conductivity of the material. The negative sign indicates that heat flows from hot to cold.

Thermal conductivity k varies enormously among materials. Diamond has among the highest values, approximately 2000 watts per meter-Kelvin, while air has a value of about 0.025 watts per meter-Kelvin — a factor of 80,000 lower. The thermal conductivity of still air is so low that it is air, not the solid material of insulating materials like fiberglass batts or aerogel, that is primarily responsible for their insulating performance. These materials work by trapping pockets of air and suppressing convection, relying on the poor conductivity of the trapped air to impede heat flow. Metals have conductivities ranging from roughly 10 to 430 watts per meter-Kelvin, with copper and silver at the high end. The relationship between thermal conductivity and electrical conductivity in metals is described by the Wiedemann-Franz law, which states that their ratio is proportional to temperature, reflecting the common role of free electrons in both transport processes.

Convection is heat transfer by the bulk movement of a fluid, which carries thermal energy from one location to another. Natural or free convection occurs when temperature differences within a fluid create density differences, and gravity drives the lighter, warmer fluid upward and the denser, cooler fluid downward, establishing a circulating flow pattern. The heat transferred from a warm surface to the air above it on a quiet day is primarily through natural convection. Forced convection occurs when an external agent — a pump, a fan, or wind — drives the fluid motion, typically increasing the heat transfer rate substantially. The heat transferred from a warm body in a breeze is faster than in still air because the moving air continuously replaces the warm boundary layer near the surface with cooler air from the bulk.

The governing equation for convective heat transfer at a surface is Newton's law of cooling: the heat flux equals h times the temperature difference between the surface and the fluid far from the surface, where h is the convective heat transfer coefficient. Unlike thermal conductivity, the convective heat transfer coefficient is not a material property but depends on the geometry of the surface, the fluid properties (density, viscosity, thermal conductivity, specific heat), the flow velocity, and whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. Turbulent flow enhances convective heat transfer considerably compared to laminar flow, because turbulent mixing brings cool fluid into close contact with the warm surface more effectively than the slower molecular diffusion in a laminar boundary layer. Correlations for heat transfer coefficients are among the most practically important results of heat transfer engineering.

Radiation is heat transfer by electromagnetic radiation, primarily in the infrared portion of the spectrum for objects at temperatures near room temperature. Unlike conduction and convection, radiation does not require a material medium and can transfer heat through a vacuum. The Sun heats the Earth by radiation across 150 million kilometers of essentially empty space. The Stefan-Boltzmann law governs the power radiated by a surface: the total radiative power emitted per unit area is proportional to the fourth power of the absolute temperature. For a perfect blackbody emitter, the proportionality constant is the Stefan-Boltzmann constant, approximately 5.67 times 10 to the minus-8 watts per square meter per Kelvin to the fourth. Real surfaces emit less than a blackbody by a factor called the emissivity, which ranges from near zero for highly polished metallic surfaces to near one for matte, dark surfaces.

Kirchhoff's law of thermal radiation states that at thermal equilibrium, the emissivity of a surface at a given wavelength equals its absorptivity at that wavelength. A perfect absorber is a perfect emitter. This is why good radiators of heat — matte black surfaces — are also good absorbers of radiation, and why shiny metallic surfaces both reflect and emit radiation poorly in the infrared. The wavelength distribution of radiation emitted by a blackbody is given by the Planck function, which peaks at a wavelength inversely proportional to temperature according to Wien's displacement law. For the Sun at roughly 5800 Kelvin, the peak is in the visible range; for objects at room temperature (300 Kelvin), the peak is in the mid-infrared at about 10 micrometers.

In many engineering applications, all three modes of heat transfer must be considered together and may interact in complex ways. Thermal insulation in buildings must account for the conductive resistance of the insulation material, the convective boundary layers on both surfaces, and the radiative exchange between the interior wall surfaces and between wall and roof. The design of heat sinks for electronic components balances conduction through the base material and fins, forced convection from air flow, and sometimes radiative cooling. Industrial furnaces rely on all three modes, with high-temperature radiation dominating the transfer from flame to workpiece. Fourier's law, Newton's law of cooling, and the Stefan-Boltzmann law, together with the principles of fluid dynamics and mass conservation, constitute the governing equations for these analyses, and their solutions range from simple algebraic estimates to elaborate finite-element simulations.
