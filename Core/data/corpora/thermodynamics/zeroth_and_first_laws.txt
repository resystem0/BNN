Thermodynamics is the branch of physics that deals with heat, work, temperature, and their relationship to energy, entropy, and the physical properties of matter. Its four laws form the bedrock of our understanding of energy transformations and set fundamental limits on what physical processes are possible. The zeroth and first laws together establish the concepts of thermal equilibrium and internal energy, providing the conceptual foundation on which the rest of thermodynamics is built.

The zeroth law of thermodynamics, so named because its foundational importance was recognized after the first and second laws were already established and numbered, states that if two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, then they are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This law is simple to state but profound in its implications. Thermal equilibrium is the condition in which two systems in thermal contact — meaning they can exchange heat but not work or matter — no longer exchange net energy. When equilibrium is reached, both systems share a common property, and that property is what we call temperature. The zeroth law therefore gives us the right to define temperature as a consistent, transitive property: if A has the same temperature as C, and B has the same temperature as C, then A and B have the same temperature. Without this law, the very concept of a thermometer would be logically undermined, because a thermometer works by reaching thermal equilibrium with whatever it measures.

Temperature in this context is an empirical quantity at first, defined by reference to specific thermometric properties like the expansion of mercury or the electrical resistance of a platinum wire. Different empirical temperature scales can be constructed based on different thermometric properties, and they generally agree with each other only approximately. The development of the ideal gas temperature scale, based on the behavior of a dilute gas at low pressures, provides a more theoretically principled definition. The absolute or Kelvin scale goes further, setting a natural zero point at absolute zero, the temperature at which a classical ideal gas would have zero pressure and zero thermal energy. Absolute zero is approximately minus 273.15 degrees Celsius or zero Kelvin. The third law of thermodynamics states that absolute zero cannot be reached in a finite number of steps.

The first law of thermodynamics is a statement of energy conservation. It asserts that the change in the internal energy of a system equals the heat added to the system minus the work done by the system on its surroundings. Written as an equation: the change in U equals Q minus W, where U is the internal energy, Q is heat absorbed, and W is work done by the system. This law unifies the concepts of heat and mechanical work as two different forms of energy transfer and establishes that internal energy is a well-defined state function — its value depends only on the current state of the system, not on how the system arrived at that state.

Internal energy encompasses all the microscopic forms of energy within a system: the kinetic energies of the constituent particles, the potential energies associated with inter-particle forces, and in some contexts the internal energies of the particles themselves (electronic, nuclear). For an ideal gas, internal energy depends only on temperature, a consequence of the fact that ideal gas molecules do not interact except through elastic collisions. For real gases and other systems, the internal energy also depends on volume or pressure.

Heat and work are both forms of energy transfer, but they differ in character. Work involves an organized, macroscopic energy transfer — when a gas expands against a piston, the molecules collectively push the piston, doing work. Heat involves a disorganized energy transfer driven by temperature difference — when a hot block contacts a cold block, the faster-moving molecules in the hot block transfer energy to the slower-moving molecules in the cold block through collisions at the contact surface. This distinction between organized and disorganized energy transfer will become important when we consider the second law.

The historical development of the first law is closely tied to the work of James Prescott Joule in the 1840s. Joule performed a series of meticulous experiments demonstrating that a specific amount of mechanical work always produces the same amount of temperature rise in a given quantity of water, regardless of how the work is delivered — by a falling weight, by electric current, by friction. This mechanical equivalent of heat established that heat and work are interconvertible forms of energy and demolished the caloric theory, which had held that heat was a conserved fluid substance distinct from mechanical energy. Joule's experiments, combined with the theoretical work of Rudolf Clausius and William Thomson (Lord Kelvin), led to the formulation of the first law in its modern form.

An important consequence of the first law is that the internal energy is a state function while heat and work are not. The internal energy change between two states is the same regardless of the path taken — reversible or irreversible, fast or slow. But the amounts of heat and work exchanged depend on the path. This is because Q and W are not properties of the state of the system but properties of the process by which the system changes. A useful analogy is that internal energy is like altitude — fixed for a given location — while heat and work are like the uphill and downhill components of a path between two altitudes, which vary depending on which route is taken.

The first law also applies to open systems that exchange matter with their surroundings, but in that case additional terms accounting for the energy carried in or out by matter flows must be included. Chemical thermodynamics extends the first law to systems where chemical reactions occur, introducing the concept of enthalpy, defined as H equals U plus PV, which is the natural quantity to work with in processes at constant pressure. The enthalpy change in a reaction equals the heat exchanged at constant pressure, and the vast databases of enthalpy of formation for chemical compounds allow chemists to calculate the heat released or absorbed in virtually any chemical reaction.
