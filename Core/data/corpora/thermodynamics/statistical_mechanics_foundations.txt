Statistical mechanics is the branch of theoretical physics that explains the thermodynamic properties of macroscopic systems in terms of the collective behavior of their microscopic constituents. Rather than tracking the motion of every individual molecule — an impossible task for any system containing more than a few particles — statistical mechanics works with probability distributions over the possible microscopic states of a system and derives macroscopic quantities as statistical averages. The foundations of the subject were laid by James Clerk Maxwell, Ludwig Boltzmann, and Josiah Willard Gibbs in the second half of the nineteenth century.

The central concept is the microstate, a complete specification of the microscopic configuration of the system. For a classical gas, a microstate specifies the position and momentum of every molecule. For a quantum system, a microstate is a quantum state of the full many-body system. A macrostate, by contrast, specifies only a small number of macroscopic quantities — total energy, volume, number of particles, and perhaps a few others. The fundamental postulate of statistical mechanics, which cannot itself be derived from more basic principles but is amply confirmed by its consequences, is the principle of equal a priori probabilities: for an isolated system in equilibrium with fixed energy, all accessible microstates are equally probable.

This simple postulate has enormous consequences. For a macrostate characterized by total energy U, volume V, and number of particles N, the number of microstates that correspond to that macrostate is called the multiplicity or statistical weight, denoted omega. Boltzmann showed that the entropy of the macrostate is S equals k times the natural logarithm of omega, where k is Boltzmann's constant, approximately 1.38 times 10 to the minus-23 joules per Kelvin. This equation, engraved on Boltzmann's tombstone in Vienna, is one of the most important in physics. It connects the macroscopic thermodynamic quantity entropy to a microscopic count of possibilities.

When two systems are allowed to exchange energy, they evolve toward the macrostate that maximizes total multiplicity — equivalently, that maximizes total entropy — subject to the constraint that total energy is fixed. This is the statistical mechanical basis for the second law. The overwhelming majority of microstates of the combined system correspond to the high-entropy, equilibrium macrostate, so the system is overwhelmingly likely to be found there. The temperature, from the statistical mechanics perspective, emerges as the parameter that adjusts so that the entropy gain of a system receiving a small amount of energy equals the entropy loss of the system giving up that energy. Specifically, one over T equals the partial derivative of S with respect to U at fixed V and N.

The partition function is the central computational object in equilibrium statistical mechanics. For a system in contact with a thermal reservoir at temperature T — the canonical ensemble, appropriate for a system that can exchange energy but not particles with its environment — the probability of the system being in microstate i with energy E-i is given by the Boltzmann distribution: P-i equals one over Z times the exponential of negative E-i over k-T, where Z is the partition function defined as the sum over all states i of the exponential of negative E-i over k-T. The partition function encodes all thermodynamic properties of the system. The Helmholtz free energy F is related to Z by F equals negative k-T times the natural logarithm of Z. All other thermodynamic quantities follow by differentiation: the entropy is minus the partial derivative of F with respect to T; the pressure is minus the partial derivative of F with respect to V; the internal energy is the partial derivative of the logarithm of Z with respect to negative one over k-T.

The Boltzmann distribution tells us that at low temperatures, the system is overwhelmingly likely to be in its lowest-energy states, and at high temperatures, all states are roughly equally probable. This is why matter becomes ordered at low temperature (crystals, magnets, superfluids) and disordered at high temperature (gases, paramagnets, normal fluids). The competition between energy, which favors ordered low-energy states, and entropy, which favors disordered high-multiplicity states, is the fundamental driver of all phase transitions.

The grand canonical ensemble extends the formalism to systems that can exchange both energy and particles with a reservoir. The chemical potential mu appears as the thermodynamic conjugate of particle number, analogous to temperature being the conjugate of energy. The grand partition function sums over all states with all possible particle numbers, weighted by Boltzmann factors that include a term proportional to mu times the particle number. This ensemble is natural for open systems and is essential for treating quantum statistical mechanics, where particles are identical and the distinction between exchanged and not-exchanged particles requires careful treatment.

Quantum statistical mechanics comes in two flavors depending on the quantum statistics of the particles. Fermions, particles with half-integer spin, obey the Pauli exclusion principle: no two fermions can occupy the same quantum state. Their distribution function is the Fermi-Dirac distribution, which at zero temperature fills all states up to the Fermi energy and is zero above. At high temperatures, the Fermi-Dirac distribution approaches the classical Boltzmann distribution. Bosons, particles with integer spin, can and in fact prefer to occupy the same state — multiple bosons in one state contribute more to the partition function than if distributed. Their distribution is the Bose-Einstein distribution, which at low temperatures for massive bosons leads to Bose-Einstein condensation, where a macroscopic fraction of particles occupy the ground state simultaneously. The photons in a blackbody radiation field follow Bose-Einstein statistics, and the resulting Planck distribution of radiation intensities matches observations perfectly, resolving the ultraviolet catastrophe of classical physics and historically initiating quantum theory.

Statistical mechanics not only derives classical thermodynamics from microscopic principles but extends it. It can compute quantities that classical thermodynamics cannot, such as the equation of state of a real gas from a knowledge of interparticle potentials, the heat capacity of solids from phonon distributions, or the magnetic susceptibility of materials from spin models. It also explains fluctuations — deviations of macroscopic quantities from their averages — which are negligible for large systems but important for small systems and near phase transitions.
