The Carnot cycle is the theoretical ideal for a heat engine operating between two thermal reservoirs at fixed temperatures, and it occupies a central place in the history and logic of thermodynamics. Conceived by Sadi Carnot in 1824, long before the second law was formally stated or entropy defined, the Carnot cycle established the fundamental efficiency limit for all heat engines and laid the groundwork for the thermodynamic thinking of the following decades. Understanding the Carnot cycle requires grasping both its mechanics and its deeper significance as a statement about the nature of irreversibility.

A heat engine is a device that absorbs heat from a hot reservoir, converts some of it into work, and rejects the remainder to a cold reservoir. The thermal efficiency of a heat engine is defined as the ratio of the work output to the heat absorbed from the hot reservoir. A perfectly efficient engine would convert all absorbed heat into work, with no heat rejected. Carnot's great insight was to prove that no heat engine operating between reservoirs at temperatures T-hot and T-cold can be more efficient than a specific ideal engine — the Carnot engine — and that all reversible engines operating between the same two temperatures have the same efficiency, given by one minus T-cold divided by T-hot, where the temperatures are measured on the absolute (Kelvin) scale.

The Carnot cycle itself consists of four reversible steps, each of which is an idealization. The first step is isothermal expansion: the working fluid (a gas, in the classical description) expands in contact with the hot reservoir at temperature T-hot, absorbing heat Q-hot. Because the expansion is isothermal, the temperature of the gas remains constant, and the expansion must be quasi-static — infinitely slow so that the gas is always in equilibrium with the reservoir. The second step is adiabatic expansion: the gas is thermally isolated and allowed to expand further, doing work and cooling to the temperature T-cold. No heat is exchanged in this step. The third step is isothermal compression: the gas is compressed in contact with the cold reservoir at T-cold, rejecting heat Q-cold. The fourth step is adiabatic compression: the gas is thermally isolated and compressed back to its original temperature T-hot, completing the cycle.

The net work done by the engine over one cycle equals Q-hot minus Q-cold, the difference between heat absorbed from the hot reservoir and heat rejected to the cold reservoir. The efficiency is therefore one minus Q-cold over Q-hot. For a reversible cycle, the ratio Q-cold over Q-hot equals T-cold over T-hot, which gives the Carnot efficiency of one minus T-cold over T-hot. This equality of heat ratios and temperature ratios for reversible cycles is not a trivial algebraic coincidence but a deep thermodynamic result that in fact defines the absolute temperature scale.

Carnot's proof that no engine can exceed this efficiency is a proof by contradiction using the second law. Suppose an engine E could exceed the Carnot efficiency, converting a greater fraction of Q-hot into work. Use the work output of E to run a Carnot engine in reverse — as a refrigerator — pumping heat from the cold reservoir to the hot reservoir. Because E is more efficient than the Carnot engine, the work it produces is more than enough to run the reversed Carnot engine, and the combined system would absorb a net amount of heat from the cold reservoir and deposit it in the hot reservoir with no other effect. This would violate the Clausius statement of the second law: heat does not spontaneously flow from cold to hot. Therefore, no engine can exceed the Carnot efficiency.

The same argument shows that all reversible engines operating between the same two temperatures must have the same efficiency. If one reversible engine had higher efficiency than another, we could use the surplus work to run the less efficient one in reverse, again creating a device that spontaneously moves heat from cold to hot. Since no reversible engine exceeds the Carnot efficiency and since a reversible Carnot engine achieves it, the Carnot efficiency is the maximum possible.

What makes an engine reversible in the thermodynamic sense? A process is thermodynamically reversible if it can be run in either direction without net change to the universe — without net entropy production. This requires that the process be quasi-static (always infinitely close to equilibrium) and involve no friction, no heat exchange across finite temperature differences, and no other sources of irreversibility. Real engines are irreversible because heat transfer necessarily occurs across temperature gradients, moving parts have friction, and processes happen at finite rates rather than infinitely slowly. Every source of irreversibility reduces efficiency below the Carnot limit.

The practical significance of the Carnot efficiency is twofold. First, it gives engineers an absolute ceiling on the efficiency of any real heat engine operating between given temperatures. A steam power plant with steam at 600 Kelvin and cooling water at 300 Kelvin cannot, by any means, achieve an efficiency greater than one minus 300 over 600, which is 50 percent. Real plants achieve considerably less due to irreversibilities. Second, it shows that the way to improve efficiency is to increase the temperature ratio T-hot over T-cold. This is why modern power plants operate at the highest temperatures that materials can withstand and reject heat at the lowest available temperature.

The Carnot cycle also applies in reverse as a refrigerator or heat pump. A refrigerator absorbs heat from a cold reservoir (the interior of the refrigerator) and rejects heat to the hot reservoir (the room), requiring work input. The coefficient of performance of a refrigerator is the heat extracted from the cold reservoir divided by the work input. For a Carnot refrigerator, this is T-cold divided by the quantity T-hot minus T-cold. A heat pump does the same thing but is evaluated by the heat delivered to the hot reservoir divided by work input, giving T-hot over the quantity T-hot minus T-cold. Both coefficients of performance can exceed one — and typically do — because the goal is not to create energy but to move it. The Carnot bounds on these coefficients are, again, the maximum achievable by any real device operating between the same temperatures, and irreversibilities in real refrigerators and heat pumps reduce performance below these bounds.
