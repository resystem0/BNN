Phase transitions are among the most dramatic and interesting phenomena in all of physics. When water boils, when iron magnetizes, when a superconductor loses its electrical resistance — in each case a system undergoes a qualitative change in its macroscopic behavior, a reorganization of the collective state of its many constituent particles. Understanding phase transitions has been one of the central achievements of twentieth-century theoretical physics, requiring new mathematical tools and revealing unexpected connections between seemingly unrelated physical systems.

A phase is a region of parameter space — characterized by temperature, pressure, magnetic field, or other control variables — within which the macroscopic properties of a system vary smoothly and continuously. A phase transition occurs when these properties change abruptly as a control parameter crosses a phase boundary. The most familiar examples are the transitions between solid, liquid, and gas, but phase transitions also include magnetic ordering, superconductivity, superfluidity, Bose-Einstein condensation, and many others.

Phase transitions are classified into two broad categories based on the behavior of thermodynamic quantities at the transition. First-order transitions are characterized by a discontinuity in the first derivative of the free energy with respect to a thermodynamic variable. The transition from liquid water to steam at atmospheric pressure and 100 degrees Celsius is first-order: at the transition temperature and pressure, the density (related to the first derivative of the Gibbs free energy with respect to pressure) jumps discontinuously. The system absorbs or releases a definite amount of heat, called the latent heat, without changing temperature while the transition occurs. During a first-order transition, two phases coexist in equilibrium — liquid and steam coexist at the boiling point, solid and liquid at the melting point.

Latent heat reflects the energy required to break the bonds or structural order of one phase and reconstitute it in another. For water at 100 degrees Celsius and atmospheric pressure, the latent heat of vaporization is about 2,260 joules per gram, enormously larger than the heat required to raise the temperature of water by one degree (4.18 joules per gram per degree). This large latent heat is why a steam burn is so much more severe than a hot water burn at the same temperature, and why evaporative cooling is so effective.

Second-order transitions, also called continuous transitions, have continuous first derivatives of the free energy but discontinuous or divergent second derivatives. There is no latent heat and no two-phase coexistence. Instead, the system passes smoothly between phases, but certain properties change character fundamentally. The transition of iron from ferromagnetic to paramagnetic at the Curie temperature (about 770 degrees Celsius) is a canonical example. Below the Curie temperature, iron has a spontaneous magnetic moment; above it, the magnetization is zero in the absence of an external field. At the transition temperature exactly, the magnetization approaches zero continuously as the temperature increases through the Curie point.

The concept of an order parameter is central to the theory of phase transitions. An order parameter is a quantity that is zero in the disordered phase and nonzero in the ordered phase. For the ferromagnetic transition, the order parameter is the magnetization. For the liquid-gas transition treated as a continuous transition near the critical point, the order parameter is the difference in density between liquid and gas. For a superconductor, the order parameter is a complex quantum mechanical amplitude related to the density and phase of Cooper pairs. The order parameter provides a macroscopic characterization of the degree of order in the system.

Near a continuous phase transition, systems exhibit critical phenomena — remarkable behaviors associated with the divergence of fluctuations. The correlation length, which measures how far fluctuations at one point in the system are correlated with fluctuations at another, diverges at the critical point. Near the critical point, the system develops fluctuations on all length scales simultaneously, from the microscopic to the macroscopic. This scale invariance is reflected in power-law behaviors: thermodynamic quantities diverge or go to zero as power laws of the distance from the critical temperature, with exponents called critical exponents. Remarkably, the critical exponents of systems that seem physically very different — a magnetic system and a liquid-gas system, for instance — can be identical. This universality indicates that the critical behavior depends only on the dimensionality of space and the symmetry of the order parameter, not on the microscopic details.

The theoretical framework for understanding universality is the renormalization group, developed by Kenneth Wilson in the 1970s, for which he received the Nobel Prize in 1982. The renormalization group is a systematic procedure for integrating out short-wavelength fluctuations and studying how the effective description of the system changes as the length scale is varied. Near a critical point, the system's behavior is governed by a fixed point of the renormalization group transformation, and systems that flow to the same fixed point share the same critical exponents — they belong to the same universality class. The renormalization group provides both a theoretical explanation for universality and a practical tool for computing critical exponents.

The phase diagram of a substance maps the phases present as a function of control parameters, typically temperature and pressure. For water, the phase diagram shows solid, liquid, and gas regions separated by phase boundaries, which meet at the triple point where all three phases coexist simultaneously. The liquid-gas phase boundary ends at the critical point, where the distinction between liquid and gas disappears — the densities of the two phases become equal and the interface between them vanishes. Above the critical temperature and pressure, water is a supercritical fluid with properties intermediate between liquid and gas, and it is possible to move continuously from a gas-like to a liquid-like state without crossing a phase boundary. The existence of the critical point and the rich behavior near it are characteristic features of many phase transitions beyond the simple liquid-gas case.
