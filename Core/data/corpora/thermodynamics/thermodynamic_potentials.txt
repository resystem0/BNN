Thermodynamic potentials are energy-like state functions that, when minimized or maximized under appropriate constraints, determine the equilibrium state of a thermodynamic system. Each potential is adapted to a particular set of experimental conditions — a particular choice of which variables are held fixed and which are allowed to vary. The four principal thermodynamic potentials are the internal energy U, the enthalpy H, the Helmholtz free energy F (also denoted A), and the Gibbs free energy G. A fifth important quantity, the grand potential, is appropriate for systems that can exchange particles with a reservoir. Together these potentials form a unified framework for analyzing equilibrium thermodynamics in different physical and chemical settings.

The internal energy U is the most fundamental thermodynamic potential. For a simple single-component system, its natural variables are entropy S, volume V, and number of particles N: changes in U are expressed as dU equals T dS minus P dV plus mu dN, where T is temperature, P is pressure, and mu is chemical potential. This fundamental relation encodes all thermodynamic information about the system. The temperature, pressure, and chemical potential emerge as partial derivatives of U with respect to its natural variables: T equals the partial derivative of U with respect to S at fixed V and N; P equals negative the partial derivative of U with respect to V at fixed S and N; mu equals the partial derivative of U with respect to N at fixed S and V. The internal energy is minimized at equilibrium when entropy and volume are held fixed, which corresponds to an isolated system with a rigid, impermeable boundary.

In practice, many processes occur not at constant entropy but at constant entropy is difficult to maintain experimentally. It is more common to hold temperature constant. This calls for a different potential. The Helmholtz free energy is defined as F equals U minus TS. Its natural variables are temperature T, volume V, and particle number N, and its differential is dF equals negative S dT minus P dV plus mu dN. The Helmholtz free energy is minimized at equilibrium for systems at constant temperature and volume. The name "free energy" reflects the interpretation: at constant temperature, the maximum work a system can do on its surroundings in a reversible process equals the decrease in Helmholtz free energy. The part of the internal energy "bound up" in entropy, namely TS, is not available to do work in an isothermal process; only F, the "free" part, can be extracted as work.

The enthalpy H is defined as H equals U plus PV. Its natural variables are entropy S, pressure P, and particle number N, and its differential is dH equals T dS plus V dP plus mu dN. The enthalpy is minimized at equilibrium for systems at constant entropy and pressure. More practically, it is the natural quantity for processes that occur at constant pressure — which describes most chemical reactions in open vessels. The heat released or absorbed in a chemical reaction at constant pressure equals the change in enthalpy, which is why tables of thermodynamic data list enthalpies of formation for chemical compounds. The enthalpy of a reaction, sometimes called the heat of reaction, tells a chemist directly how much heat will flow when the reaction proceeds at constant pressure.

The Gibbs free energy G is defined as G equals U minus TS plus PV equals H minus TS equals F plus PV. Its natural variables are temperature T, pressure P, and particle number N, and its differential is dG equals negative S dT plus V dP plus mu dN. The Gibbs free energy is minimized at equilibrium for systems at constant temperature and pressure. Since most practical processes — chemical reactions in solution, industrial processes, biological processes — occur at essentially constant temperature and pressure, the Gibbs free energy is the most practically important thermodynamic potential. A reaction is spontaneous if it decreases the Gibbs free energy; equilibrium is reached when G is minimized. The criterion for a reaction to proceed spontaneously at constant T and P is that delta-G is negative.

The chemical potential mu, which appears in all these potentials, is the change in thermodynamic potential per particle added to the system at constant other natural variables. It plays the role for particle transfer that temperature plays for heat transfer and pressure plays for volume change. Particles flow spontaneously from regions of high chemical potential to regions of low chemical potential, just as heat flows from high temperature to low temperature and pressure differences drive volume changes. At equilibrium between two phases or two regions in contact, the chemical potential must be equal. This is why, at the boiling point of water, the chemical potential of liquid water equals the chemical potential of steam — otherwise, water molecules would spontaneously transfer from one phase to the other.

The Maxwell relations are a set of mathematical identities among partial derivatives of thermodynamic potentials that follow from the fact that these potentials are exact differentials (their mixed partial derivatives are equal). From the Helmholtz free energy, for example, the equality of mixed partials gives the identity that the partial derivative of entropy with respect to volume at constant T equals the partial derivative of pressure with respect to temperature at constant V. These relations connect quantities that are difficult to measure directly (like how entropy changes with volume) to quantities that are easy to measure (like how pressure changes with temperature). They are practically valuable in deriving relationships between measurable properties of substances.

Thermodynamic stability requires that the appropriate potential have a minimum, not merely a stationary point, at equilibrium. Stability conditions impose constraints on the second derivatives of the potential: the heat capacity at constant volume must be positive (systems should heat up when energy is added, not cool down), the isothermal compressibility must be positive (systems should shrink when compressed, not expand), and so on. Violations of these stability conditions indicate that the system is in an unstable phase that will spontaneously separate into coexisting phases. The spinodal, the boundary of thermodynamic instability, lies inside the coexistence region of a phase diagram and marks where the stability conditions are first violated.

The grand potential, defined as omega equals F minus mu N, is appropriate for open systems that exchange particles with a particle reservoir at fixed chemical potential. Its natural variables are temperature T, volume V, and chemical potential mu, and it is the natural potential for quantum statistical mechanics, where the number of particles fluctuates and the grand canonical ensemble is used.
