Integrated Information Theory, commonly abbreviated as IIT, is a quantitative theory of consciousness developed primarily by the neuroscientist and psychiatrist Giulio Tononi, with significant contributions from Christof Koch and others. IIT takes a fundamentally different approach from most theories of consciousness by starting not with neuroscience or cognitive psychology but with the phenomenology of experience itself — with an analysis of what consciousness intrinsically is — and then deriving from that analysis what physical systems must be like in order to be conscious. The result is a theory that is simultaneously ambitious, mathematically precise, and philosophically provocative.

Tononi begins with a set of phenomenological axioms — claims about the essential properties of conscious experience that are meant to be self-evidently true to any conscious subject. Experience exists: there is something it is like to have it, and this is an undeniable first-person fact. Experience is structured: it is composed of distinctions — this experience rather than that one. Experience is specific: every experience is what it is precisely and not something else. Experience is unified: it is a single, integrated whole that cannot be decomposed into independent parts. And experience is definite: it has a particular content and excludes other possible contents.

From these phenomenological axioms, Tononi derives postulates about the physical substrates of consciousness. A system is conscious to the degree that it is irreducibly integrated — to the degree that the information it generates as a whole is more than the sum of the information generated by its parts considered independently. This integration is measured by a quantity called phi (the Greek letter), which quantifies the amount of information generated by the whole system above and beyond the information generated by its parts. Phi is calculated by comparing the cause-effect power of the system as a whole against the maximum cause-effect power achievable if the system were partitioned into independent parts at the weakest point.

The concept of cause-effect power is central to IIT. A system has cause-effect power to the degree that its current state constrains what states preceded it (its causes) and what states will follow it (its effects). A system that constrains many possible past and future states — that rules out many alternatives — has high cause-effect power. Consciousness, on IIT's account, is identical to the cause-effect power of a system above and beyond its parts: it is the intrinsic causal efficacy of integrated information.

Phi is high in systems that are both highly differentiated and highly integrated. The cerebral cortex of mammals has precisely these properties: it contains billions of neurons arranged in modules that are specialized for different functions (high differentiation) but are extensively interconnected in ways that allow information to be shared globally (high integration). Cerebellum, by contrast, contains even more neurons but is organized very differently — it is highly modular and its connections are largely feedforward with little recurrent integration — and IIT predicts that despite its size, the cerebellum contributes little to consciousness, a prediction that is broadly consistent with clinical observations showing that cerebellar damage has surprisingly little effect on conscious experience.

The implications of IIT extend far beyond neuroscience. Because the theory is defined in terms of abstract causal structure rather than in terms of biological neurons, it applies in principle to any physical system. A system has nonzero phi if and only if it has any irreducible causal integration at all. This means that very simple systems — even a photodiode or a thermostat — have some small but nonzero phi and therefore some minimal form of consciousness. IIT thus implies a form of panpsychism: consciousness is present wherever there is integrated information, which is essentially everywhere in nature, though in vanishingly small amounts in simple systems.

This panpsychist implication is one of the most controversial aspects of IIT. Many philosophers and scientists find it counterintuitive to attribute any form of experience to rocks or transistors. Tononi and his collaborators argue that the panpsychist consequence is not a reductio ad absurdum but rather a sign that IIT is getting something fundamentally right about the nature of consciousness: that it is a natural feature of certain physical configurations, graded and ubiquitous rather than abrupt and confined to brains.

IIT also makes the striking prediction that digital computers, even very sophisticated ones, are not conscious or are only minimally so. Standard computer architectures, such as the von Neumann architecture common in modern computers, have a structure that IIT predicts should have very low phi. The central processing unit is essentially a feedforward system that processes information serially, and while the computer as a whole may process vast amounts of information, it does not integrate that information in the irreducible way that IIT requires for consciousness. This prediction puts IIT at odds with functionalist views of mind that hold that what matters for consciousness is the pattern of information processing, not the physical substrate.

The measurement of phi is computationally intractable for systems larger than a few dozen elements, which creates serious practical challenges for testing IIT empirically. Various approximations have been developed, and some empirical studies have found that measures related to phi correlate with states of consciousness — being higher during wakefulness than during slow-wave sleep or anesthesia — but the relationship between these approximations and the actual theoretical quantity is contested.

IIT has been criticized on several grounds. Some argue that its axioms, while phenomenologically plausible, are not the only plausible starting point and that different axioms would yield different theories. Others argue that the mathematics of phi does not capture what it is supposed to capture, or that the theory is unfalsifiable in practice. The debate over IIT is among the most active in consciousness science, reflecting both its theoretical ambition and its genuine novelty as an attempt to derive a theory of consciousness from first principles rather than from neuroscience alone.
