Higher-order theories of consciousness are a family of philosophical accounts that share the central idea that a mental state is conscious not in virtue of its intrinsic properties but in virtue of being represented by another mental state. On these views, what makes a state conscious is the fact that the subject is in some sense aware of being in that state — that there is a higher-order representation directed at the first-order state. A visual experience of red is conscious, for instance, not simply because it represents redness but because there is some further mental state that represents the subject as having that visual experience. Without the higher-order representation, the first-order state might still exist and still function, but it would be unconscious.

The most widely discussed version of this framework is the higher-order thought theory, associated primarily with David Rosenthal. On Rosenthal's account, a mental state is conscious if and only if it is accompanied by a suitable higher-order thought — a thought to the effect that one is in that mental state. The higher-order thought need not itself be conscious; indeed, Rosenthal insists that it typically is not. What matters is that there is such a thought, and that it suitably represents the first-order state. The intuitive motivation is that consciousness seems to involve a kind of self-awareness: when you are consciously seeing red, you are in some sense aware that you are seeing red, not merely undergoing an unconscious visual process.

Rosenthal distinguishes his view from the claim that consciousness requires introspection or deliberate reflection. The higher-order thought is not an act of attending to one's inner states but rather a kind of standing representational condition that normally accompanies conscious experience without being noticed. It is the difference between consciously seeing something and reflectively thinking about the fact that one is conscious of it. On the higher-order thought view, the former requires only the background higher-order representation, not the latter act of deliberate introspection.

A related but distinct version of the higher-order framework is the higher-order perception or inner sense theory, associated with William Lycan. On this account, what makes a state conscious is not a thought but a perceptual-like monitoring of the first-order state by a higher-order faculty analogous to perception. Lycan proposes that the mind includes an internal monitor — a kind of inner sense — that scans lower-level mental processes and forms representations of them, just as the outer senses scan the external environment and form representations of it. Consciousness is what it is like to have one's states monitored in this way.

Both versions of the theory face a common family of objections. One is the problem of the absent higher-order representation: a subject might plausibly be in a first-order pain state that influences behavior in all the ways pain normally does but without any higher-order representation of it. Would such a state be an unconscious pain? Higher-order theorists typically say yes, which strikes many as counterintuitive — it seems odd to say that someone can be in genuine pain without that pain being conscious. The theory also faces the problem of the wrong-order representation: one might have a higher-order thought representing oneself as being in a state that one is not actually in. Higher-order theorists typically say that this would constitute a kind of illusory or hallucinatory conscious experience, which may be correct but requires careful handling.

A more recent development in the higher-order tradition is the self-representationalist theory, developed by Uriah Kriegel. Kriegel argues that a mental state is conscious in virtue of representing itself, not in virtue of being represented by a numerically distinct higher-order state. On this view, conscious states are self-representational: they represent the world (or the body, or whatever their content concerns) and simultaneously represent themselves as doing so. This avoids some of the worries about the higher-order theories — in particular, it avoids the concern that consciousness requires an additional mental state beyond the first-order one — while preserving the basic insight that consciousness involves a kind of inner awareness.

The connection between higher-order theories and metacognition is significant. Metacognition — cognition about cognition — has been studied extensively in cognitive psychology and neuroscience, and higher-order theories predict a tight link between metacognitive processes and consciousness. Research on confidence judgments, error monitoring, and introspective reporting has revealed a class of processes that represent and evaluate first-order cognitive states, and these are plausible candidates for the higher-order representations posited by the theory. The prefrontal cortex is implicated in many of these metacognitive functions, consistent with the idea that higher-order representation depends on frontal systems.

Critics from the first-order tradition, associated with philosophers like Fred Dretske and Tye, argue that higher-order theories get the order of explanation backwards. On first-order views, what makes a state conscious is that it represents the world in the right kind of way — that it makes the subject directly aware of external objects or properties — not that it is represented by any other state. Consciousness is a matter of the first-order state's intentionality, not of any further meta-representation. The debate between first-order and higher-order views is partly empirical and partly conceptual, turning on intuitions about the nature of awareness and the relationship between consciousness and self-knowledge.

Representational accounts of consciousness more generally — whether first- or higher-order — share the commitment that consciousness is constituted by representational relations. This contrasts with views that take consciousness to be a non-representational, intrinsic feature of certain states. The appeal of representational accounts is that they promise to naturalize consciousness by grounding it in the same broadly physical-functional relations that ground intentionality and cognition more generally. Whether that promise can be fulfilled without residue — without leaving the hard problem of consciousness in place — remains the central question for all representational theories of mind.
