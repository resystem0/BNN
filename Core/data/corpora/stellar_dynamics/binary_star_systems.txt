The majority of stars in the galaxy do not live in isolation. Most stars are members of binary or higher-order multiple systems — gravitationally bound pairs or groups of stars that orbit their common center of mass. This is not merely a statistical curiosity: binary interactions profoundly affect stellar evolution, enabling phenomena — mass transfer, common envelope evolution, Type Ia supernovae, X-ray binaries, gravitational wave sources, recycled pulsars — that would be impossible for single stars. The study of binary star systems is therefore not a specialized subdiscipline of stellar astronomy but an essential part of understanding how stars live and die.

Visual binaries, in which both stars can be resolved as separate points of light, provide the most direct determinations of stellar masses through Kepler's third law: the orbital period and the angular separation, combined with knowledge of the system's distance, yield the sum of the stellar masses. Spectroscopic binaries, detected through the periodic Doppler shifts of their spectral lines as the stars orbit their common center of mass, extend the census of binary systems to pairs too close to resolve spatially. Eclipsing binaries, in which the orbital plane is aligned nearly edge-on to the line of sight so that the stars periodically pass in front of each other, provide both masses and radii from the combination of orbital dynamics and eclipse light curve modeling. Eclipsing spectroscopic binaries are particularly valuable because they provide all the stellar parameters without requiring knowledge of the system's distance, making them the primary observational foundation for the mass-radius-luminosity relations that underlie stellar structure theory.

The Roche lobe concept is central to understanding binary star interactions. The Roche lobe of a star in a binary system is the region of space around it within which material is gravitationally bound to that star rather than to its companion. It is approximately an elongated, teardrop-shaped region whose inner boundary — the L1 Lagrange point — is the saddle point of the gravitational potential between the two stars. When a star expands during its post-main-sequence evolution and its radius approaches the Roche lobe radius, material at the stellar surface can flow through the L1 point onto the companion star in a process called Roche lobe overflow. This mass transfer can occur steadily or episodically, can transfer substantial fractions of the donor star's mass to the accretor, and can dramatically alter the subsequent evolution of both stars.

Cataclysmic variables are binary systems in which a white dwarf accretes hydrogen-rich material from a companion star, typically a main sequence or evolved star that is filling its Roche lobe. The accreted material spirals down through an accretion disk onto the white dwarf's surface, converting gravitational potential energy to heat and radiation. The disk is the primary source of light in the system and produces characteristic ultraviolet and optical emission lines of hydrogen, helium, and other species. Periodic instabilities in the accretion disk produce outbursts called dwarf novae, in which the system brightens by several magnitudes over days and then declines over weeks. In classical novae, hydrogen accumulated on the white dwarf surface undergoes thermonuclear runaway when the temperature and pressure in the accumulated layer reach the ignition threshold; the resulting explosion ejects the accumulated layer but leaves the white dwarf largely intact. If the white dwarf is close to the Chandrasekhar mass and accretion continues over many nova cycles, the white dwarf may eventually approach the Chandrasekhar limit and explode as a Type Ia supernova, though the details of this pathway are still debated.

X-ray binaries contain either a neutron star or a black hole accreting matter from a stellar companion, and they are among the most luminous objects in the X-ray sky. The extreme gravitational potential well of the compact object converts a substantial fraction of the accreted rest mass to X-ray luminosity — an energy conversion efficiency of order 10 percent, compared to the 0.7 percent efficiency of hydrogen fusion in stellar cores. High-mass X-ray binaries contain massive O or B star companions that supply accretion material through their powerful stellar winds or through Roche lobe overflow; the compact object was produced by the supernova death of the originally more massive star in the binary. Low-mass X-ray binaries contain low-mass companions that are filling their Roche lobes and transferring material through an accretion disk; the system parameters and evolutionary history are very different from the high-mass case.

The common envelope phase is one of the most important and poorly understood stages of binary stellar evolution. When the mass transfer rate becomes so high that the accretor cannot absorb the infalling material, the transferred material builds up around both stars, forming a "common envelope" that envelops the binary system. Frictional forces between the two stellar cores and the surrounding envelope cause the cores to spiral inward toward each other, converting orbital energy to thermal energy in the envelope. If enough energy is deposited in the envelope, it can be ejected, leaving behind a much tighter binary of the stellar cores. The common envelope phase is essential for producing the close binary systems — cataclysmic variables, double white dwarfs, double neutron stars — that undergo the interesting astrophysical phenomena described above, because the progenitor binaries must have been much wider to accommodate the giant stars that preceded the compact remnants.

Gravitational wave sources — merging compact object binaries detected by LIGO and Virgo — are the evolutionary descendants of the binary interactions described above. Double neutron star systems like the Hulse-Taylor binary are formed through two sequential supernova explosions in a massive binary, with a common envelope phase that tightened the orbit sufficiently for gravitational wave emission to drive the binary to merger within a Hubble time. Double black hole binaries, which LIGO detected first in 2015 (GW150914), likely formed either through similar channels in massive binary stellar evolution or through dynamical capture in dense stellar clusters. Neutron star-black hole binaries, first detected in 2021, have mass ratios that constrain both the mass functions of compact remnants and the mechanisms of compact object formation. The growing catalog of gravitational wave detections from merging compact binaries is providing a new window onto stellar evolution, binary interaction, and the demographics of compact objects throughout the universe.
