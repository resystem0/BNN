When a star exhausts the hydrogen fuel in its core, its subsequent evolution accelerates dramatically and becomes considerably more complex than its long, stable main sequence phase. The exhaustion of core hydrogen marks the end of the main sequence and the beginning of the star's journey toward its ultimate fate — a journey that differs profoundly depending on the star's initial mass. For stars in the low to intermediate mass range (from about 0.8 to about 8 solar masses, which encompasses the great majority of stars), the post-main-sequence evolution leads through a red giant phase, a helium-burning phase, and ultimately to the production of a white dwarf surrounded by a planetary nebula. Understanding this sequence of events requires tracking the delicate interplay between the evolution of the stellar core and the dramatic changes in the stellar envelope.

When core hydrogen is exhausted, nuclear burning ceases in the core but continues in a thin shell surrounding the helium-rich core. This shell burning drives a profound structural change in the star: the inner regions — the core and the shell — contract and heat up, while the outer envelope expands and cools enormously. The radius of the star increases by factors of ten to a hundred while its surface temperature falls, moving the star off the main sequence to the right and upward in the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram along the subgiant and red giant branch. The physical reason for this expansion is not immediately obvious from simple arguments; it requires a careful treatment of the stellar structure equations with a shell-burning source, and it is sometimes described heuristically by saying that the expanding envelope reacts to the contraction of the core in a kind of thermal anti-correlation. The Sun will expand to roughly 200 times its current radius when it reaches the tip of the red giant branch in about 5 billion years, engulfing the inner planets.

As the inert helium core accretes mass from the overlying hydrogen-burning shell, it contracts and heats up. In stars below about 2 solar masses, the core becomes so dense before helium ignition temperatures are reached that the electrons in the core become degenerate — they are packed so tightly that their quantum mechanical exclusion principle (electrons cannot occupy the same quantum state) provides most of the pressure rather than thermal motion. This degenerate state has a crucial consequence: degenerate electron pressure is nearly independent of temperature. When helium ignition eventually occurs in the degenerate core — at a temperature of about 100 million Kelvin — the energy released by the initial helium burning does not cause the core to expand and cool in the normal way. Instead, the temperature rises, which increases the nuclear burning rate, which increases the temperature further in an uncontrolled runaway. This helium flash is a dramatic and violent event, releasing energy equivalent to an entire galaxy for a brief moment, though the energy is absorbed in the envelope expansion and does not produce an observable optical brightening of the star. After the helium flash, the degeneracy is lifted, the core expands, cools, and settles into a stable helium-burning equilibrium on the horizontal branch or red clump in the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram.

The asymptotic giant branch (AGB) phase follows when helium in the core is exhausted, leaving a carbon-oxygen core surrounded by concentric shells of helium-burning and hydrogen-burning material. The star ascends the asymptotic giant branch in the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram, becoming even larger and more luminous than it was at the tip of the first red giant branch. The AGB phase is characterized by the thermal pulse cycle: helium builds up in the shell until it ignites in a runaway similar to the core helium flash, called a thermal pulse or helium shell flash. The pulse drives convection that mixes carbon produced by helium burning up into the envelope — a process called third dredge-up — progressively enriching the stellar surface in carbon. Stars whose carbon abundance becomes greater than their oxygen abundance are classified as carbon stars and show distinctive molecular absorption bands of carbon compounds in their spectra. AGB stars become powerful dust factories, creating silicate dust grains in oxygen-rich stars and carbonaceous grains in carbon stars that are then expelled into the interstellar medium by stellar winds.

Mass loss during the AGB phase is both dramatic and critically important for understanding the fate of intermediate-mass stars. AGB stars drive powerful stellar winds, losing mass at rates millions to hundreds of millions of times greater than the current solar wind. The driving mechanism of these winds is not fully understood but involves the formation of dust grains that are then accelerated by radiation pressure from the luminous stellar interior. Over the AGB lifetime, a star that began with several solar masses may lose all but about half a solar mass to its surroundings, forming a dense circumstellar envelope of gas and dust. When the remaining hot core eventually terminates nuclear burning and the outer layers are expelled, the UV radiation from the hot core ionizes the surrounding gas, producing the beautiful, often intricate structures of planetary nebulae — so called because their round shapes superficially resemble planets when observed through small telescopes, though they have nothing to do with planets.

The remnant of the stellar core after the planetary nebula phase is a white dwarf: a dense, compact object roughly the size of Earth but with a mass close to that of the Sun. White dwarfs are supported against gravitational collapse not by thermal pressure or nuclear burning but by the quantum mechanical pressure of degenerate electrons, which is effectively independent of temperature. Without an energy source, white dwarfs gradually cool over billions of years, fading from hot, luminous blue-white objects to cool, dim red stars and eventually — over timescales far exceeding the current age of the universe — to cold, dark black dwarfs. The cooling of white dwarfs follows a well-understood physics, making white dwarf cooling ages a useful chronometer for old stellar populations. The oldest white dwarfs in globular clusters have cooling ages that imply cluster formation near the beginning of galaxy formation, consistent with independent age estimates from main sequence turnoff points.

The chemical enrichment of the interstellar medium by AGB stars and planetary nebulae is a fundamental component of galactic chemical evolution. AGB stars are the primary sources of the s-process heavy elements barium, strontium, yttrium, and others, as well as of the carbon and nitrogen that are essential for organic chemistry. Planetary nebula shells return this chemically enriched material to the interstellar medium, where it mixes with existing gas and is incorporated into the next generation of stars and planets. The chemical composition of a star therefore reflects the integrated contributions of all previous generations of stellar evolution in its environment, encoded in the abundances of elements in its atmosphere. The Sun's photospheric abundance of s-process elements is a record of the AGB star nucleosynthesis that occurred in the solar neighborhood before the Sun formed.
