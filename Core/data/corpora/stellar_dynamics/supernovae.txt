Supernovae are among the most energetic explosions in the universe, briefly outshining entire galaxies before fading over weeks to months. They are the forge in which most of the elements heavier than hydrogen and helium are produced, the engines that drive galactic chemical evolution, the sources of the cosmic ray flux that bathes the Earth, and, in certain cases, standard candles of sufficient consistency to measure the expansion history of the universe across billions of light years. Understanding supernovae requires stellar physics, nuclear physics, neutrino physics, and general relativity, and despite enormous progress the mechanism of the most common type — the core-collapse supernova — remains an active area of research.

Core-collapse supernovae, also called Type II or Type Ib/c supernovae depending on the hydrogen content of their spectra, arise from the deaths of massive stars with initial masses above about eight solar masses. After such a star has burned through its available nuclear fuels in successive shells — hydrogen, helium, carbon, neon, oxygen, silicon — it develops a layered structure like an onion, with progressively heavier elements in each successive inner shell and an inert iron core at the center. Iron is the endpoint of exothermic nuclear fusion: iron-56 has the highest binding energy per nucleon of any nucleus, so neither fusing it with other nuclei nor splitting it releases energy. When the iron core grows to the Chandrasekhar mass through silicon burning in the surrounding shell, it can no longer be supported by electron degeneracy pressure and collapses catastrophically. The collapse takes less than a second, during which the core falls from roughly the size of Earth to roughly the size of a small city.

At nuclear density, the strong nuclear repulsion and neutron degeneracy pressure halt the collapse and the core "bounces." This bounce drives a shock wave outward through the infalling outer layers of the core. The shock wave initially stalls as it loses energy by dissociating iron nuclei (an endothermic process) and by the emission of electron neutrinos from electron capture reactions (which carry away energy faster than the shock can absorb it). The critical question — how does the supernova actually explode? — remains not fully answered. The currently favored mechanism is neutrino heating: the collapsed proto-neutron star emits an enormous flux of neutrinos of all flavors, carrying away about 99 percent of the total gravitational energy released by the collapse (roughly 3 times ten to the forty-six joules, equivalent to the Sun's entire main sequence luminosity times ten thousand). A few percent of these neutrinos are absorbed by the stalled shock, heating the material behind the shock and re-energizing it. Multi-dimensional effects — convection, standing accretion shock instability, neutrino-driven winds — appear to be essential for successful shock revival and explosion, and current three-dimensional supernova simulations are only beginning to achieve consistent explosions across the range of progenitor masses.

The nucleosynthesis in core-collapse supernovae is responsible for most of the oxygen, neon, magnesium, silicon, sulfur, argon, calcium, and iron in the universe. As the shock wave propagates outward through the pre-explosion onion-shell structure, it heats each layer explosively, triggering explosive nuclear burning that synthesizes a different mixture of elements depending on the peak temperature reached. The innermost regions, heated to the highest temperatures, undergo complete silicon burning to iron-peak elements. Slightly outer regions undergo incomplete silicon burning, producing silicon, sulfur, and argon. Further out, oxygen burning and carbon burning produce oxygen, neon, and magnesium. The ejecta carry these freshly synthesized elements into the interstellar medium, where they are incorporated into subsequent generations of stars and planets. The oxygen in Earth's atmosphere, the calcium in our bones, and the iron in our blood were all produced in the supernova deaths of massive stars that lived and died before the Sun formed.

Type Ia supernovae are fundamentally different in their origin but equally important in their consequences. They arise not from the collapse of a stellar core but from thermonuclear runaway in a carbon-oxygen white dwarf that has been destabilized by mass accretion. When a white dwarf's mass approaches the Chandrasekhar limit — through mass transfer from a binary companion in the single-degenerate channel, or through the merger of two white dwarfs in the double-degenerate channel — carbon ignites at the center under degenerate conditions. The thermonuclear runaway propagates through the white dwarf as a combination of subsonic deflagration and supersonic detonation, incinerating the entire white dwarf within seconds and releasing enough energy (roughly one to two times ten to the forty-four joules) to unbind the star completely, leaving no compact remnant. The explosion synthesizes predominantly iron-peak elements, particularly nickel-56, which decays radioactively through cobalt-56 to iron-56 on timescales of weeks, powering the optical light curve through gamma ray deposition in the expanding ejecta.

The use of Type Ia supernovae as standard candles for cosmology rests on the observed uniformity of their peak luminosities, which are correlated with the width of the light curve: brighter events have broader, slower-declining light curves, a relationship calibrated by Philips and others. This "Phillips relation" allows the intrinsic luminosity to be estimated from the light curve shape, turning Type Ia supernovae into calibrated distance indicators. By measuring the apparent brightness of Type Ia supernovae at known redshifts, astronomers can trace the expansion history of the universe across cosmological distances. The 1998 discovery, by two independent teams using high-redshift Type Ia supernovae, that the expansion of the universe is accelerating rather than decelerating earned Perlmutter, Schmidt, and Riess the 2011 Nobel Prize in Physics and established the existence of dark energy as the dominant component of the universe's energy budget.

The light curves and spectra of supernovae are rich sources of information about the physics of the explosion and the composition of the ejecta. Photometric light curves trace the deposition of radioactive decay energy in the expanding ejecta. Spectroscopic observations reveal the chemical composition and velocity structure of the ejecta through the Doppler shifts and relative strengths of absorption and emission lines. In Type Ia supernovae, the characteristic sequence of spectral features — from silicon, sulfur, calcium, and iron at peak to iron-peak elements dominating at late times — confirms the thermonuclear nature of the explosion and provides diagnostics of the burning physics. In core-collapse supernovae, the hydrogen Balmer lines of Type II events versus their absence in Type Ib/c events reflects whether the hydrogen envelope was retained or stripped before the explosion, providing information about the mass loss history of the progenitor and the effects of binary interaction.
