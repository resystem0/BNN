The Sun produces approximately 3.8 times ten to the twenty-sixth watts of power — enough to boil the entire volume of Earth's oceans every fifteen minutes — from a volume only slightly larger than a million Earths, and it has been doing so with remarkable steadiness for 4.6 billion years. This prodigious and sustained energy output is the product of nuclear fusion reactions occurring in the stellar core, where temperatures and densities are high enough that atomic nuclei can overcome their mutual electrostatic repulsion and fuse together to form heavier nuclei, releasing energy in the process. Understanding nuclear fusion in stars requires quantum mechanics, nuclear physics, and plasma physics, and it stands as one of the great triumphs of twentieth-century astrophysics that the physics worked out in laboratories on Earth can fully account for the properties and lifetimes of stars.

Nuclear fusion releases energy because of the binding energy of atomic nuclei. The mass of a nucleus is slightly less than the sum of the masses of its constituent protons and neutrons — the binding energy holds the nucleus together and manifests as a mass deficit. When light nuclei fuse to form a heavier nucleus, the new nucleus has a larger binding energy per nucleon than the original nuclei, and this extra binding energy is released as kinetic energy of the products and as gamma radiation. The curve of binding energy per nucleon versus atomic mass number peaks at iron-56, which means that fusion of elements lighter than iron releases energy, while fusion of elements heavier than iron requires energy. All stellar nucleosynthesis of elements from hydrogen to iron is therefore exothermic, and all stellar nucleosynthesis of elements heavier than iron requires the addition of energy from an external source such as a supernova explosion or a neutron star merger.

The proton-proton chain is the dominant hydrogen fusion process in stars like the Sun and in all stars with core temperatures below about 1.7 times ten to the seventh Kelvin. It begins with the fusion of two protons to form deuterium, releasing a positron and a neutrino in a reaction that involves the weak nuclear force. This is by far the slowest step in the chain and the one that determines the overall rate of energy production: the cross-section for this reaction is so small that a given proton in the solar core will wait on average about ten billion years before fusing with another proton. The deuterium produced immediately captures another proton to form helium-3, releasing a gamma ray, and two helium-3 nuclei then fuse to form helium-4, releasing two protons. The net result of this complete pp chain cycle is the conversion of four hydrogen nuclei into one helium-4 nucleus, with the release of two positrons, two neutrinos, and energy totaling about 26.7 MeV per reaction cycle, of which about 2 percent is carried away by the neutrinos and escapes the star directly.

The CNO cycle, named for the carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen nuclei that serve as catalysts, is the dominant hydrogen fusion pathway in stars more massive than about 1.3 solar masses, where core temperatures exceed roughly 1.7 times ten to the seventh Kelvin. In the CNO cycle, carbon-12 captures a proton to form nitrogen-13, which undergoes beta decay to carbon-13, which captures another proton to form nitrogen-14, which captures a proton to form oxygen-15, which undergoes beta decay to nitrogen-15, which finally captures a proton and emits a helium-4 nucleus, regenerating carbon-12 and completing the cycle. The net result is the same as the pp chain — four protons converted to one helium-4 nucleus with the release of energy and neutrinos — but the catalytic nuclei are recycled, not consumed. The CNO cycle has a much steeper temperature dependence than the pp chain (the rate scales approximately as temperature to the eighteenth power versus temperature to the fourth power for the pp chain), which is why it dominates in the hotter cores of more massive stars and why massive stars burn their fuel so much more rapidly.

Neutrinos produced by stellar fusion provide a direct window into stellar interiors that is impossible for photons, which are trapped in the dense stellar plasma and take tens of thousands of years to random-walk from the core to the surface. Solar neutrinos were first detected in the famous Homestake experiment led by Raymond Davis Jr., which began taking data in the late 1960s. Davis found that the flux of solar neutrinos reaching Earth was only about one-third of the value predicted by the standard solar model — the celebrated solar neutrino problem. This discrepancy persisted for three decades and motivated both extensive refinements of the solar model and intensive experimental efforts. The resolution came from neutrino oscillation: the electron neutrinos produced by pp chain reactions in the Sun oscillate between different flavor states during their flight to Earth, and earlier detectors were sensitive only to electron neutrinos, missing the converted muon and tau neutrinos. The Sudbury Neutrino Observatory, using heavy water that is sensitive to all neutrino flavors, confirmed in 2001 that the total solar neutrino flux matches the standard solar model prediction, providing simultaneously a dramatic vindication of solar physics and a discovery that neutrinos have mass.

Helium burning, which ignites in stellar cores after hydrogen is exhausted, proceeds primarily through the triple-alpha process: two helium-4 nuclei fuse to form beryllium-8, which is highly unstable and almost immediately decays back to two helium-4 nuclei. A third helium-4 nucleus must fuse with the beryllium-8 before it decays, producing carbon-12. This reaction was predicted by Fred Hoyle before it was measured in the laboratory, because Hoyle realized that carbon could not be produced in stars without a near-resonance between the energy of the beryllium-8 plus helium-4 system and an excited state of carbon-12 — a resonance that was subsequently discovered experimentally. Carbon-12 can then capture a fourth helium-4 to produce oxygen-16, and the ratio of carbon to oxygen produced by helium burning, which depends on the ratio of the triple-alpha and carbon-alpha cross-sections, is a crucial parameter in stellar nucleosynthesis because it determines the relative abundances of these two critical elements in the universe.

The s-process and r-process are nucleosynthesis pathways that produce elements heavier than iron through neutron capture. The s-process (slow neutron capture) occurs in the helium-burning shells of asymptotic giant branch stars, where neutrons are produced by specific reactions and captured by seed nuclei on timescales long compared to beta decay — allowing beta decay to occur before the next neutron capture, keeping the reaction path near the valley of nuclear stability. The r-process (rapid neutron capture) occurs in environments with extremely high neutron fluxes — originally attributed to core-collapse supernovae but now primarily associated with neutron star mergers — where nuclei capture many neutrons before beta decaying, building up very neutron-rich isotopes that then decay to stable heavy elements including gold, platinum, and uranium. The detection of a gravitational wave signal from a neutron star merger (GW170817) coincident with a kilonova optical transient confirmed that neutron star mergers are major r-process nucleosynthesis sites.
