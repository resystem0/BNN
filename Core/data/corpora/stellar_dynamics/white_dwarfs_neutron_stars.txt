White dwarfs and neutron stars represent the two most common endpoints of stellar evolution, and they are among the most physically exotic objects in the observable universe. Both are supported against gravity not by thermal pressure or nuclear burning but by quantum mechanical degeneracy pressure, making them qualitatively different from any form of matter encountered in ordinary experience. Their existence, their properties, and the physics that determines their structure provide a remarkable confluence of quantum mechanics, general relativity, and nuclear physics, and they serve as natural laboratories for testing theories of matter at densities far beyond anything achievable in terrestrial experiments.

A white dwarf is the electron-degenerate remnant left when a low or intermediate mass star exhausts its nuclear fuel and expels its outer layers as a planetary nebula. Typical white dwarfs have masses between 0.5 and 1.0 solar masses packed into a volume comparable to Earth — densities of roughly one million grams per cubic centimeter, or about one million times the density of water. At these densities, the electrons in the stellar material are squeezed into such a small volume that their quantum mechanical wave functions overlap substantially, and the Pauli exclusion principle — which forbids two electrons from occupying the same quantum state — forces the electrons into high momentum states even at zero temperature. The resulting degeneracy pressure is independent of temperature; unlike thermal pressure, it cannot be reduced by cooling. This is why white dwarfs can cool indefinitely without collapsing: as the thermal energy radiates away, the degeneracy pressure maintains the structure.

The Chandrasekhar limit, derived by Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar in 1930 while on a sea voyage from India to England, is one of the most important results in astrophysics. Chandrasekhar showed that as the mass of a white dwarf increases, the electrons become increasingly relativistic — their velocities approach the speed of light — and the relativistic corrections reduce the degeneracy pressure relative to the non-relativistic case. Above a critical mass of approximately 1.4 solar masses, relativistic degeneracy pressure is insufficient to support the star, and the white dwarf must collapse. This Chandrasekhar limit defines the maximum mass of a stable white dwarf and has profound consequences for the physics of supernovae: when a white dwarf accreting mass from a binary companion approaches the Chandrasekhar limit, it undergoes thermonuclear runaway and explodes as a Type Ia supernova. Chandrasekhar received the Nobel Prize in Physics for this work in 1983, fifty-three years after the original derivation.

The internal structure of white dwarfs reflects their cooling history and composition. Most white dwarfs have a core composed primarily of carbon and oxygen, the products of helium burning in their progenitor AGB stars. This core is surrounded by a thin layer of helium and an even thinner outer atmosphere of hydrogen, whose spectral absorption lines classify white dwarfs as DA types. A smaller fraction show only helium lines (DB types), suggesting that some process has removed the hydrogen from the atmosphere, possibly through convective mixing. White dwarf atmospheres are extremely stratified by atomic diffusion — heavy elements sink rapidly under the strong surface gravity — which means that any pollution of the atmosphere by infalling asteroids or planetary debris is directly observable as absorption lines of heavy metals. The detection of metal-polluted white dwarfs, now quite common, has provided a new window onto the composition of the rocky bodies in extrasolar planetary systems.

Neutron stars form from the cores of massive stars that undergo core collapse supernovae. When the iron core of a massive star exceeds roughly 1.4 solar masses and collapses under gravity, the density rapidly reaches nuclear density — about 2 times ten to the fourteenth grams per cubic centimeter — at which point neutron degeneracy pressure and the repulsive core of the strong nuclear force halt the collapse. The energy deposited by the collapse drives the explosive ejection of the stellar envelope, and the remaining neutron star is an object of 1.4 to perhaps 2 solar masses compressed into a radius of only about 10 to 12 kilometers. The average density of a neutron star is comparable to that of an atomic nucleus, and the equation of state of matter at these extreme densities — which determines the maximum mass and the radius-mass relation of neutron stars — is a major unsolved problem in nuclear physics.

Pulsars are rotating neutron stars that emit beams of radio waves, X-rays, or gamma rays from their magnetic poles, sweeping these beams across the sky like a cosmic lighthouse. Pulsars were discovered in 1967 by Jocelyn Bell Burnell and Anthony Hewish, who detected regular radio pulses with periods of order one second and remarkable stability. The regularity of the pulse period — as stable as an atomic clock for the most stable pulsars — arises because the neutron star's enormous moment of inertia makes it a superb flywheel; it resists changes in rotation rate. Young pulsars spin rapidly — the Crab pulsar, the remnant of a supernova observed in 1054 AD, spins 30 times per second — and gradually slow over time as they radiate energy through magnetic dipole radiation. Millisecond pulsars, which spin hundreds of times per second, have been "recycled" by mass transfer from a binary companion that has spun them back up to high rates. These recycled millisecond pulsars have extremely stable rotation and serve as exquisitely precise natural clocks for testing general relativity and searching for gravitational waves.

Pulsar timing provides one of the most precise tests of general relativity through the Hulse-Taylor binary pulsar, discovered in 1974. The orbital decay of this binary neutron star system due to gravitational wave emission matches the predictions of general relativity to better than 0.2 percent accuracy, providing the first indirect evidence for gravitational waves and earning Hulse and Taylor the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1993. Arrays of millisecond pulsars across the sky — pulsar timing arrays — are used to detect very-low-frequency gravitational waves produced by supermassive black hole binaries, providing a gravitational wave window at nanohertz frequencies complementary to the kilohertz frequencies of LIGO.

The maximum mass of a neutron star, which depends on the uncertain equation of state of dense nuclear matter, is observationally constrained by the most massive pulsars with well-measured masses. The discovery of two solar mass pulsars through Shapiro delay measurements of their binary companions (PSR J1614-2230 and PSR J0348+0432) ruled out many proposed "soft" equations of state that predicted lower maximum masses, constraining the equation of state of dense matter and limiting the stiffness required to support two solar masses. The simultaneous LIGO detection of gravitational waves and electromagnetic counterparts from the neutron star merger GW170817 provided additional constraints on the neutron star equation of state through measurement of the tidal deformability of the merging stars, a parameter that depends on the equation of state and affects the gravitational waveform during the inspiral phase.
