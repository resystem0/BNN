The Hertzsprung-Russell diagram, independently constructed by the Danish astronomer Ejnar Hertzsprung and the American Henry Norris Russell in the early twentieth century, is the most important diagram in stellar astronomy. It plots stellar luminosity — the total power radiated by a star — against stellar surface temperature, and when the properties of thousands of stars are displayed in this space, a striking pattern emerges: stars do not scatter randomly across the diagram but cluster in specific regions that reflect the distinct stages of their lives. The most prominent feature is the main sequence, a narrow diagonal band running from hot, luminous blue stars in the upper left to cool, dim red stars in the lower right, along which the great majority of observed stars fall. The physics underlying the main sequence, and the reasons stars move off it over time, constitute the core of stellar evolution theory.

A star joins the zero-age main sequence when it first achieves hydrostatic equilibrium with hydrogen burning in its core. The position of a star on the zero-age main sequence is determined almost entirely by its initial mass: more massive stars are hotter, larger, and far more luminous. This mass-luminosity relation, observed empirically and confirmed theoretically, is steep: a star twice the mass of the Sun is roughly eight times more luminous; a star ten times the mass of the Sun is about ten thousand times more luminous. This steep luminosity-mass scaling has a dramatic consequence for stellar lifetimes: since a star's nuclear fuel supply is roughly proportional to its mass, and its rate of fuel consumption is proportional to its luminosity, the main sequence lifetime is proportional to mass divided by luminosity — roughly proportional to the inverse of mass squared or cubed. The Sun, with a main sequence lifetime of about ten billion years, is an intermediate case; the most massive O-type stars consume their hydrogen in just a few million years, while the lowest-mass red dwarf stars can shine for hundreds of billions of years.

The mass-luminosity relation arises from the stellar structure equations in a way that reveals the fundamental physics of stellar equilibria. A more massive star must generate more luminosity to support its greater weight against gravity, because the pressure required for hydrostatic equilibrium scales steeply with mass. The rate at which nuclear reactions generate energy depends steeply on temperature, and the core temperature needed to sustain the required luminosity scales with stellar mass. More massive stars therefore have hotter cores, higher nuclear burning rates, and vastly higher luminosities. The same physics that makes massive stars bright makes them short-lived: the product of mass and luminosity per unit mass determines the fuel supply and consumption rate, and the fuel runs out far sooner in massive stars than in their lower-mass counterparts.

As a star burns hydrogen in its core, its internal structure gradually evolves even while it remains on the main sequence. The core becomes progressively enriched in helium as hydrogen is consumed, and helium has a higher molecular weight than the hydrogen it replaces. This compositional change causes the stellar core to contract slightly and heat up, which increases the nuclear burning rate and causes the star to become gradually more luminous over its main sequence lifetime. The Sun has increased in luminosity by about 30 percent since it formed 4.6 billion years ago, and it will continue to brighten until it eventually exhausts its core hydrogen. This slow brightening of the Sun over geological time is the origin of the "faint young Sun paradox" — the young Sun was significantly less luminous than the current Sun, yet geological and biological evidence suggests that Earth's early surface was warm and liquid water was present, implying that the early climate was warmer than naive calculations of solar heating would predict.

The turnoff point on the main sequence is a valuable astronomical chronometer. In a cluster of stars formed simultaneously from the same gas cloud — all of the same age and initially the same composition — the most massive stars evolve off the main sequence first. The main sequence turnoff point, which marks the boundary between stars still on the main sequence and stars that have exhausted their core hydrogen, moves progressively toward lower luminosities as the cluster ages. By identifying the current turnoff luminosity of a cluster, astronomers can determine its age: the turnoff point corresponds to the mass of stars whose main sequence lifetime equals the cluster's age. This technique has been used to determine the ages of globular clusters, which are among the oldest stellar populations in the Milky Way, and has provided an independent estimate of the age of the universe that is consistent with the value inferred from cosmological observations.

The spectral classification system — O, B, A, F, G, K, M in order of decreasing surface temperature — was developed originally by Annie Jump Cannon at Harvard Observatory in the early twentieth century through the empirical classification of hundreds of thousands of stellar spectra. The spectral sequence corresponds to a temperature sequence because the appearance of absorption lines in stellar spectra depends on the excitation and ionization state of the atmospheric gas, which is determined primarily by temperature. O stars, with surface temperatures above thirty thousand Kelvin, show absorption lines of highly ionized helium and ionized metals. B stars show neutral helium lines and ionized metal lines. A stars show the strongest hydrogen Balmer absorption lines because hydrogen atoms are most likely to be in the appropriate excited state for absorbing visible-wavelength photons at the temperatures around ten thousand Kelvin that characterize A-type stars. Cooler K and M stars show the absorption lines of neutral metals and even of molecular species such as TiO, which can survive as molecules only at the cool temperatures below about four thousand Kelvin that characterize these stars.

The mass-radius relation for main sequence stars reveals another systematic trend: more massive stars are larger, with radii scaling approximately as mass to the power of 0.8 for solar-type and more massive stars. The Sun has a radius of about 700,000 kilometers; the most massive O stars can have radii ten to fifteen times larger. At the lower end of the mass spectrum, the minimum mass for a star to sustain hydrogen fusion is about 0.08 solar masses — the minimum mass needed for the core temperature to reach the 10 million Kelvin threshold for the proton-proton chain. Objects below this threshold are brown dwarfs, which shine by gravitational contraction and deuterium burning but cannot sustain hydrogen fusion and gradually cool and fade over time. The distinction between the lowest-mass stars and the highest-mass brown dwarfs, set by the nuclear physics of hydrogen fusion, defines one of the fundamental boundaries in the stellar population.
