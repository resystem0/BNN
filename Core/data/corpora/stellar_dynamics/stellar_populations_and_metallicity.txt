The stars in a galaxy are not all alike. Even setting aside the differences in mass, radius, and luminosity that characterize stars at different points in their evolution, there is a deeper division among stars that reflects when in the history of the universe they formed. Stars formed early in galactic history, when the universe was composed almost entirely of hydrogen and helium with only trace amounts of heavier elements, have very different chemical compositions from stars formed later, after many generations of stellar nucleosynthesis had enriched the interstellar medium with oxygen, carbon, silicon, iron, and other heavy elements. This systematic variation in chemical composition with age and location is encoded in the concept of stellar populations, and understanding it is essential for reconstructing the history of star formation and chemical evolution in galaxies.

Astronomers characterize the heavy element content of a star by its metallicity, a historical term that astronomers use for the abundance of all elements heavier than helium, regardless of whether they are technically metals. The metallicity is most commonly quantified as the iron abundance relative to hydrogen, expressed as the logarithm of the ratio of iron to hydrogen in the star relative to the same ratio in the Sun: a star with solar metallicity has [Fe/H] = 0, a star with one-tenth solar metallicity has [Fe/H] = -1, and a star with one-hundredth solar metallicity has [Fe/H] = -2. The metallicity range observed among the stars of the Milky Way spans from near-solar in the youngest disk stars to values of [Fe/H] = -6 or lower in the most metal-poor halo stars found so far, a range of a million in the iron-to-hydrogen ratio.

Walter Baade introduced the concept of stellar populations during World War II, when the Los Angeles blackout required for wartime security provided unusually dark skies that allowed him to resolve individual stars in the Andromeda galaxy and its companion elliptical galaxies. Baade found that the stellar populations of the disk of Andromeda resembled the solar neighborhood, with a mix of blue and red stars, while the spheroidal components — the halo and bulge — were dominated by red, evolved stars without luminous blue main sequence members. He proposed a two-population scheme: Population I, the stars of galactic disks, characterized by solar-like chemical compositions, ongoing star formation, and relatively young ages; and Population II, the stars of galactic halos and bulges, characterized by low metallicities, old ages, and the absence of current star formation. This two-population scheme, while a simplification, captured a genuine and important division in stellar properties.

The Physical basis of the Population I/II distinction lies in galactic chemical evolution. Galaxies formed from gas that was nearly metal-free in the early universe, reflecting the composition left by Big Bang nucleosynthesis: roughly 75 percent hydrogen and 25 percent helium by mass, with only trace amounts of deuterium, helium-3, and lithium. The first stars formed from this nearly primordial gas, lived fast as massive, luminous objects, and died as supernovae that enriched the surrounding interstellar medium with the first metals. Each subsequent generation of stars formed from gas enriched by the deaths of previous generations, so that later-forming stars have progressively higher metallicities. Galaxies that formed most of their stars early — elliptical galaxies and the halos and bulges of spirals — have predominantly old, metal-poor stellar populations. Galaxies that have continued forming stars to the present — spiral disks and irregular galaxies — have a wide range of stellar ages and metallicities.

Population III stars, the first stars formed in the universe, have never been directly observed but are predicted by theory and are required by cosmological simulations of early structure formation. These stars formed from essentially metal-free gas with a composition very close to the Big Bang output. The complete absence of metals in Population III star-forming gas is expected to produce very different star formation physics from what is observed today: metals and dust are important coolants in current molecular clouds, allowing the gas to fragment into small-mass clumps that form low-mass stars. Without these coolants, early molecular clouds may have been unable to fragment as finely, producing a stellar initial mass function strongly weighted toward massive stars. Population III stars of perhaps 100 to 1000 solar masses would have lived only a few million years before dying as supernovae, seeding the universe with the first metals and transforming the conditions for subsequent star formation.

Globular clusters, which are ancient, gravitationally bound collections of hundreds of thousands to millions of stars, are the primary observable tracers of Population II in the Milky Way and external galaxies. The stars in a globular cluster are all approximately the same age and have the same metallicity, making clusters ideal testbeds for stellar evolution theory. The oldest globular clusters have ages of 12 to 13 billion years, placing their formation in the first billion years of cosmic history. Their metallicities span a wide range, from [Fe/H] = -2.4 in the most metal-poor systems to nearly solar metallicity in the most metal-rich, reflecting the variety of environments and epochs in which globular clusters formed. The detailed abundance patterns in globular cluster stars — including the anti-correlations between oxygen and sodium, and between magnesium and aluminum, that are characteristic of globular cluster chemical evolution — provide clues about the nucleosynthesis occurring in the first generation of massive stars that polluted the material from which subsequent generations formed.

The study of individual stellar chemical abundances — stellar spectroscopy applied to large samples of stars spanning a wide range of ages, metallicities, and locations in the galaxy — has become a major tool for reconstructing galactic history. Surveys like APOGEE, GALAH, and Gaia-ESO have measured detailed chemical abundances for hundreds of thousands of stars in the Milky Way, revealing systematic trends in the abundance ratios of different elements with metallicity. The ratio of alpha-elements — oxygen, magnesium, silicon, calcium, and titanium, produced primarily by core-collapse supernovae on short timescales — to iron — produced by both core-collapse and Type Ia supernovae, the latter on longer timescales of order one billion years — varies systematically with metallicity and with the stellar population. High alpha-to-iron ratios indicate formation from material enriched by core-collapse supernovae before Type Ia supernovae had time to contribute; low ratios indicate formation from material that has been enriched by the longer-timescale iron contribution of Type Ia supernovae. This "chemical clock" provides information about the star formation rate history of different galactic components that is largely independent of direct age measurements.
