A star is, at its most fundamental level, a self-gravitating ball of plasma held together by its own immense gravity and prevented from collapsing by the pressure of the hot gas and radiation within it. The remarkable stability of stars — the Sun has been shining at roughly its current luminosity for over four billion years — is a consequence of a precise balance between these two opposing forces, a state called hydrostatic equilibrium. Understanding how this equilibrium is established, maintained, and eventually disrupted is the foundation of stellar structure theory, and it explains why stars of different masses have such dramatically different properties, lifetimes, and fates.

Hydrostatic equilibrium is expressed mathematically by the condition that the pressure gradient within a star exactly counterbalances the gravitational force per unit volume at every point. Consider a thin shell of gas at some radius within the star: the weight of all the gas above it presses inward, while the pressure difference between the gas just below it and the gas just above it pushes outward. In equilibrium, these forces are exactly equal at every radius. This condition, combined with the equation of state relating pressure, density, and temperature, and with the equations describing how energy is generated and transported through the stellar interior, constitutes the set of stellar structure equations that determine the properties of a star in complete generality.

The pressure supporting a star against gravity is primarily thermal pressure — the pressure of the hot gas generated by the thermal motion of particles in the stellar interior. This thermal pressure is maintained by the nuclear reactions occurring in the stellar core, which convert a small fraction of the rest mass of hydrogen into energy in the form of gamma rays and neutrinos. Without this energy source, a star would gradually cool and contract as it radiated energy into space. As it contracted, the gas would heat up due to compression (a consequence of the virial theorem), which would partly compensate for the energy loss but could not prevent ultimate collapse in the absence of a nuclear energy source. The genius of the stellar equilibrium is its self-regulating nature: if the core temperature rises slightly, the nuclear reaction rate increases, producing more energy, which causes the core to expand and cool, reducing the reaction rate back toward the equilibrium value. This thermostat mechanism ensures that stars burn their nuclear fuel at a rate determined by their mass rather than by the accidental details of their ignition.

The transport of energy from the core to the surface is another critical aspect of stellar structure. Energy generated by nuclear reactions in the core must travel outward through the stellar interior and eventually be radiated from the photosphere into space. There are two main energy transport mechanisms: radiative diffusion, in which energy is carried by photons that are repeatedly absorbed and reemitted as they random-walk outward through the opaque stellar plasma, and convection, in which buoyant hot gas bubbles rise toward the surface while cooler, denser gas sinks toward the core in a circulatory pattern. Which mechanism dominates at a given layer depends on the temperature gradient and the opacity of the gas. In the Sun, the inner roughly two-thirds of the radius is radiative — energy is transported outward by photons — while the outer third is convective. More massive stars are convective in their cores and radiative in their outer layers, because their much higher luminosities demand steep temperature gradients that trigger convective instability.

The Vogt-Russell theorem states that the structure of a star in hydrostatic equilibrium with steady nuclear burning is uniquely determined by its mass and chemical composition. This theorem, which holds under idealized conditions, explains why stars of the same mass and composition are essentially identical in their properties — radius, luminosity, and surface temperature — regardless of how they formed. It also explains why the main sequence in the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram is a narrow band rather than a broad scatter: stars on the main sequence are in hydrostatic equilibrium with hydrogen-burning cores, and for each mass there is a unique equilibrium structure that determines the star's position on the diagram. The Vogt-Russell theorem breaks down under certain conditions — rapid rotation, strong magnetic fields, or substantial mass loss — but it provides the conceptual foundation for understanding why the main sequence exists and why it has the properties it does.

Stellar models, numerical computations that solve the stellar structure equations simultaneously at thousands of radial shells, have been refined over decades and now agree with observations with remarkable precision. The standard solar model, which predicts the Sun's internal temperature, density, and composition profiles, is tested most stringently by helioseismology — the study of acoustic oscillations (p-modes) that propagate through the Sun's interior and are observed at the surface as Doppler shifts in spectral lines. The frequencies of these oscillations depend sensitively on the sound speed profile within the Sun, which in turn depends on the temperature, density, and composition profiles. The agreement between the predicted and observed oscillation frequencies is better than one part in a thousand in most of the solar interior, providing strong confirmation that our understanding of stellar structure is fundamentally correct. A small discrepancy near the base of the convective zone, known as the solar abundance problem, is associated with recent revisions to solar photospheric abundances and remains an active area of research.

The virial theorem provides a powerful general relationship between the gravitational potential energy and the kinetic energy of a gravitationally bound system in equilibrium. For an ideal gas, the virial theorem states that the total kinetic energy is minus one-half the total gravitational potential energy. This relationship has a remarkable implication: when a star loses energy by radiating, its total energy decreases, but its thermal energy increases — the star heats up as it cools. This counterintuitive property is sometimes called the negative heat capacity of stars and is a direct consequence of the virial theorem. It explains why main sequence stars powered by nuclear burning can maintain stable equilibria: if the core cools slightly, the star contracts, heating up and increasing the nuclear burning rate until equilibrium is restored. It also explains why stars in their final stages of evolution, after nuclear fuel is exhausted, simply contract and heat up rather than cooling.

The internal structure of different types of stars reveals the full richness of the stellar structure equations. Low-mass red dwarf stars, with masses below about 0.3 solar masses, are fully convective throughout their interiors, because the high opacity of their cool gas and the steep temperature gradient generated by their luminosity demands convective transport everywhere. Solar-type stars have a radiative core and a convective envelope, as described above. Massive O-type stars have convective cores driven by the steep temperature dependence of the CNO cycle (which dominates their energy production) and radiative envelopes. The differences in internal structure among stars of different masses have observable consequences: fully convective stars mix their hydrogen fuel throughout their interiors and can sustain nuclear burning for far longer relative to their mass than partially mixed stars; massive stars drive strong stellar winds from their radiative envelopes; and the depth of the convective envelope in solar-type stars determines the efficiency of magnetic dynamo activity and thus the stellar rotation rate and activity level.
