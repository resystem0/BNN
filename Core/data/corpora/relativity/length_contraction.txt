Length contraction, also known as Lorentz contraction or Lorentz-FitzGerald contraction, is the phenomenon whereby an object in motion relative to an observer is measured to be shorter along the direction of motion than it is when measured at rest. Like time dilation, it is a real physical effect and not a perspective illusion, though its interpretation requires careful attention to what we mean by measuring the length of a moving object.

To measure the length of a moving rod, an observer must record the positions of both ends of the rod simultaneously, in the observer's own frame of reference. This requirement — that both endpoints be noted at the same time — is crucial, because in relativity there is no absolute notion of simultaneity. What "at the same time" means depends on who is asking. Given this careful definition, an observer who sees a rod moving past at speed v will find that the rod's length L in the direction of motion is shorter than its proper length L-naught, which is the length measured in the rod's rest frame. The relationship is L equals L-naught divided by gamma, where gamma is the Lorentz factor. Since gamma is always at least one, the observed length is always less than or equal to the proper length, with equality only when the rod is at rest relative to the observer.

The mathematical origin of length contraction is the Lorentz transformation, the set of equations relating the coordinates of an event as measured in two different inertial frames in relative motion. These transformations, which Einstein derived from his two postulates, relate not just time intervals but spatial intervals as well. A spatial separation that appears as a pure distance in one frame appears in another frame as a mixture of spatial and temporal separation, because the Lorentz transformation mixes space and time coordinates. Length contraction is the spatial manifestation of this mixing, just as time dilation is the temporal manifestation.

The connection between length contraction and the relativity of simultaneity is deep and worth exploring. Suppose Alice is at rest and measures the length of a rod that Bob carries past her. She marks the position of the rod's front and back end simultaneously, in her frame, and measures the distance between the marks. This is shorter than the rod's proper length because the marks were made simultaneously in Alice's frame. But from Bob's perspective, Alice did not mark both ends simultaneously — she marked the front end before the back end (or vice versa), because the two marking events happen at different locations and Bob's concept of "simultaneous" differs from Alice's. From Bob's perspective, Alice measured the distance between a point at the front of the rod and a point behind the back of the rod, because she waited too long before marking the back. Of course the result was shorter than the proper length — she was not measuring the rod correctly, from Bob's point of view. Yet both descriptions are internally consistent and both agree on the measured length. The relativity of simultaneity is not just a philosophical curiosity but the very mechanism that makes length contraction consistent between observers.

The concept of a reference frame deserves elaboration. A reference frame is a coordinate system with respect to which positions and times of events are specified, together with a set of synchronized clocks distributed throughout space. An inertial reference frame is one that is not accelerating. In special relativity, the laws of physics take their simplest form in inertial frames, and the Lorentz transformation gives the relationship between any two such frames. The proper length of an object is defined in the object's rest frame, the frame in which the object is at rest. Length contraction tells us that any other inertial frame, moving relative to the object, will measure a shorter length along the direction of motion. There is no contraction perpendicular to the direction of motion; transverse dimensions are unaffected.

A vivid thought experiment illustrating both length contraction and the role of reference frames is the pole-barn paradox, also called the ladder paradox. Suppose a runner carries a pole whose proper length is ten meters, and the pole is moving at high speed toward a barn whose proper length is eight meters. Due to length contraction, the pole in the barn's rest frame is shortened, and if the speed is high enough, the contracted pole fits entirely inside the barn at one moment. The barn doors can briefly both be closed simultaneously, with the pole inside. Now consider the runner's frame. In this frame, the barn is moving toward the runner and is therefore length-contracted, making it even shorter than eight meters, while the pole retains its proper length of ten meters. How can the pole fit inside a barn shorter than itself? The resolution lies in simultaneity. The two events — closing the front door and closing the back door — are simultaneous in the barn's rest frame but not in the runner's frame. In the runner's frame, the doors do not close at the same time, and a careful analysis shows that the pole is never fully inside the barn at any single moment according to the runner. Both accounts are consistent; they simply describe the same physical events using different notions of simultaneity.

Length contraction has been indirectly confirmed by experiments on relativistic particle beams, where the contracted fields of fast-moving charges have measurable effects, and by the successful application of relativistic kinematics in particle accelerators. Direct measurement of length contraction for macroscopic objects has not been achieved because the velocities involved would be impractically large, but the consistency of special relativity's predictions with experimental data in countless other contexts provides overwhelming indirect confirmation.

An important subtlety is that length contraction is not what a moving object actually looks like to the eye or a camera. Visual observation involves light traveling from the object to the observer, and since different parts of the object are at different distances, the light reaching the observer at any instant departed at different times. When this light-travel-time effect is properly accounted for, a rapidly moving sphere appears rotated rather than flattened, an effect described by Penrose-Terrell rotation. The contraction is real in the sense of a length measurement — marking endpoints simultaneously in the observer's frame — but visual appearance is a different matter that requires accounting for the propagation of light.
