The special theory of relativity, published by Albert Einstein in 1905, rests on two deceptively simple postulates that together overturn centuries of classical intuition about space, time, and motion. Understanding these postulates and the experimental crisis that motivated them requires tracing a thread from nineteenth-century electromagnetism through to one of the most consequential restructurings of physical thought in history.

The first postulate is the principle of relativity, which states that the laws of physics are the same in all inertial reference frames. An inertial frame is one that moves at constant velocity — neither accelerating nor rotating. This principle was not new with Einstein; Galileo had articulated a version of it for mechanics centuries earlier. If you are on a smoothly moving train and drop a ball, the ball falls straight down relative to you, just as it would if you were standing still on a platform. The laws governing the ball's motion do not distinguish between your state of rest and your state of uniform motion. Einstein extended this equivalence to encompass all of physics, including electromagnetism and optics, not merely mechanics.

The second postulate is more radical and more surprising: the speed of light in a vacuum is the same for all inertial observers, regardless of the motion of the light source or the observer. Light travels at approximately 299,792,458 meters per second, and this speed, conventionally denoted c, is not relative to anything. Whether you are moving toward the light source, away from it, or perpendicular to it, you measure the same speed for light. This strikes most newcomers as bizarre, because our everyday experience with relative velocities suggests otherwise. If you are driving at 60 kilometers per hour and another car passes you at 80 kilometers per hour, you perceive the other car as moving at 20 kilometers per hour relative to you. But light refuses to behave this way.

The experimental background for Einstein's second postulate is closely tied to the famous Michelson-Morley experiment of 1887. In the nineteenth century, physicists believed that light, as a wave, must propagate through a medium, just as sound waves travel through air. This hypothetical medium was called the luminiferous aether, and it was imagined to fill all of space. If the aether existed, then Earth moving through it would create an "aether wind," and the speed of light measured in the direction of Earth's motion should differ from the speed measured perpendicular to it, by an amount calculable from Earth's orbital velocity.

Albert Michelson, later joined by Edward Morley, built an extraordinarily precise interferometer to detect this difference. The device split a beam of light into two perpendicular paths, reflected both beams back to a common detector, and looked for interference fringes that would indicate a difference in travel time. The experiment was sensitive enough to detect the expected aether wind. Yet the result was null: no difference was found. Michelson and Morley repeated the experiment at different times of year, different orientations, different locations, always with the same null result. The aether simply did not reveal itself.

Various attempts were made to salvage the aether concept. Hendrik Lorentz and George FitzGerald independently proposed that objects contract in the direction of motion through the aether by just the right amount to explain the null result. This ad hoc contraction, derived by Lorentz from the equations of electromagnetism, correctly predicted the interferometer outcome but provided no deeper understanding. The Lorentz transformation equations that describe this contraction would later appear in Einstein's theory, but with a completely different physical interpretation.

Einstein's genius was to take the null result not as a puzzle to be patched but as a fundamental truth about nature. Rather than asking why the aether wind is undetectable, he proposed that there is no aether, that the speed of light is genuinely invariant, and then asked what this implies about the structure of space and time. The answer required abandoning the Newtonian concept of absolute simultaneity — the assumption that two events happening at the same time for one observer happen at the same time for all observers.

When the two postulates are combined, they generate a rich and internally consistent theory. The Lorentz transformation equations, which Lorentz had derived phenomenologically, emerge from Einstein's postulates as necessary consequences. Time dilation, length contraction, and the relativity of simultaneity all follow. The theory also explains why Maxwell's equations of electromagnetism, which had seemed to single out one reference frame as special (the one in which the speed of light has the value c), are actually valid in all inertial frames — because c is the same everywhere and for everyone.

The scope of the two postulates is worth emphasizing. The first postulate tells us that no experiment — mechanical, optical, electrical, or otherwise — can distinguish between being at rest and being in uniform motion. There is no absolute rest. The second postulate tells us that the measurement of the speed of light is one such experiment that yields the same result regardless of one's state of motion, which is the most extreme possible manifestation of the first postulate applied to electromagnetism. Together they imply that the universe has a fundamental speed limit, that time is not universal, and that what we call space and time are aspects of a unified four-dimensional entity called spacetime. All of this from two statements that individually seem almost obvious or at worst surprising, but whose combination reshapes the entire framework of physics.
