General relativity, published by Einstein in its final form in November 1915, is the modern theory of gravitation and one of the two pillars of contemporary physics alongside quantum mechanics. Where special relativity addressed the relationship between inertial frames in uniform relative motion, general relativity extended the framework to include accelerating frames and, most profoundly, described gravity not as a force but as the curvature of spacetime caused by the presence of mass and energy.

The conceptual foundation of general relativity is the equivalence principle, a deep physical insight that Einstein later called "the happiest thought of my life." The equivalence principle in its weak form states that gravitational mass and inertial mass are equal. Gravitational mass is the charge-like quantity that determines how strongly an object is attracted by gravity; inertial mass is the quantity that resists acceleration. Experiment shows these are the same, but Newton's theory offered no explanation for this coincidence. Einstein elevated this equality to a fundamental principle and drew far-reaching consequences from it.

The strong equivalence principle states that all physical laws, including those of special relativity, hold in a sufficiently small freely falling laboratory, and that no local experiment can distinguish between being in a gravitational field and being in an accelerating reference frame. If you are in a windowless box and feel a downward force, you cannot tell whether you are sitting on Earth's surface in a gravitational field or being accelerated upward in empty space by a rocket engine. Conversely, if you are in free fall — in an orbiting spacecraft, for instance — you feel weightless, just as you would in deep space far from any gravitational source. Gravity and acceleration are locally equivalent.

This equivalence has a striking implication for light. If you are in a box accelerating upward in empty space and you shine a horizontal beam of light across the box, the beam will appear to curve downward because the floor is accelerating up to meet the light. By the equivalence principle, the same must happen in a gravitational field: light must bend when passing through a gravitational field. This prediction was spectacularly confirmed during the solar eclipse of 1919, when Arthur Eddington measured the apparent displacement of stars near the Sun's edge and found a deflection consistent with Einstein's theory. The result made Einstein world-famous and established general relativity as a serious competitor to Newtonian gravity.

The mathematical framework of general relativity is Riemannian geometry — the geometry of curved spaces generalized to four-dimensional spacetime. In this framework, the geometry of spacetime is described by a metric tensor, a mathematical object that specifies how to measure distances and time intervals at each point in spacetime. The metric tensor carries all the information about the curvature of spacetime. In the absence of matter and energy, spacetime is flat and the metric reduces to the Minkowski metric of special relativity. In the presence of mass and energy, the metric is curved.

The governing equations of general relativity are the Einstein field equations, which relate the curvature of spacetime to the distribution of matter and energy. Written compactly, they state that the Einstein tensor G equals 8 pi G over c-fourth times the stress-energy tensor T, where G here is Newton's gravitational constant and T is the tensor encoding the density and flux of energy, momentum, and stress in spacetime. The Einstein tensor is a particular combination of the Ricci curvature tensor and the metric, constructed so that the equations are consistent with conservation of energy and momentum. Despite their compact notation, the Einstein field equations are ten coupled, nonlinear partial differential equations — enormously difficult to solve in general.

General relativity reduces to Newtonian gravity in the limit of weak gravitational fields and slow motion. This correspondence principle was built into the theory by construction and ensures that Einstein's theory agrees with centuries of successful astronomical predictions in the appropriate regime. But in strong-field or high-velocity situations, the predictions diverge sharply, and general relativity has been confirmed in each case.

The precession of Mercury's perihelion was an anomaly in classical celestial mechanics — Mercury's orbit precesses slightly more than can be explained by the gravitational influence of the other planets. The discrepancy is about 43 arcseconds per century, small but robustly measured. General relativity predicts exactly this amount due to the curvature of spacetime near the relatively strong gravitational field of the Sun. This was one of the first confirmations of general relativity, and it resolved a puzzle that had persisted for decades.

Gravitational time dilation is another prediction of general relativity. Clocks run more slowly in stronger gravitational fields. A clock on Earth's surface runs slightly slower than a clock at high altitude, where the gravitational field is weaker. This effect is distinct from the special relativistic time dilation due to velocity, though in practice both occur simultaneously for satellites. The Pound-Rebka experiment in 1959 confirmed gravitational time dilation using gamma rays sent up and down a tower at Harvard, measuring the gravitational redshift of photons climbing out of Earth's gravitational field.

The Einstein field equations admit a cosmological constant term, which Einstein originally introduced to allow a static universe — a decision he later called his greatest blunder after Hubble discovered the expansion of the universe. The cosmological constant has returned to prominence as a description of dark energy, the mysterious component driving the accelerating expansion of the universe discovered in 1998.

Among the most dramatic predictions of general relativity are black holes, gravitational waves, and the expanding universe — all of which have now been directly observed. The theory has passed every experimental test to date, from precise measurements in the solar system to observations of compact binary pulsars to the detection of gravitational waves from merging black holes by LIGO. Yet general relativity is known to be incomplete: it is a classical theory that breaks down at the quantum scale, and reconciling it with quantum mechanics remains one of the deepest unsolved problems in theoretical physics.
