Black holes are regions of spacetime where the curvature has become so extreme that nothing — not matter, not light, not any signal — can escape to the outside universe. They are among the most remarkable predictions of general relativity and among the most studied objects in modern astrophysics. The boundary of a black hole, the surface of no return, is called the event horizon, and it plays a central role in the physics and philosophy of these objects.

The simplest black hole solution in general relativity is the Schwarzschild solution, found by Karl Schwarzschild in 1916 within weeks of Einstein's publication of the field equations, while Schwarzschild was serving on the Russian front in World War I. The Schwarzschild solution describes the spacetime geometry outside a perfectly spherical, non-rotating, uncharged mass. At the heart of this solution lies the Schwarzschild radius, given by r_s equals 2GM over c-squared, where G is Newton's gravitational constant, M is the mass, and c is the speed of light. For the Sun, the Schwarzschild radius is about 3 kilometers; for Earth it is about 9 millimeters. If all of the Sun's mass were compressed inside a sphere of radius 3 kilometers, the escape velocity would equal the speed of light at that surface, and a black hole would form.

The Schwarzschild radius defines the event horizon of a non-rotating black hole. An observer falling through the event horizon from outside would, in their own experience, cross it without any local drama — the horizon is not a wall or a physical surface, and a freely falling observer feels nothing special at the moment of crossing. However, they can never communicate back to the outside world. Events inside the horizon are causally disconnected from the external universe: no signal, no matter what its speed, can travel outward from inside the horizon.

From the perspective of a distant external observer watching someone fall into a black hole, the picture is entirely different due to gravitational time dilation and the behavior of light near the horizon. As the infalling observer approaches the horizon, their clock appears to slow more and more from the external observer's perspective. Light emitted by the infalling observer becomes increasingly redshifted as it climbs out of the deepening gravitational well. In the external observer's coordinate time, the infalling observer appears to asymptotically approach the horizon but never quite reach it, fading and redshifting into invisibility. The infalling observer, however, crosses the horizon in finite proper time and continues to fall toward the singularity.

Inside the event horizon, the roles of space and time are effectively exchanged in the Schwarzschild coordinates. The singularity at the center is not a point in space but a moment in time — all timelike trajectories inside the horizon lead inevitably to the singularity, just as all trajectories in the outside universe lead inevitably to the future. This is a consequence of the extreme curvature and the causal structure of the Schwarzschild interior.

At the singularity, the curvature of spacetime becomes infinite according to general relativity. Physical quantities like tidal forces and energy densities diverge. This is generally interpreted not as a physical fact about nature but as a signal that general relativity breaks down at the singularity — that a complete theory of quantum gravity would be needed to describe what actually happens at the center of a black hole. Roger Penrose's singularity theorems, proven in the 1960s, show that singularities are generic predictions of general relativity whenever matter undergoes gravitational collapse past a certain point, under very general conditions. The theorems do not describe the nature of the singularity in detail, but they establish that the formation of a singularity is unavoidable given the premises of classical general relativity.

Rotating black holes are described by the Kerr solution, found in 1963. Real astrophysical black holes are expected to have angular momentum, accumulated from the angular momentum of the matter that formed them. The Kerr solution introduces new features including a region outside the event horizon called the ergosphere, where the rotation of spacetime itself is so extreme that nothing can remain stationary relative to the distant stars. Objects in the ergosphere are dragged around by the rotation of spacetime regardless of their own motion. The Penrose process allows energy to be extracted from a Kerr black hole by exploiting the ergosphere — a particle falling into the ergosphere can split such that one fragment falls into the black hole with negative energy and the other escapes with more energy than the original particle, extracting rotational energy from the black hole.

Stephen Hawking's 1974 discovery that black holes are not entirely black was a landmark in theoretical physics. Applying quantum field theory in the curved spacetime of a black hole, Hawking showed that black holes emit thermal radiation with a temperature inversely proportional to their mass. This Hawking radiation arises because the event horizon separates pairs of virtual particles that are continuously produced by quantum fluctuations in the vacuum. One particle of a pair falls into the black hole and one escapes, and the escaping particle carries away energy. Over time, if a black hole is not accreting matter, it loses mass through Hawking radiation and eventually evaporates. For stellar-mass and larger black holes, the Hawking temperature is far below the cosmic microwave background temperature and the effect is negligible. For microscopic black holes, the temperature would be enormous and the evaporation rapid.

Hawking radiation poses the black hole information paradox. If a black hole forms from a specific quantum state of matter and then evaporates by emitting thermal radiation, has the information about the initial state been irretrievably lost? Thermal radiation is random and carries no detailed information about what fell in. Yet the laws of quantum mechanics are unitary, meaning information is never lost. The resolution of this paradox remains one of the most active research areas in fundamental physics, and it connects general relativity, quantum mechanics, thermodynamics, and information theory in ways not yet fully understood.
