Time dilation is one of the most striking predictions of special relativity, and it has been confirmed experimentally to extraordinary precision. The phenomenon arises directly from the two postulates of special relativity: when a clock moves relative to an observer, that observer measures the moving clock as ticking more slowly than an identical clock at rest relative to them. This is not an illusion or a measurement artifact — it is a genuine difference in the rate at which time elapses along different paths through spacetime.

The simplest way to derive time dilation is to consider a light clock, a device that marks time by bouncing a photon between two parallel mirrors. When the clock is at rest relative to an observer, the photon travels straight up and down, and each tick represents the time for the photon to traverse the mirror separation distance d at speed c, giving a tick duration of 2d/c. Now consider this same clock moving horizontally at speed v relative to a second observer. From the perspective of the second observer, the photon does not travel straight up and down but rather traces a zigzag path, because while the photon is in transit the mirrors have moved horizontally. The photon still travels at speed c, because the speed of light is invariant, but it now travels a longer diagonal path. Simple Pythagorean geometry shows that the path length per tick for the moving observer is longer by the Lorentz factor gamma, where gamma equals one divided by the square root of one minus v-squared over c-squared. Since the photon covers more distance at the same speed, it takes longer to complete each tick. The moving clock, from the stationary observer's perspective, runs slow by the factor gamma.

This factor gamma is always greater than or equal to one, equaling one when v equals zero and growing without bound as v approaches c. At everyday speeds, gamma is so close to one that the effect is imperceptible. At half the speed of light, gamma is approximately 1.15, meaning clocks run about 15 percent slower. At 99 percent of the speed of light, gamma is approximately 7.09, meaning a moving clock runs more than seven times slower. At 99.9 percent of c, gamma exceeds 22.

The twin paradox is the most famous thought experiment illustrating time dilation. Imagine two twins, conventionally named Alice and Bob. Alice stays on Earth while Bob boards a spacecraft and travels at a high fraction of the speed of light to a distant star, then turns around and returns. From Alice's perspective on Earth, Bob's clock runs slow during his entire journey, so when he returns, less time has elapsed for Bob than for Alice. Bob is younger than Alice when they reunite. The apparent paradox arises when one naively asks why we cannot view Alice as the one who was "moving" relative to Bob and therefore expect the roles to be reversed. The resolution is that the situation is not symmetric: Bob undergoes acceleration when he turns around at the distant star, while Alice remains in an inertial frame throughout. The asymmetry in the twins' histories — specifically, Bob's change of inertial frames — is what makes Bob's clock genuinely accumulate less time. A full calculation, properly accounting for the acceleration phase or using the methods of general relativity, confirms that Bob is younger on his return.

The twin paradox is not merely hypothetical. Experiments using precise atomic clocks have confirmed the time dilation prediction. The most direct test was performed by Hafele and Keating in 1971, when they flew cesium atomic clocks around the world on commercial aircraft and compared them to clocks that remained on the ground. The results agreed with the relativistic prediction to within experimental uncertainty, accounting for both special relativistic time dilation due to the aircraft's speed and general relativistic effects due to altitude. Similarly, muons created by cosmic rays in the upper atmosphere provide a natural confirmation: muons are unstable particles with a half-life of about 2.2 microseconds in their rest frame. At the speeds they travel, roughly 99.7 percent of c, classical physics would predict that very few should survive the journey to Earth's surface. Yet large numbers reach the ground, exactly as predicted by time dilation — their internal clocks run slow relative to the lab frame, effectively prolonging their lifetime from the observer's perspective.

Perhaps the most practical application of time dilation is in the Global Positioning System. GPS satellites orbit Earth at altitudes of about 20,200 kilometers and travel at approximately 3.9 kilometers per second relative to the ground. This speed causes their clocks to run slow by about 7.2 microseconds per day relative to ground clocks, a special relativistic effect. However, the satellites are also at higher altitude, where the gravitational potential is weaker, and this general relativistic effect causes their clocks to run fast relative to ground clocks by about 45.9 microseconds per day. The net effect is that GPS satellite clocks run fast by about 38.4 microseconds per day. This is a substantial discrepancy: since GPS uses timing to determine position with a precision of meters, an uncorrected 38.4 microsecond per day error would cause position errors of roughly 10 kilometers per day, accumulating continuously. GPS receivers and satellite systems are therefore specifically designed to account for relativistic corrections. The success of GPS navigation is, among other things, an ongoing experimental confirmation of relativistic time dilation built into the fabric of everyday life.

Time dilation forces us to abandon the classical notion of a universal time that flows at the same rate everywhere and for everyone. Instead, time is a coordinate in spacetime, and the rate at which a clock advances depends on the clock's trajectory through that four-dimensional geometry. Clocks at rest in a given frame all agree with each other, but clocks in relative motion do not, and the disagreement is not symmetric in any absolute sense — it depends on the entire history of the clock's motion, including any accelerations.
