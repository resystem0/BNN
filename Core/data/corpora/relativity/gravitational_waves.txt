Gravitational waves are ripples in the curvature of spacetime that propagate outward from accelerating masses at the speed of light. Predicted by Einstein in 1916 as a consequence of his general theory of relativity, they went undetected for a century due to their extraordinary weakness — by the time they reach Earth from astronomical sources, the distortions in spacetime they cause are measured in fractions of the diameter of a proton. Their direct detection in 2015 by the LIGO collaboration opened an entirely new window on the universe and confirmed one of general relativity's most dramatic predictions.

The physical origin of gravitational waves can be understood by analogy with electromagnetic waves, though the analogy is imperfect. An accelerating electric charge radiates electromagnetic waves. Similarly, accelerating masses radiate gravitational waves, but the details differ significantly because gravity is described by a tensor field rather than a vector field, and there is no negative gravitational charge equivalent to the negative electric charge. As a result, gravitational radiation requires not just acceleration but a time-varying quadrupole moment — roughly speaking, an asymmetric acceleration. A perfectly spherically symmetric pulsating star radiates no gravitational waves. A binary system of two massive objects orbiting each other, however, has a large and rapidly varying quadrupole moment and is an efficient source.

When a gravitational wave passes through a region of space, it alternately stretches space in one direction while squeezing it in the perpendicular direction, and then reverses. This is called a quadrupolar strain pattern. If you imagine a ring of free particles in the plane perpendicular to the wave's direction of travel, the wave will distort the ring into an ellipse that oscillates between elongation in one axis and elongation in the perpendicular axis, at twice the frequency of the wave source. The amplitude of this strain is characterized by a dimensionless number h, called the strain, which measures the fractional change in distance between two test masses. For the first detected gravitational wave event, the strain at Earth was approximately 10 to the minus-21 — meaning that two mirrors separated by 4 kilometers changed their separation by about 10 to the minus-18 meters, less than one thousandth the diameter of a proton.

The Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory, LIGO, consists of two facilities in the United States — one in Hanford, Washington, and one in Livingston, Louisiana — separated by about 3,000 kilometers. Each facility houses an L-shaped interferometer with arms 4 kilometers long. A laser beam is split and sent down both arms, reflected off mirrors at the ends, and recombined. In the absence of a gravitational wave, the two beams arrive back at the beam splitter with a precisely calibrated phase relationship. When a gravitational wave passes, it changes the arm lengths by different amounts — stretching one arm while squeezing the other — and this introduces a phase shift in the recombined beams that can be detected as a change in the interference pattern. The sensitivity required to detect a strain of 10 to the minus-21 over a 4-kilometer baseline is staggering and required decades of engineering development to achieve.

On September 14, 2015, both LIGO detectors recorded a signal consistent with gravitational waves from a binary black hole merger. The signal, lasting about 0.2 seconds and sweeping upward in frequency from about 35 Hz to 150 Hz before terminating abruptly, matched the predictions of general relativity for the inspiral and merger of two black holes with masses of approximately 29 and 36 solar masses, at a distance of about 1.3 billion light-years. The resulting merged black hole had a mass of about 62 solar masses, with approximately 3 solar masses of energy radiated away as gravitational waves in a fraction of a second — briefly outshining the electromagnetic output of the entire observable universe. This event, designated GW150914, was announced in February 2016 and earned the LIGO founders the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2017.

Since then, LIGO and its European counterpart Virgo have detected dozens of gravitational wave events, including mergers of binary black holes, binary neutron stars, and mixed systems. The binary neutron star merger detected on August 17, 2017, designated GW170817, was particularly significant because it was accompanied by electromagnetic signals — a short gamma-ray burst detected by the Fermi telescope and optical, X-ray, radio, and ultraviolet counterparts observed across the world. This multimessenger event provided constraints on neutron star equations of state, confirmed that short gamma-ray bursts are produced by neutron star mergers, measured the speed of gravitational waves to be equal to the speed of light to within 10 to the minus-15, and enabled a new independent measurement of the Hubble constant.

Long before direct detection, indirect evidence for gravitational wave emission had been accumulating from the Hulse-Taylor binary pulsar, discovered in 1974. This system consists of two neutron stars in a tight orbit, one of which is a pulsar whose precise radio pulses serve as a clock. Over decades of observation, the orbital period of the binary has been decreasing at exactly the rate predicted by general relativity if the system is losing energy to gravitational wave emission. Hulse and Taylor received the Nobel Prize in 1993 for this discovery.

The future of gravitational wave astronomy includes the LISA mission, a space-based interferometer consisting of three spacecraft separated by 2.5 million kilometers in a triangular formation, planned for launch in the 2030s. LISA will be sensitive to much lower frequencies than ground-based detectors and will detect gravitational waves from sources including supermassive black hole mergers, extreme mass-ratio inspirals, and a stochastic background from the early universe. Pulsar timing arrays, which use networks of millisecond pulsars as natural arms of a galaxy-sized gravitational wave detector, have recently reported evidence for a gravitational wave background at nanohertz frequencies, likely from supermassive binary black holes distributed throughout the cosmos. The era of gravitational wave astronomy has just begun, and each new detection carries information about the most extreme events in the universe, conveyed by a messenger that passes through ordinary matter without interaction.
