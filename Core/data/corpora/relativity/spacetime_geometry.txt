The geometry of spacetime in special relativity is described by Minkowski space, the mathematical framework introduced by Hermann Minkowski in 1908, building on Einstein's 1905 work. Minkowski's insight was that special relativity could be understood most naturally not as a theory about transformations between reference frames but as a theory about the geometry of a four-dimensional continuum in which space and time are unified. "Henceforth," Minkowski declared in a famous lecture, "space by itself, and time by itself, are doomed to fade away into mere shadows, and only a kind of union of the two will preserve an independent reality."

Minkowski space is a four-dimensional manifold equipped with a metric that differs fundamentally from the Euclidean metric of ordinary geometry. In Euclidean space, the distance between two points is given by the square root of the sum of squares of the coordinate differences: the familiar Pythagorean theorem. The Minkowski metric introduces a crucial sign difference. The spacetime interval between two events, rather than being a positive-definite distance, is given by s-squared equals negative c-squared t-squared plus x-squared plus y-squared plus z-squared, or equivalently with the sign convention flipped. This interval can be positive, negative, or zero, and the sign carries profound physical meaning.

When the spacetime interval squared is negative (using the sign convention above), the interval is called timelike. Two events with a timelike separation can be causally connected — it is possible for a signal or a massive object to travel from one event to the other, since the necessary speed would be less than c. When the interval squared is positive, the interval is spacelike. Events with spacelike separation cannot influence each other because no signal traveling at or below c could connect them. When the interval squared is zero, the interval is null or lightlike: the two events are connected only by a light signal traveling at exactly c.

The spacetime interval, unlike coordinate distances or time separations, is an invariant — all inertial observers agree on its value, despite disagreeing about the individual spatial and temporal coordinate differences. This invariance is the spacetime analogue of rotational invariance in Euclidean geometry, where two observers oriented differently agree on distances even though they assign different x and y coordinate differences. In Minkowski space, the "rotation" that mixes space and time coordinates when changing reference frames is a Lorentz transformation, and it preserves the spacetime interval exactly as an ordinary rotation preserves Euclidean distance.

Light cones are among the most important structures in spacetime geometry. At any event P in spacetime, the set of all null intervals from P forms two cone-shaped surfaces in spacetime — one opening toward the future and one toward the past. These are the future and past light cones of P. The future light cone contains all events that can be reached from P by a light signal or by any signal traveling at or below the speed of light. The past light cone contains all events from which a signal could have reached P. Everything inside the future light cone of P is causally influenced by P; everything inside the past light cone could have causally influenced P. Events outside both light cones — in the spacelike region — are causally disconnected from P.

The causal structure of spacetime, defined by the light cone at every point, is one of the most fundamental and robust features of spacetime geometry. In general relativity, where spacetime is curved, the local causal structure is still governed by local light cones, but the global causal structure can become very complex. In the Schwarzschild geometry of a black hole, for instance, the light cones tilt increasingly toward the singularity as one approaches and crosses the event horizon. Once inside the horizon, all future-directed causal paths lead toward the singularity — the singularity is in the causal future of every interior event.

The worldline of a particle is its trajectory through spacetime, a curve tracing the sequence of events the particle occupies at successive moments. For a massive particle moving at constant velocity, the worldline is a straight line in spacetime. For an accelerating particle, the worldline is curved. In Minkowski space, the proper time elapsed along a worldline — the time measured by a clock carried along that worldline — is given by the integral of the square root of the negative of the metric applied to the tangent vector. Straight worldlines, corresponding to inertial motion, maximize the proper time between two events. This is the geometric statement of the twin paradox: the twin who travels in a straight line through spacetime (stays at rest) ages more than the twin whose worldline is curved by acceleration.

In general relativity, the metric is no longer the flat Minkowski metric but varies from point to point, encoding the gravitational field. Geodesics — the generalization of straight lines to curved spacetime — are the worldlines of freely falling objects. The equivalence principle is reflected geometrically: in a freely falling frame, spacetime is locally flat and the geodesics are locally straight, recovering special relativity in the small. On larger scales, the curvature of spacetime — caused by the distribution of mass and energy — causes geodesics to converge or diverge, which is what we observe macroscopically as gravitational attraction or repulsion.

The Penrose diagram, invented by Roger Penrose, is a powerful tool for representing the global causal structure of a spacetime. By applying a conformal transformation that compresses infinite distances and times into a finite diagram while preserving the local light cone structure, Penrose diagrams make it possible to visualize the global properties of spacetimes including black holes, de Sitter space, and cosmological models. In a Penrose diagram for the Schwarzschild black hole, for example, the entire external universe occupies a finite triangular region, and the future singularity appears as a spacelike boundary above the event horizon, clearly showing that all future-directed paths inside the horizon lead to the singularity.

The geometry of spacetime is not merely a mathematical convenience but a physical reality. The curvature of spacetime can be measured — by observing the deflection of light, the precession of orbits, the gravitational redshift of photons, or the passage of gravitational waves. The universe has a geometry, and that geometry is dynamical, influenced by matter and energy, and in turn determining the motions of matter and energy. This is the central insight of general relativity and the fundamental departure from the static, absolute space and time of Newtonian physics.
