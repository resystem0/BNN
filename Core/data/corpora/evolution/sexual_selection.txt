Sexual selection, the component of natural selection that arises from differential success in obtaining mates, was recognized by Darwin as a distinct and sometimes puzzling evolutionary force. Darwin was troubled by traits like the peacock's tail, the elaborate plumage of male birds of paradise, and the massive antlers of elk — structures so costly to produce and maintain, and so conspicuous to predators, that they seemed to defy the logic of survival-of-the-fittest natural selection. His solution was to distinguish two categories of natural selection: natural selection acting on survival, and sexual selection acting on mating success. A peacock's tail may reduce its bearer's survival probability, but if it dramatically increases his probability of mating, the net effect on reproductive success — the true currency of evolution — can still be positive. Over evolutionary time, sexual selection can drive the elaboration of traits far beyond what survival selection alone would produce.

Darwin distinguished two mechanisms of sexual selection: mate competition and mate choice. Competition among members of one sex — typically males — for access to members of the other sex produces selection for traits that enhance competitive ability: physical size and strength for combat, weapons like horns and antlers for fighting, resource-holding ability, or social dominance. The outcome of sexual selection by male-male competition is often sexual dimorphism, in which males and females differ dramatically in body size and armament. In polygynous species such as elephant seals, where a small number of dominant males monopolize mating access to large numbers of females, sexual dimorphism can be extreme: adult male elephant seals are four to five times heavier than females and bear large nose structures used in threat displays. Male-biased size dimorphism is broadly correlated with the degree of polygyny across mammal and bird species, providing comparative evidence that competition for mates drives the evolution of male-male competitive traits.

Mate choice, the other face of sexual selection, involves members of one sex — typically females — actively choosing among potential mates, and it produces selection for traits that make the choosy sex prefer certain individuals over others. The existence of female choice was considered controversial in Darwin's time, partly because it seemed to impute rational preferences to animals. But the evidence is now overwhelming: females of hundreds of species actively discriminate among potential mates based on phenotypic cues, and their preferences are consistent and heritable. The evolutionary puzzle shifts from whether female choice exists to why it evolved and what maintains it. Fisher proposed two mechanisms. First, direct benefits: females may choose mates who provide tangible resources — better territories, more parental care, fewer parasites — that directly enhance offspring survival. Second, indirect genetic benefits: females may choose genetically superior mates whose heritable qualities will be expressed in their offspring's own fitness.

The Fisherian runaway process is one of the most elegant and counterintuitive mechanisms in evolutionary biology. Fisher proposed that if females have a heritable preference for a male trait, and if the trait is also heritable, then the genes for the preference and the genes for the trait become genetically correlated through non-random mating. Sons of choosy females tend to inherit both the attractive trait and the sons' daughters' preference for it. This genetic correlation creates a positive feedback loop: the more females prefer long tails, the more attractive long-tailed males are, the more sons and daughters benefit from inheriting long tails and preferences for long tails respectively. The runaway process can theoretically drive traits to extreme values that are far beyond the optimum for natural selection, halted only when the survival cost of the extreme trait balances the mating advantage. The peacock's tail, the extraordinary plumes of male birds of paradise, and the complex songs of many songbirds are plausible candidates for Fisherian runaway elaboration.

The handicap principle, proposed by Amotz Zahavi in 1975 and given rigorous formal justification by Alan Grafen, offers an alternative explanation for why extreme, costly traits can be reliable indicators of genetic quality. Zahavi argued that a costly signal is precisely what makes it honest: only genuinely high-quality males can afford to bear the cost of an extreme ornament or a dangerous display. The peacock's tail is costly to grow and maintain, increases predation risk, and impairs flight — but precisely because it is costly, only peacocks in good condition can afford the luxury. Females who choose the most extravagant males are therefore choosing the most fit males, because only the fittest can bear the handicap. The handicap principle was initially resisted because it was unclear how such a system could be evolutionarily stable — wouldn't low-quality males also evolve costly ornaments as a bluff? Grafen's mathematical models showed that the handicap mechanism can indeed be evolutionarily stable when the cost of the signal scales with quality, so that low-quality individuals cannot profitably fake the signal.

The honest signals that females use in mate assessment span an enormous range of modalities and mechanisms. Carotenoid-based plumage colors in many birds reflect diet quality and immune function because carotenoids cannot be synthesized by vertebrates and must be obtained from food; only healthy, well-fed individuals with effective immune systems can invest carotenoids in bright plumage rather than in immune defense. The elaborate songs of many passerine birds reflect developmental experience during a sensitive period of vocal learning; only individuals raised in high-quality environments with good nutrition develop the most complex songs. Lek behavior, in which males gather at communal display grounds and females visit to assess and choose mates, is a particularly clear case of mate assessment because females can directly compare many males simultaneously, presumably making the assessment process most efficient and the signals most informative.

Good genes models predict that female choice should lead to the evolution of condition-dependent honest signals, the genetic correlation of preferred traits with heritable fitness, and the reduction of genetic load in the population over time — but they also predict that these processes should erode the genetic variation they depend on, creating a paradox known as the lek paradox. If females consistently choose genetically superior males, heritable variation in fitness should be depleted by selection over time, undermining the benefits of choice. The resolution of this paradox likely involves a combination of mechanisms: mutation-selection balance continuously regenerates genetic variation; host-parasite coevolution (the Red Queen) keeps the fitness landscape in constant motion; and the complex interactions among many loci affecting fitness may maintain variance through epistatic effects. Understanding how genetic variation in fitness is maintained in populations with strong sexual selection remains one of the most active areas of evolutionary genetics.
