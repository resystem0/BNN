The history of life on Earth is not a smooth, continuous process of gradual change. Instead, it is punctuated by a relatively small number of pivotal transformations in the fundamental organization of life — events so significant that they changed the rules by which evolution itself operates. John Maynard Smith and Eors Szathmary identified eight such "major evolutionary transitions" in their 1995 book of the same name, arguing that each transition involved a fundamental change in the way information is stored, transmitted, and used by living systems. These transitions are the great creative events of evolution, and understanding them requires going beyond the standard mechanisms of mutation and natural selection to ask deeper questions about the conditions under which new levels of biological organization become possible and stable.

The origin of life itself — the transition from chemistry to biology — is the first and deepest major transition. The central challenge is explaining how self-replicating, heritable information systems could have arisen from non-biological chemistry. The RNA world hypothesis proposes that RNA, which can both carry genetic information and catalyze chemical reactions, played the dual role now divided between DNA (information storage) and proteins (catalysis). Short RNA molecules capable of self-replication may have arisen in protected microenvironments such as hydrothermal vents or mineral surfaces, underwent Darwinian selection for better replication fidelity and faster replication rate, and eventually became compartmentalized within lipid vesicle membranes that provided both physical protection and the spatial proximity necessary for cooperative molecular evolution. The origin of the genetic code — the specific mapping between RNA codons and amino acids — represents a further transition whose mechanistic explanation remains actively debated.

The origin of the eukaryotic cell, roughly 1.5 to 2 billion years ago, represents a transition so profound that it divides life into two major categories: prokaryotes, whose cells lack membrane-bound nuclei and other organelles, and eukaryotes, whose cells possess a nucleus, mitochondria, an endomembrane system, and a cytoskeleton. The most important event in eukaryotic origins was the engulfment of an alphaproteobacterium by an archaeal host cell and the subsequent evolution of a stable endosymbiotic relationship, eventually producing the mitochondrion. This event was transformative because it provided the eukaryotic cell with a vastly expanded capacity for energy production, which may have enabled the evolution of the much larger genomes and more complex gene regulation that characterize eukaryotes. The precise nature of the archaeal host and the mechanism of engulfment are still subjects of active investigation, with new archaeal lineages ("Asgard archaea") recently discovered that are the closest known relatives of eukaryotes.

The origin of sex — the combination of genomes from two parents to produce offspring — is a transition whose adaptive significance has been debated since the late nineteenth century. Sex is in many ways costly: it requires finding mates, and half of sexual offspring are male, producing no eggs of their own. Yet sexual reproduction is nearly universal among eukaryotes and has been maintained for over a billion years. The Red Queen hypothesis, discussed elsewhere, invokes coevolution with parasites as the primary selective force maintaining sex. But sex also recombines alleles in new combinations, creating offspring more variable than asexual reproduction would produce, and this variation may be advantageous in changing environments. Sex also provides a more efficient mechanism for purging deleterious mutations from the genome than asexual reproduction, which is prone to the accumulation of mutations through Muller's ratchet.

The origin of multicellularity — the transition from unicellular to multicellular life — is a transition that has occurred independently many times across the eukaryotic tree of life, in animals, plants, fungi, and multiple algal lineages, indicating that the conditions for this transition are not extraordinarily demanding once certain prerequisites are met. The key prerequisites include cell adhesion (cells must stick together), cell differentiation (different cells must perform different functions), and programmed cell death (cells must sacrifice themselves for the good of the whole). In animal evolution, multicellularity arose from a single-celled ancestor in the choanoflagellate lineage and involved the co-option of genes originally evolved for other purposes — adhesion molecules, signaling molecules, transcription factors — into new roles in cell-cell communication and development. The transition from unicellular to multicellular life transformed the possibilities of evolution because it created new levels of organization at which selection could act.

The origin of chromosomes — the packaging of multiple genes into a single linkage group — is another major transition that addressed a fundamental problem in early evolution. When genes are physically separate, they can evolve independently, and "selfish" genetic elements that replicate faster than their partners can spread at the expense of the cooperative replication system. Packaging genes into chromosomes aligns the evolutionary fates of different genes, because all genes on the same chromosome survive or are lost together. This enforced cooperation reduces the temptation for individual genes to defect from cooperative molecular replication and provides the conditions for the evolution of the sophisticated, coordinated genome organization seen in all modern cellular life.

The emergence of language and cumulative culture in the hominin lineage constitutes the most recent major evolutionary transition and the one most directly relevant to human uniqueness. Language is not simply a more sophisticated form of animal communication; it has recursive, compositional structure that allows an infinite number of novel meanings to be expressed from a finite set of elements. This property, unique to human language, made possible the accumulation and transmission of learned knowledge across generations — the cultural ratchet — which has transformed the pace and nature of human adaptation. Cultural evolution, operating through different mechanisms than genetic evolution, has enabled humans to adapt to virtually every terrestrial environment within a few thousand years, a speed that would require millions of years of genetic evolution. The emergence of language and culture represents a transition in which a new inheritance system — cultural transmission of learned information — was added to the existing system of genetic inheritance.

The major evolutionary transitions share a common theme: they are instances in which previously independent replicating units become integrated into a higher-level unit of replication, with the lower-level units sacrificing their autonomy for the benefits of cooperation. Genes became chromosomes, replicating molecules became cells, prokaryotes became the organelles of eukaryotes, cells became multicellular organisms, individuals became social groups. At each level, the transition required mechanisms to suppress conflicts of interest among the lower-level units — the individual selfish interests of genes, bacteria, or cells — and to align their fitness with the fitness of the higher-level unit. Understanding how such conflict resolution evolves is central to understanding how new levels of biological organization become evolutionarily stable.
