Speciation is the process by which one lineage splits into two or more reproductively isolated lineages, and it is the fundamental mechanism by which the diversity of life is generated. Every species alive today, and every species that has ever existed, arose through speciation from some ancestral lineage. The tree of life is, at its deepest level, a tree of speciation events, and understanding how and why speciation occurs is central to understanding the patterns of biodiversity we observe across the globe. The mechanism of speciation is also one of the areas where evolutionary theory has been most productively extended since Darwin, with the development of formal concepts of species, mathematical models of divergence, and empirical studies of speciation in progress.

The biological species concept, formulated by Ernst Mayr in 1942, remains the most widely used definition of species: a species is a group of actually or potentially interbreeding natural populations that are reproductively isolated from other such groups. This definition makes reproductive isolation the defining criterion of species status, and it directs attention to the mechanisms — prezygotic and postzygotic barriers — that prevent gene flow between populations. Prezygotic barriers prevent the formation of zygotes: geographic separation, temporal differences in breeding season, behavioral incompatibilities in mate recognition, mechanical incompatibilities in copulatory structures, and the failure of sperm to fertilize eggs across species lines. Postzygotic barriers reduce the fitness of hybrid offspring even when they are produced: hybrid inviability, hybrid sterility (as in the mule, a horse-donkey hybrid), and hybrid breakdown, in which second-generation hybrids have reduced fitness.

Allopatric speciation — speciation that occurs when populations are geographically separated — is widely regarded as the most common mode of speciation, particularly for animals with limited dispersal ability. When a population is divided by a geographic barrier — a mountain range, a river, a sea, a desert — gene flow between the subpopulations ceases, and each subpopulation evolves independently under the influence of its local selective pressures, genetic drift, and mutation. Over time, the genetic divergence accumulated in the two subpopulations may reach the point where, if the subpopulations come back into contact, they no longer interbreed effectively — reproductive isolation has evolved as a byproduct of independent evolution in different environments. The rate of allopatric speciation depends on the strength of local selection, the mutation rate, and the effective population sizes of the isolated subpopulations.

Sympatric speciation — speciation in the absence of geographic isolation, within a single freely interbreeding population — was long considered theoretically difficult or impossible, because gene flow should prevent the accumulation of genetic divergence between subgroups sharing the same geographic area. However, theoretical models have shown that sympatric speciation is possible when assortative mating (the tendency to mate with phenotypically similar individuals) is strongly correlated with ecologically divergent traits under disruptive selection. Empirical cases of putative sympatric speciation include the cichlid fishes of Lake Victoria, the apple maggot fly Rhagoletis pomonella (which has formed a distinct host race on introduced apple trees in North America), and several plant species that have diverged on different soil types within the same geographic area. The relative contribution of allopatric and sympatric speciation to overall biodiversity remains actively debated.

Ring species provide particularly compelling evidence for the gradual accumulation of reproductive isolation during geographic divergence. A ring species is a situation in which a chain of populations encircles a geographic barrier, with adjacent populations in the chain interbreeding freely, but the two end populations — which overlap geographically at one point — behaving as distinct species. The Larus gulls that encircle the Arctic and the Ensatina salamanders that encircle the Central Valley of California are classic examples. Ring species are important because they make the speciation process visible in contemporary populations: we can observe, in the living members of the ring, a continuous gradient from full interbreeding through partial reproductive isolation to complete reproductive isolation, illustrating that species boundaries are not categorical barriers imposed from outside but continuous processes that accumulate over time.

Polyploidy — the possession of more than two complete sets of chromosomes — is a major speciation mechanism in plants and is responsible for a substantial fraction of plant speciation events. Polyploidy can produce instant reproductive isolation: a tetraploid plant (with four chromosome sets) cannot produce viable diploid offspring when it mates with a normal diploid parent, because the triploid offspring are typically sterile. Allopolyploidy, in which a polyploid is derived from a hybrid between two different species, has been especially important: it combines the genomes of two parental species into a single organism, creating a new species that is reproductively isolated from both parents while potentially possessing hybrid vigor from the combination of divergent gene pools. Many important crop plants, including wheat, cotton, canola, and strawberry, are allopolyploids, and their origins can be traced to ancient hybridization events.

Reinforcement is a process by which natural selection can strengthen prezygotic reproductive barriers when two partially diverged populations come back into secondary contact and produce hybrids of reduced fitness. If hybrids are less fit than the parental genotypes, selection favors alleles that cause individuals to avoid mating with members of the other population, because individuals that successfully discriminate against heterospecific mates leave more fit offspring. Reinforcement therefore provides a mechanism by which the completion of speciation can be accelerated in zones of secondary contact. Evidence for reinforcement has been found in Drosophila fruit flies, in Ficedula flycatchers, and in plants, and it represents one of the best-documented cases of natural selection driving the evolution of a specific reproductive isolation mechanism.

The genomics revolution has transformed the study of speciation by making it possible to examine the fine-scale distribution of genetic divergence across the entire genome in pairs of closely related species or diverging populations. These "speciation genomics" studies have revealed that divergence is typically not uniform across the genome but is concentrated in specific regions — "islands of divergence" — that are associated with genes involved in adaptation to local environments or in reproductive isolation itself. The genome of a pair of diverging populations contains a rich record of the evolutionary forces that have acted on each locus, and the challenge of disentangling selection, drift, gene flow, and chromosomal rearrangements to reconstruct the history of a speciation event is one of the most active areas of modern evolutionary biology.
