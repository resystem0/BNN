Evolutionary developmental biology, commonly called evo-devo, emerged as a distinct discipline in the late 1980s and early 1990s with the discovery that the genes controlling the development of body plans are deeply conserved across the animal kingdom. Where the modern evolutionary synthesis had treated developmental biology and evolutionary biology as largely separate disciplines, evo-devo revealed that understanding how organisms develop is essential for understanding how they evolve. The tools of molecular developmental biology provided evolutionary biologists with a new window onto the mechanisms by which morphological novelty is generated and constrained, fundamentally changing our understanding of the relationship between genotype, phenotype, and evolutionary change.

The most striking discovery of evo-devo was the conservation of homeotic, or Hox, genes across the entire animal kingdom. Hox genes are a class of transcription factors that specify positional identity along the anterior-posterior axis of animal bodies. In every animal examined, from worms to flies to mice to humans, a cluster of Hox genes is expressed in spatially restricted domains along the body axis, and their order in the cluster corresponds to the order of their expression domains along the body — a property called colinearity. The genes are so conserved that the mouse Hox gene can substitute for the homologous Drosophila gene in an experimental context, even though the two lineages diverged over 600 million years ago. This extraordinary conservation implies that the basic developmental architecture of the animal body plan was established early in animal evolution and has been maintained largely intact ever since.

The discovery of Hox gene conservation did not diminish the importance of evolutionary change; rather, it focused attention on how changes in the regulation of conserved genes could produce dramatic differences in body plan without requiring the invention of new genes. The key concept is that morphological evolution frequently involves changes not in the protein-coding sequences of developmental genes but in the cis-regulatory elements — enhancers and silencers — that control when, where, and at what level those genes are expressed. Because the same transcription factor is often deployed in many developmental contexts, a change in the protein-coding sequence would typically affect all of those contexts simultaneously, most likely in a harmful way. A change in a specific enhancer, however, can alter expression in one tissue or developmental stage while leaving other deployments of the same gene unaffected. Cis-regulatory evolution is therefore a much safer path to morphological change than protein-coding evolution.

Sean Carroll and colleagues used evo-devo tools to analyze the evolution of butterfly wing patterns, one of the most spectacular examples of rapid morphological diversification in nature. The eyespot patterns on butterfly wings are controlled by a developmental organizing center that is associated with the expression of certain transcription factors, and the size, position, and color of the eyespots can all be modified by changes in the cis-regulatory elements controlling these genes. Different butterfly species, and different wing positions within the same species, produce differently sized eyespots because of differences in the boundaries of the organizing center's activity. The same molecular toolkit produces the whole rainbow of butterfly wing patterns by varying the spatial parameters of the developmental system, illustrating how "descent with modification" operates at the level of gene regulatory networks.

Developmental constraints are the flip side of developmental conservation: if the same developmental machinery is used to build many different structures, and if changes in that machinery typically affect multiple structures simultaneously, then evolution may be channeled along specific directions determined by the structure of the developmental system rather than by natural selection alone. Constraint means that not all possible phenotypes are equally accessible by mutation from any given starting point; the developmental system has a bias that makes some evolutionary trajectories more likely than others. This recognition has important implications for the interpretation of macroevolutionary patterns: the recurrent evolution of similar morphologies in independent lineages — convergent evolution — may reflect not independent optimization to the same selective demands but shared developmental biases that make certain solutions more accessible.

The evolution of body plan novelties — the origin of new phyla and classes — remains one of the deepest puzzles of evolutionary biology, and evo-devo has provided important new perspectives. The Cambrian explosion, which occurred roughly 540 million years ago, saw the rapid appearance in the fossil record of most of the major animal body plans that exist today. The traditional view attributed this burst of diversification to ecological opportunity following the extinction of competing Ediacaran organisms. Evo-devo researchers have added another perspective: the evolution of new regulatory toolkits, including the Hox cluster and the complex cis-regulatory networks that control body plan specification, may have opened up large new regions of morphological space that were previously inaccessible. Once these regulatory innovations appeared, the rapid exploration of a vast landscape of possible body plans became possible, accounting for the apparently explosive nature of the Cambrian diversification.

Phenotypic plasticity — the ability of a single genotype to produce different phenotypes in different environments — is increasingly recognized as an important factor in evolution, and evo-devo has provided mechanistic insights into how plasticity works and how it evolves. In many organisms, the same genotype can produce dramatically different morphologies depending on environmental conditions: queen versus worker castes in social insects are determined by larval nutrition, not by genetic differences; the shell shape of many mollusks depends on the presence of predators; human gene expression patterns vary systematically with early life experiences. West-Eberhard argued that phenotypic plasticity can facilitate evolution by allowing organisms to respond adaptively to new environments with their existing developmental systems, potentially leading to the genetic fixation of initially plastic phenotypic responses — a process called genetic accommodation.

The integration of evo-devo with systems biology and genomics has opened new possibilities for understanding the evolution of gene regulatory networks at the genome-wide level. The comparison of gene expression programs across species using transcriptomics and single-cell sequencing is revealing how developmental programs have been modified in evolution, identifying which genes have changed expression in which lineages and connecting these changes to morphological differences. The emerging picture is that developmental evolution involves both the tinkering of existing regulatory relationships — small adjustments to the timing, level, or spatial domain of gene expression — and the occasional rewiring of regulatory network architecture through the gain or loss of transcription factor binding sites. The rich gene expression datasets now available across the animal kingdom are providing an unprecedented view into the molecular mechanisms of morphological evolution.
