For much of the twentieth century, the dominant narrative of molecular evolution assumed that natural selection was the primary determinant of genetic change. Every nucleotide difference between species, the thinking went, had been fixed by selection because it conferred some adaptive advantage. This adaptationist program was challenged decisively in 1968 when Japanese geneticist Motoo Kimura proposed the neutral theory of molecular evolution. Kimura's theory argued that the vast majority of evolutionary change at the molecular level is not driven by natural selection at all but by the random fixation of mutations that are selectively neutral — neither beneficial nor detrimental to the organisms that carry them. The neutral theory transformed molecular evolutionary biology and is now recognized as a foundational pillar of the field, providing both a null hypothesis for detecting natural selection and a quantitative framework for molecular population genetics.

Genetic drift is the underlying mechanism of neutral evolution. In any finite population, allele frequencies change from generation to generation purely as a result of random sampling during reproduction. Even if two alleles have identical effects on fitness, their frequencies will fluctuate randomly over time because the individuals who happen to survive and reproduce in any given generation are a random sample of the population. In a small population, this sampling effect is large, and allele frequencies can change dramatically in just a few generations by chance alone. In a large population, the sampling effect is smaller relative to the population size, and drift is weaker. Over sufficient time, drift inevitably leads to fixation: one allele wins and all others are lost, purely by chance, with no connection to fitness. This stochastic process operates continuously in all real populations and is the dominant force of evolution at the molecular level according to the neutral theory.

The mathematics of genetic drift were worked out by Sewall Wright in the 1930s, decades before Kimura, using the diffusion equation approximation to describe the probability distribution of allele frequencies in finite populations over time. Wright showed that the effective population size — a parameter that captures the rate at which genetic diversity is lost to drift — is typically much smaller than the census population size because of factors such as unequal sex ratios, population size fluctuations, and variance in reproductive success among individuals. In many real populations, the effective size is an order of magnitude or more smaller than the census size, meaning that drift is much more powerful than a naive count of individuals would suggest. This discrepancy has profound implications for the interpretation of genetic diversity in natural populations.

Kimura's neutral theory made specific and testable quantitative predictions about the pattern of molecular evolution. Most importantly, it predicted that the rate of neutral molecular evolution should be approximately constant per unit time, proportional to the mutation rate, because the rate at which neutral mutations are fixed by drift equals the rate at which they are generated by mutation. This prediction — the molecular clock — was confirmed by comparisons of protein sequences across species: the rate of amino acid substitution in proteins like cytochrome c, hemoglobin, and fibrinopeptides appeared remarkably constant when plotted against geological time estimates of species divergence. The molecular clock has since become an indispensable tool in molecular systematics, allowing researchers to date evolutionary divergences and infer historical biogeographic events from molecular data.

The neutral theory also predicted a specific relationship between standing genetic variation within species and divergence between species. If evolution is primarily neutral, then species with larger effective population sizes should harbor more genetic diversity at neutral sites, because diversity accumulates when drift is weak. Conversely, the rate of divergence between species should reflect the mutation rate, not the population size. These predictions have been broadly confirmed by genomic data across diverse organisms, though violations of the predictions — particularly the observation that diversity across species spans a much narrower range than population size, a pattern called Lewontin's paradox — have motivated ongoing refinements of the theory.

The nearly neutral theory, developed by Kimura's colleague Tomoko Ohta, extended the neutral theory to include slightly deleterious mutations — mutations with fitness effects so small that they behave neutrally in large populations but are effectively deleterious in small populations where selection is overwhelmed by drift. Ohta's theory predicts that the proportion of mutations fixed by drift versus selection depends on the product of effective population size and selection coefficient: when this product is much less than one, mutations behave neutrally; when it is much greater than one, selection dominates. The nearly neutral theory provides a more realistic account of the spectrum of mutational effects and makes additional predictions about patterns of molecular evolution that have been confirmed by genomic comparisons.

Distinguishing natural selection from genetic drift in empirical data is one of the central challenges of molecular population genetics. The standard approach compares the ratio of nonsynonymous to synonymous substitution rates (the dN/dS ratio) across protein-coding genes. Synonymous mutations do not change the amino acid sequence and are largely neutral, so they accumulate at a rate that reflects the mutation rate and drift. Nonsynonymous mutations do change the amino acid sequence and may be subject to selection. Genes under positive selection accumulate nonsynonymous mutations faster than synonymous ones (dN/dS greater than one), while genes under purifying selection maintain fewer nonsynonymous mutations than expected under neutrality (dN/dS less than one). Scans for selection across sequenced genomes have revealed numerous genomic regions with signatures of recent positive or purifying selection, providing direct evidence for the ongoing action of natural selection on the molecular level.

The neutral theory and its descendants have had profound methodological consequences for evolutionary biology beyond molecular evolution. The recognition that much genetic variation is selectively neutral or nearly neutral means that the distribution of genetic diversity within and between populations carries a rich historical signal that can be analyzed using the tools of coalescent theory. The coalescent framework, developed by John Kingman in the 1980s, describes the ancestry of a sample of gene copies drawn from a population, showing that they will all trace back to a single common ancestor (coalesce) at a rate determined by the effective population size. By analyzing patterns of molecular diversity, researchers can infer the history of population size changes, population splits, and migration events that shaped the evolutionary history of a lineage — a field now called statistical phylogeography that has transformed our understanding of human prehistory and the history of life more broadly.
