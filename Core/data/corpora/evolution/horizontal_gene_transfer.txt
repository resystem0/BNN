The tree of life has long served as the central metaphor for evolution: a branching tree whose trunk represents the common ancestor of all life, whose branches represent the diversification of lineages over billions of years, and whose leaves represent the species alive today. This tree metaphor captures something real and important about the history of life — the fact that life had a single origin and that species inherit their characteristics from a continuous lineage of ancestors. But the discovery of horizontal gene transfer, the movement of genetic material between organisms through routes other than vertical inheritance from parent to offspring, has revealed that the tree of life is not purely a tree but is more like a tangled bush, at least at the prokaryotic level, where horizontal gene transfer is so frequent that the relationships between bacterial and archaeal lineages are more properly described by a network than a bifurcating tree.

Horizontal gene transfer, also called lateral gene transfer, occurs through three main mechanisms in bacteria. Transformation involves the uptake of free DNA from the environment by competent bacterial cells; many bacteria can actively take up DNA fragments released by the breakdown of dead cells in their environment, and some species have evolved elaborate molecular machinery specifically for this purpose. Transduction involves the transfer of bacterial genes by bacteriophages, viruses that infect bacteria; when a phage integrates into the bacterial genome, replicates along with it, and later excises to produce new phage particles, it sometimes packages bacterial DNA along with or instead of phage DNA, carrying those genes into the next infected cell. Conjugation involves the direct transfer of DNA from one bacterial cell to another through a physical connection called a pilus; conjugative plasmids and conjugative transposons can spread themselves and any genes they carry through a bacterial population in this way.

The evolutionary significance of horizontal gene transfer in bacteria is enormous. Antibiotic resistance genes provide the most medically consequential example: resistance to multiple classes of antibiotics can be encoded on mobile genetic elements — plasmids and transposons — that spread by conjugation among bacterial cells, crossing not just species boundaries but even the boundaries between major bacterial phyla. A clinically naive patient can harbor bacteria that become resistant to multiple antibiotics within hours, not because those bacteria evolved resistance through mutation and selection but because they acquired ready-made resistance genes from other bacteria in the environment. The epidemic spread of antibiotic resistance in hospitals and communities is driven largely by horizontal gene transfer, making it one of the most important public health consequences of evolution in real time.

The frequency of horizontal gene transfer among prokaryotes has complicated the reconstruction of microbial evolutionary history. Phylogenetic analysis of different genes from the same bacterial genome often produces conflicting trees — different genes suggest different relationships among the same set of organisms — because each gene has its own evolutionary history, some reflecting vertical inheritance from ancestral lineages and others reflecting horizontal acquisition from unrelated donors. This gene tree discordance makes it difficult to reconstruct the "species tree" of bacteria and archaea, and it has led to proposals that the concept of a species, defined by shared ancestry and reproductive isolation, does not apply to bacteria in the same way it does to sexually reproducing eukaryotes. The net of life, the web of life, or the tangled tree are alternative metaphors that better capture the reticulate evolutionary history of prokaryotes.

Endosymbiosis is a special form of horizontal gene transfer with profound evolutionary consequences. When one organism takes up permanent residence inside the cells of another, the endosymbiont's genome typically undergoes massive reduction as genes that are redundant with the host's genome are lost, and other endosymbiont genes migrate to the host's nuclear genome. The mitochondria of eukaryotic cells descended from an alphaproteobacterial endosymbiont, and the chloroplasts of plants and algae descended from a cyanobacterial endosymbiont. The origin of the eukaryotic cell, one of the most consequential events in the history of life, involved the horizontal transfer of thousands of genes from the bacterial endosymbiont to the archaeal host's genome, fundamentally altering the composition, organization, and capabilities of the recipient genome. Lynn Margulis' advocacy for the endosymbiotic theory of organelle origins, initially dismissed, is now one of the best-supported ideas in evolutionary biology.

The horizontal transfer of genes from organelles to the nucleus has continued throughout eukaryotic evolution. Many plant genomes contain thousands of mitochondria-derived and chloroplast-derived gene copies that have been transferred to the nucleus over evolutionary time, a process called endosymbiotic gene transfer. These transfers have been directly observed in experimental systems, occur at measurable rates in plants, and have left a rich record in genome sequences of the ongoing movement of genetic material from cytoplasmic organelles to the nuclear genome. The functional consequence of these transfers is that many of the proteins encoded by organellar-derived nuclear genes are synthesized in the cytoplasm and then imported back into the organelle, having evolved elaborate targeting sequences for this purpose.

Horizontal gene transfer also occurs between eukaryotes, though at a lower frequency than in prokaryotes. Many examples involve transfer from bacteria or mitochondria to nuclear genomes, as described above. But examples of eukaryote-to-eukaryote transfer are also known: bdelloid rotifers, microscopic aquatic animals, have acquired hundreds of genes from bacteria, fungi, plants, and algae through horizontal transfer, apparently as a consequence of their unusual biology — they can survive desiccation and repair their fragmented DNA upon rehydration, creating opportunities for the integration of foreign DNA fragments. Horizontal gene transfer has also been documented in aphids, which have acquired genes for carotenoid synthesis from fungi, in sea slugs that have acquired genes from the algae they eat, and in the nuclear genomes of many eukaryotes as integrated retroviral sequences.

The discovery of the extent and evolutionary significance of horizontal gene transfer has forced a reconsideration of the concept of the Last Universal Common Ancestor, or LUCA. The LUCA was presumably a population of cells that gave rise to all subsequent life, and it must have possessed the fundamental features shared by all living organisms: DNA-based heredity, the genetic code, ribosomes for protein synthesis, and a lipid bilayer membrane. However, the genome of the LUCA was not a fixed entity inherited by a single lineage; it was a pool of genes shared among a community of interacting cells that exchanged genetic material horizontally. This means that the origin of life cannot be understood as the emergence of a single lineage but as the emergence of a community of interacting organisms that collectively maintained the chemical innovations necessary for life. The tree of life is real and important for the post-LUCA history of life, but the root of that tree was a community rather than a single organism.
