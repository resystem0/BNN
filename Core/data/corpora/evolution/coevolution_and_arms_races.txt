Coevolution is the process by which two or more species reciprocally influence each other's evolution, with each species acting as a selective force on the other in an ongoing dynamic that can drive continuous evolutionary change in both lineages simultaneously. The concept captures the deep ecological interconnectedness of species and the recognition that the evolutionary environment of any organism consists not just of physical factors like climate and resource availability but also of the other species with which it interacts — predators, prey, parasites, hosts, pollinators, competitors, and mutualists. When evolutionary change in one species generates selective pressure that drives evolutionary change in another, which in turn generates new selective pressure on the first, the result is coevolution, and the dynamics can be strikingly similar to a technological arms race.

The predator-prey arms race is the archetype of antagonistic coevolution. Faster prey escape predators more effectively, generating selection for faster predators; faster predators catch prey more effectively, generating selection for faster prey; the result is an escalating cycle of evolutionary improvement in which both parties become better at their respective jobs over time without either gaining a permanent advantage. The fossil record provides evidence for this kind of escalation: the shells of marine mollusks have become progressively thicker and more elaborately armored over evolutionary time, in apparent response to the evolution of shell-crushing predators; the venoms of snakes have diversified in parallel with the resistance of prey species. However, demonstrating that arms races have occurred in deep evolutionary time is methodologically challenging, because the same pattern of increasing complexity could arise from other causes.

Host-parasite coevolution is one of the most intensively studied forms of antagonistic coevolution, because the rapid generation times and large population sizes of parasites make it possible to observe evolutionary dynamics in real time. The myxoma virus was introduced to Australia in 1950 to control the introduced rabbit population, and within a few years both virus and rabbit had evolved: the virus became less virulent (because killing the host quickly reduced its transmission success), and the rabbits became more resistant to the virus. This natural experiment illustrated several key features of host-parasite coevolution: the evolution of virulence is itself under selection, intermediate virulence is often optimal for the parasite, and host resistance and parasite counter-adaptation occur simultaneously on observable timescales. The molecular arms race between the immune systems of vertebrate hosts and their pathogens is one of the major drivers of genetic diversity in immune system genes, which show among the highest rates of adaptive molecular evolution in the genome.

The Red Queen hypothesis, proposed by Leigh Van Valen in 1973, captures the dynamics of coevolution in a striking metaphor drawn from Lewis Carroll. Van Valen observed that the probability of extinction of a lineage appears constant over evolutionary time, independent of the lineage's age — a pattern he called the Red Queen effect, after the Queen in Through the Looking Glass who must keep running just to stay in the same place. The hypothesis proposes that species must continuously evolve merely to maintain their fitness relative to the other species in their biotic environment, because those other species are also evolving. The Red Queen has been invoked most influentially in discussions of the evolution of sexual reproduction: the prevalence of sex in eukaryotes is puzzling because sexual reproduction is expensive — it requires finding mates, and half of offspring are male and do not directly reproduce. The Red Queen provides a potential explanation: sexual reproduction generates genetic diversity in offspring, and this diversity may provide the necessary variation to stay ahead of rapidly evolving parasites that are evolving to exploit the most common genotypes in the host population.

Mutualistic coevolution, in which both species benefit from the interaction, produces a very different dynamic from arms races but is equally important for understanding biodiversity. The coevolution between flowering plants and their pollinators is one of the most celebrated examples. Many flowering plants have evolved elaborate floral structures — specific colors, scents, shapes, and nectar rewards — that match the sensory and morphological characteristics of particular pollinator species, while the pollinators have evolved behaviors, body structures, and sensory preferences that match the particular flowers they visit most profitably. Darwin famously predicted, from the deep nectary spur of the Malagasy orchid Angraecum sesquipedale, that there must exist a pollinator with a proboscis long enough to reach the nectar — and the sphinx moth Xanthopan morganii praedicta, with a proboscis exceeding 30 centimeters, was subsequently discovered. This striking case of morphological co-matching between flower and pollinator illustrates how mutualistic coevolution can drive extreme specialization.

Geographic mosaics of coevolution, a theoretical framework developed by John Thompson, recognizes that coevolution is not a single uniform process but varies across the geographic range of an interaction, creating a mosaic of locally coevolved outcomes. In some localities, both species may be in active coevolutionary arms races; in others, one species may be locally absent or the interaction may be so asymmetric that it does not drive reciprocal evolution. The geographic mosaic theory predicts that coevolutionary traits will vary across the geographic range of a species interaction, and this prediction has been confirmed in several well-studied systems including the interaction between crossbills and lodgepole pine, in which the bill depth of crossbills and the thickness of pine cone scales vary in a correlated way across the geographic range of the interaction.

The study of coevolution has been revolutionized by the availability of genomic data, which allows researchers to identify the molecular targets of coevolutionary selection in both interacting species simultaneously. In host-parasite systems, the genomic signatures of rapid adaptive evolution on both sides of the interaction can be detected using population genomic methods, revealing which genes are involved in the ongoing molecular arms race. In mutualistic systems, comparative genomics can identify the molecular changes that underlie the evolution of complementary biochemical and morphological traits. The integration of ecological, phylogenetic, and genomic approaches is producing an increasingly detailed picture of how coevolution shapes the molecular, morphological, and ecological diversity of the living world.
