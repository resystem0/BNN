Manifolds are the central objects of study in much of modern geometry and topology. An n-dimensional topological manifold is a topological space that locally looks like n-dimensional Euclidean space: every point has a neighborhood homeomorphic to an open subset of R^n. The local Euclidean property formalizes the intuition that a smooth surface, like the surface of the Earth, is flat near any given point even though it curves globally. Manifolds provide the natural setting for generalizing calculus to curved spaces and for describing the geometry of physical spacetime in general relativity.

The dimension of a manifold is a well-defined topological invariant: an m-manifold cannot be homeomorphic to an n-manifold if m and n are different, a fact that follows from the invariance of domain theorem and ultimately from the non-trivial result that R^m and R^n are not homeomorphic for m not equal to n. In low dimensions, manifolds have been completely classified and extensively studied. Zero-manifolds are discrete sets of points. One-manifolds are, up to homeomorphism, either a circle or a line — closed or open. The classification of two-dimensional manifolds, or surfaces, is a complete and elegant theorem.

The classification of compact surfaces without boundary, proved in the early twentieth century, states that every such surface is homeomorphic to exactly one of the following: a sphere (genus 0, orientable), a connected sum of g tori for some g at least 1 (orientable surfaces of genus g), or a connected sum of k real projective planes for some k at least 1 (non-orientable surfaces). The genus of an orientable surface counts the number of handles: a torus has genus 1, a double torus has genus 2, and so on. The connected sum of two surfaces is formed by removing a small disk from each and gluing along the boundary circles. This theorem means that topologically, every orientable compact surface without boundary is a sphere with some number of handles attached, and this number determines it completely.

Orientability is a key property that distinguishes surfaces in the classification. A surface is orientable if it is possible to assign a consistent notion of clockwise and counterclockwise to each local patch, in a way that agrees across all patches. The sphere and torus are orientable. The Möbius band, formed by giving a strip of paper a half-twist before joining the ends, is the simplest non-orientable surface with boundary. A traveler walking along the centerline of a Möbius band returns to the starting point with left and right reversed, demonstrating the absence of a consistent orientation. The real projective plane and the Klein bottle are the simplest compact non-orientable surfaces without boundary; neither can be embedded in three-dimensional space without self-intersection, though they can be immersed.

The real projective plane RP^2 is the quotient space of the sphere obtained by identifying antipodal points: every pair (x, -x) on the sphere is collapsed to a single point. The resulting space cannot be visualized faithfully in three-dimensional space, but its homology gives H0 = Z, H1 = Z/2Z, H2 = 0, reflecting its non-orientability. The Klein bottle is the connected sum of two projective planes and similarly defies embedding in three dimensions, though it can be described by a polygon with specific edge identifications: a square with opposite edges identified, one pair in the same direction and one pair in opposite directions.

Moving to three-manifolds, the classification becomes dramatically more complex and was one of the great open problems of mathematics for a century. The Poincaré conjecture, posed in 1904, asked whether every simply connected compact three-manifold without boundary is homeomorphic to the three-sphere. The analogous statement in dimensions 5 and above was proved by Smale in 1961, and in dimension 4 by Freedman in 1982. The three-dimensional case resisted all attempts until Perelman's proof in 2002-2003 using Hamilton's Ricci flow approach. Perelman showed that any simply connected compact three-manifold can be given a metric of constant positive curvature and is therefore the three-sphere.

Differentiable manifolds, or smooth manifolds, are manifolds equipped with an additional structure that allows calculus to be done. The smooth structure is specified by an atlas of coordinate charts related by smooth transition maps. A smooth manifold supports differential forms, vector fields, and the operations of exterior differentiation and integration, generalizing classical multivariable calculus to curved spaces. Stokes' theorem in its general form on manifolds unifies the divergence theorem, Green's theorem, and the fundamental theorem of calculus into a single statement: the integral of the exterior derivative of a form over a manifold equals the integral of the form over the boundary manifold. This sweeping generalization connects analysis, topology, and geometry in a profound way.

Riemannian manifolds carry a metric tensor, a smoothly varying inner product on tangent vectors, which allows the definition of lengths, angles, distances, areas, and volumes. The curvature of a Riemannian manifold, measured by the Riemann curvature tensor, captures how the manifold deviates locally from flat Euclidean space. Sectional curvature, Ricci curvature, and scalar curvature are progressively coarser summaries of the curvature tensor. The Gauss-Bonnet theorem for compact surfaces states that the integral of the Gaussian curvature over the surface equals 2 pi times the Euler characteristic, a remarkable identity that connects differential geometry with topology and shows that the total curvature of a compact surface is a topological invariant independent of the specific metric imposed.
