Homotopy theory investigates the continuous deformations of maps and spaces, providing algebraic invariants that capture essential topological information about the global structure of spaces in ways that simpler properties like connectedness and compactness cannot. The basic notion is a homotopy between two continuous maps: a homotopy from f to g is a continuous map H from the product space X cross [0,1] to Y such that H(x,0) = f(x) and H(x,1) = g(x) for all x. Intuitively, H provides a continuous one-parameter family of maps that deforms f into g. Two maps that are connected by a homotopy are called homotopic, and homotopy is an equivalence relation on the set of all continuous maps between two spaces.

The notion of homotopy equivalence between spaces is distinct from homeomorphism and is a coarser equivalence. Two spaces X and Y are homotopy equivalent if there exist continuous maps f from X to Y and g from Y to X such that the compositions g composed with f and f composed with g are each homotopic to the respective identity maps. Homotopy equivalence captures the idea of having the same "shape" without requiring a perfect bijective correspondence. A solid disk is homotopy equivalent to a point, since the disk can be continuously contracted to any of its points; in fact any contractible space is homotopy equivalent to a point. A circle is not homotopy equivalent to a point, and this distinction is made precise by the fundamental group.

The fundamental group of a topological space X with a chosen basepoint x0, written pi1(X, x0), is the set of equivalence classes of loops based at x0, where two loops are equivalent if one can be continuously deformed into the other while keeping the basepoint fixed. A loop is a continuous map from the unit interval [0,1] to X with f(0) = f(1) = x0. The group operation is concatenation: to multiply loops f and g, traverse f at double speed on [0, 1/2] and then g at double speed on [1/2, 1]. The identity element is the constant loop that stays at x0 throughout, and the inverse of a loop is the same loop traversed in the opposite direction. The concatenation operation is associative up to homotopy, and this defines a genuine group structure on the set of homotopy classes.

The fundamental group measures the presence and type of one-dimensional holes in a space. A simply connected space is one in which every loop can be continuously contracted to a point, meaning the fundamental group is trivial, containing only the identity element. The plane and the two-sphere are simply connected. The circle is not: a loop that winds around the circle once cannot be contracted to a point without leaving the circle. The fundamental group of the circle is the integers, with the integer n corresponding to the homotopy class of loops that wind around the circle n times in the positive direction and minus n times in the negative direction. The group operation corresponds to addition of winding numbers.

For the torus, the product of two circles, the fundamental group is the free abelian group on two generators, isomorphic to Z cross Z. One generator corresponds to loops that wind around the torus in one direction, and the other to loops that wind in the perpendicular direction. Because these generators commute — winding once in the first direction and then once in the second is homotopic to winding once in the second direction and then once in the first — the group is abelian. For surfaces of higher genus, the fundamental group is non-abelian and has the structure of a group presented by generators and relations reflecting the topology of the surface.

The fundamental group is a homotopy invariant: if two spaces are homotopy equivalent, their fundamental groups at corresponding basepoints are isomorphic. This makes the fundamental group a practical tool for distinguishing spaces: if two spaces have non-isomorphic fundamental groups, they cannot be homeomorphic, or even homotopy equivalent. The fundamental group of the complement of a knot in three-dimensional space, called the knot group, is a powerful invariant for distinguishing different knots. Two knots are equivalent if and only if their complements are homeomorphic, and the fundamental group of the complement provides algebraic information about the knot's topology.

Van Kampen's theorem provides a computational tool for calculating the fundamental group of a space assembled from simpler pieces. If a space X is the union of two path-connected open sets A and B whose intersection A intersect B is also path connected, then the fundamental group of X is the free product of the fundamental groups of A and B, amalgamated over the fundamental group of the intersection. This allows the fundamental group of complex spaces to be computed by decomposing them into simpler pieces whose fundamental groups are known. Using van Kampen's theorem, one can show that the fundamental group of a wedge sum of circles is the free group on as many generators as there are circles, and that the fundamental group of a surface of genus g is a group with 2g generators subject to one specific relation.

Higher homotopy groups pi_n(X, x0) generalize the fundamental group by replacing loops with maps from the n-dimensional sphere. The second homotopy group pi_2 consists of homotopy classes of maps from the two-sphere into X, with basepoints matching. Unlike pi_1, higher homotopy groups are always abelian. Computing homotopy groups of spheres is a central and notoriously difficult problem in algebraic topology. The n-th homotopy group of the n-sphere is Z, generated by the identity map. But the third homotopy group of the two-sphere is also Z, generated by the Hopf fibration, a remarkable map that wraps S3 around S2 in a non-trivial way. The homotopy groups of spheres exhibit complex patterns that are still not fully understood in general, and their computation has driven the development of much of algebraic topology throughout the twentieth century.
