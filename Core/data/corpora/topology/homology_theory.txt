Homology theory is a systematic method for associating sequences of abelian groups to topological spaces, where these groups capture information about the number and type of holes of various dimensions present in the space. Unlike the fundamental group, which requires a choice of basepoint and is generally non-abelian, homology groups are canonically defined, abelian, and often easier to compute. They are among the most powerful and widely applicable invariants in algebraic topology, connecting to geometry, algebra, and even physics in deep ways.

The geometric intuition is straightforward. A zero-dimensional hole is a disconnection: two points that cannot be joined by a path lie on opposite sides of a zero-dimensional hole. A one-dimensional hole is a loop that cannot be filled in by a two-dimensional disk: the circle has a one-dimensional hole because you can draw a loop that goes all the way around the circle but there is no two-dimensional region inside the circle in the circle itself to fill it. A two-dimensional hole is a hollow void: the surface of a sphere has a two-dimensional hole because the sphere encloses a three-dimensional region that is not part of the surface. Homology groups Hn count these holes of each dimension n in a precise algebraic sense.

Simplicial homology is defined for spaces that can be triangulated, meaning decomposed into simplices: points, line segments, triangles, tetrahedra, and their higher-dimensional analogues. A simplicial complex is a finite collection of simplices glued together along faces in a compatible way. Given a simplicial complex K, the n-th chain group Cn(K) is the free abelian group generated by the oriented n-simplices of K. An orientation is a choice of ordering of the vertices of each simplex, up to even permutations. The boundary operator d_n maps each n-simplex to the alternating sum of its (n-1)-dimensional faces, where the sign alternates according to which face is opposite to which vertex.

The fundamental algebraic property of the boundary operator is that applying it twice gives zero: d_{n-1} composed with d_n equals zero. This means the image of d_n, the subgroup of (n-1)-chains that are boundaries of n-chains, is contained in the kernel of d_{n-1}, the subgroup of (n-1)-chains whose own boundary is zero, called cycles. Cycles that are boundaries are topologically trivial in the sense that they bound a filled region. The n-th homology group Hn is defined as the quotient of the group of n-cycles by the subgroup of n-boundaries: Hn = ker(d_n) / image(d_{n+1}). This quotient group measures cycles that are not boundaries, which correspond precisely to the n-dimensional holes in the space.

The Betti numbers b_n are the ranks of the homology groups, measuring the free part. The zeroth Betti number b_0 counts the number of connected components. The first Betti number b_1 counts independent one-dimensional holes or loops; for a torus, b_1 equals 2 because there are two independent classes of loops, one going around the tube and one going through the hole. The second Betti number b_2 counts enclosed voids; for the torus, b_2 equals 1 because the torus encloses a single three-dimensional void. The Euler characteristic, one of the oldest topological invariants, equals the alternating sum of the Betti numbers: chi = b_0 - b_1 + b_2 - b_3 plus higher terms. For any triangulation of a space, chi also equals the alternating sum of the numbers of simplices: V - E + F for a surface, where V is the number of vertices, E edges, and F faces.

Singular homology extends the definition to arbitrary topological spaces without requiring triangulability. A singular n-simplex is any continuous map from the standard n-simplex into the space, and the singular chain groups are the free abelian groups generated by all such maps. The boundary maps are defined analogously, and the resulting homology groups agree with simplicial homology when both are defined. Singular homology is the definition used in the general theoretical development because of its functoriality: any continuous map between spaces induces homomorphisms between the corresponding homology groups, in a way that respects composition of maps. This makes homology a functor from the category of topological spaces to the category of sequences of abelian groups.

The long exact sequence of a pair is a powerful tool in homological computations. If A is a subspace of X, the relative homology Hn(X, A) measures the holes of X relative to A, treating A as if it were a point. The inclusion of A into X and the map of X onto X modulo A induce a long exact sequence connecting the homology of A, X, and the relative homology Hn(X, A). The Mayer-Vietoris sequence provides a complementary tool: if X is the union of two open sets U and V, the Mayer-Vietoris sequence connects the homology of U, V, their intersection, and X in a long exact sequence, enabling homology to be computed by decomposition.

The homology of spheres is foundational and the prototype for many computations. The n-sphere has H0 = Z, Hn = Z, and all other homology groups trivial. The single Z in dimension n corresponds to the fundamental class of the sphere, the cycle represented by the sphere itself. The calculation confirms that spheres of different dimensions are not homeomorphic, something that seems obvious geometrically but requires homology to prove rigorously. The homology of compact orientable surfaces is known: for a surface of genus g, H0 = Z, H1 = Z to the 2g power, H2 = Z, reflecting the g independent handles of the surface. For non-orientable surfaces, torsion appears in H1 in the form of Z modulo 2 summands.
