Topology is the branch of mathematics concerned with the properties of geometric objects that are preserved under continuous deformations — stretching, bending, and twisting — without tearing or gluing. Where geometry studies rigid properties like lengths and angles, topology studies the more flexible properties of connectedness, continuity, and the global shape of spaces. The central objects of topology are topological spaces, and the natural notion of equivalence between them is the homeomorphism, a bijective continuous map with a continuous inverse. Two spaces are homeomorphic if one can be continuously deformed into the other; colloquially, topology is sometimes described as rubber-sheet geometry.

The formal definition of a topological space begins with a set X and a collection tau of subsets of X called open sets, subject to three axioms. The empty set and the entire set X must belong to tau. Any union of open sets, even an uncountable union, must be open. The intersection of any finite collection of open sets must be open. The pair (X, tau) is then called a topological space, and tau is called the topology on X. This level of abstraction is necessary to capture in a single framework the very different notions of openness that arise in different mathematical contexts: the open intervals of the real line, the open balls of a metric space, the cofinite topology where open sets are complements of finite sets, and the indiscrete topology where only the empty set and the whole space are open.

The most familiar topology is the standard topology on the real numbers, where open sets are arbitrary unions of open intervals. This topology is metrizable: the open sets are precisely those that can be characterized as sets where every point has an open ball of some radius contained entirely within the set. Metric spaces, in which a distance function satisfying non-negativity, symmetry, and the triangle inequality is defined, automatically carry a topology induced by declaring open balls to be the basic open sets. The metric topology on Euclidean space is the foundation of classical real analysis, but the topological framework generalizes to spaces where no natural metric exists and to phenomena that are purely topological in character.

A function between topological spaces is continuous if and only if the preimage of every open set in the target space is open in the domain. This reformulation of continuity in terms of open sets replaces the epsilon-delta definition familiar from analysis and works without any reference to distances or metrics. The preimage definition of continuity extends immediately to the most general topological spaces and is the natural definition for the subject. Equivalent characterizations are that the preimage of every closed set is closed, and that the function preserves limit points in the sense that it maps every convergent net or filter to a convergent one.

A homeomorphism is a continuous bijection from one topological space to another whose inverse is also continuous. Homeomorphic spaces are topologically identical: any topological property of one is shared by the other. The question of classifying topological spaces up to homeomorphism — determining when two apparently different spaces are actually the same from a topological perspective — is the central problem of topology. For surfaces, the classification is complete: every compact surface without boundary is homeomorphic to either a sphere, a connected sum of tori, or a connected sum of projective planes, and these are all distinct. For higher-dimensional manifolds, complete classification is much harder and in certain dimensions provably impossible.

Topological properties are those that remain invariant under homeomorphism. Connectedness is a fundamental topological property: a space is connected if it cannot be partitioned into two disjoint nonempty open sets. Intuitively, a connected space is in one piece. The interval [0,1] is connected; the set consisting of [0,1] together with [2,3] is not, because the two closed intervals can be separated by open sets. Path connectedness is a stronger condition: a space is path connected if any two points can be joined by a continuous path. Path connected implies connected but not conversely, as exotic examples show. Compact spaces are those in which every open cover has a finite subcover; compactness is the generalization of closed and bounded in Euclidean space and plays a crucial role in analysis and geometry.

The separation axioms form a hierarchy of increasingly strong conditions on how well a topological space separates its points by open sets. The T0 or Kolmogorov axiom requires that for any two distinct points, there exists an open set containing one but not the other. The T1 or Fréchet axiom adds that for any two distinct points, each has an open neighborhood not containing the other, which implies that every singleton set is closed. The T2 or Hausdorff axiom requires that any two distinct points have disjoint open neighborhoods; Hausdorff spaces are the most frequently encountered in practice and have the property that limits of sequences, when they exist, are unique. Stronger axioms such as regularity, normality, and complete regularity each add stronger separation conditions that are useful in proving embedding theorems and metrization theorems.

Basis and subbasis provide practical tools for specifying and working with topologies without having to describe every open set explicitly. A basis for a topology is a collection of open sets such that every open set is a union of basis elements. For the standard topology on the real line, the open intervals form a basis. The product topology on a Cartesian product of spaces is defined by taking the collection of sets that are products of open sets in each factor as a subbasis, meaning that finite intersections of these product sets generate the open sets. This definition makes the product topology the coarsest topology for which all projection maps are continuous, and it ensures that a function into a product space is continuous if and only if each of its component functions is continuous.
