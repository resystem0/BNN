Foundationalism and coherentism are the two dominant responses to one of epistemology's most persistent structural problems: the problem of the regress of justification. The problem arises from reflection on what it means for a belief to be justified. If one belief is justified by another, what justifies that other belief? If justification must always be provided by a further belief, we seem to face an infinite regress of justifications, none of which ever reach firm ground. If we allow the regress to stop arbitrarily, we seem to have beliefs that are believed without justification. If we allow the chain to loop back on itself, we have circular reasoning. Foundationalism and coherentism are the two most developed attempts to show that this trilemma — infinite regress, arbitrary stopping point, or circularity — admits of a solution.

Foundationalism resolves the regress by positing two tiers of beliefs. Basic beliefs, also called foundational or non-inferential beliefs, serve as the bedrock of the entire structure. They are justified not by inference from other beliefs but by something else — direct experience, self-evidence, or reliable causal contact with the facts they represent. Non-basic beliefs are justified by inferential relations to basic beliefs, either directly or through chains of inference. The foundational metaphor is architectural: just as a building requires a foundation that supports everything above it without itself being supported from below, the structure of knowledge requires basic beliefs that support all other beliefs without themselves depending on further beliefs.

The classical version of foundationalism, associated with Descartes, located basic beliefs in what cannot be doubted: clear and distinct perceptions of the contents of one's own mind. Descartes argued that while beliefs about the external world can be doubted, beliefs about one's own occurrent mental states — that one is currently having such-and-such an experience — are incorrigible, immune to doubt. The Cartesian foundationalist project was to build up secure knowledge of the external world from this incorrigible foundation of self-knowledge. The difficulties with this project — particularly the problem of how inferential bridges can be built from subjective experience to objective facts — led later foundationalists to propose less demanding requirements for basic beliefs.

Modest foundationalism holds that basic beliefs need not be infallible or incorrigible but merely epistemically privileged — better justified than non-basic beliefs, and justified independently of inference. Beliefs formed through perception, memory, or testimony might qualify as basic on this weaker standard. They are fallible — they can be wrong — but they carry a prima facie justification that other beliefs lack, and they can serve as the starting points of epistemic justification without themselves depending on further inferential support. This modest version of foundationalism is more compatible with the fallibilism that most contemporary epistemologists accept, and it avoids the implausible claims of incorrigibility that plagued Cartesian versions.

Coherentism rejects the foundationalist picture entirely and resolves the regress by denying that justification has a linear structure at all. On the coherentist view, justification is a matter of how beliefs fit together in a mutually supporting web. No belief is basic; every belief is justified by its coherence with the overall system of beliefs. The web metaphor, famously developed by Otto Neurath, replaces the building metaphor of foundationalism. Neurath compared our knowledge to a ship that must be rebuilt at sea — we cannot take it to dry dock and rebuild from the keel up, but must repair it plank by plank while remaining afloat. No single plank is foundational; each is supported by the others.

Coherence itself requires analysis. At minimum, a coherent system is one that is free of contradictions. But mere consistency is too weak a requirement, since any single belief taken alone is consistent with itself, and so any belief would be justified on this standard. Richer accounts of coherence invoke explanatory relations: a system is more coherent to the degree that its beliefs explain and support each other, that adding a new belief increases the explanatory integration of the whole. Probabilistic accounts of coherence try to capture the idea that coherent beliefs mutually raise each other's probability.

A major objection to coherentism is the isolation objection: a coherent system of beliefs might be completely disconnected from reality. A fiction-writer could construct a richly coherent set of narratives about a non-existent world, but the coherence of the narrative does not make its claims true or justified. The objection is that coherence is an internal property of belief systems and cannot by itself generate epistemic contact with an external world. Coherentists respond that this objection misunderstands the role of perceptual and observational beliefs, which serve as inputs that constrain the coherent system from the outside — but critics argue that this concession amounts to a covert return to foundationalism.

Foundationalism faces its own central objection: how do basic beliefs get their justification? If basic beliefs are justified by perception or experience, what is the epistemic relationship between the experience and the belief? One influential answer, developed by Wilfrid Sellars in his critique of the given, is that this relationship cannot be purely causal: mere causal contact with the world does not generate justification. Justification requires that the basic belief be placed in logical space, connected to other beliefs through rational relations. But if this is right, then no belief is truly basic — every belief depends on a background of other beliefs to be justified, which seems to favor coherentism. The dilemma between the myth of the given on one side and the isolation objection on the other defines the central tension in the debate between foundationalism and coherentism.
