Naturalized epistemology is the philosophical program of bringing epistemological inquiry into close alliance with — or even reducing it to — the natural sciences, particularly cognitive science, psychology, and evolutionary biology. The movement was launched and named by W.V.O. Quine in his 1969 essay "Epistemology Naturalized," one of the most discussed and debated papers in twentieth-century philosophy. Quine argued that traditional epistemology, with its aspiration to provide a foundational justification for science from secure, prior principles, had failed, and that the appropriate response was to abandon the foundationalist project and replace it with an empirical inquiry into how human beings actually acquire knowledge — how sensory inputs are transformed, through cognitive processes shaped by evolution and learning, into a picture of the world.

Quine's starting point was the failure of the two main programs that had dominated epistemology in the first half of the twentieth century. Cartesian foundationalism had tried to reconstruct knowledge on a secure foundation of indubitable first principles, but Descartes' own project had been repeatedly challenged and no secure foundation had been established. The logical empiricist program, particularly in the hands of Rudolf Carnap, had tried to provide a rational reconstruction of science — to show how scientific knowledge could be built up from phenomenal experience through purely logical operations. Quine had already argued in "Two Dogmas of Empiricism" that this project was unachievable because the analytic-synthetic distinction on which it relied could not be drawn, and because the web of belief faces experience as a whole, not proposition by proposition.

If the normative project of justifying science from first principles is unachievable, Quine argued, epistemology should be abandoned as an autonomous philosophical discipline and absorbed into empirical science. Epistemological questions — how do we know what we know, what are the sources and limits of human knowledge, how is it possible to form a theory of the world from the limited sensory data available — should be treated as scientific questions about human cognition, answered by psychology and cognitive science. The question of how knowledge is possible is replaced by the question of how knowledge is actual — how human organisms, as a matter of psychological fact, develop their picture of the world on the basis of the sensory stimulation they receive.

The replacement versus revision question is central to debates about naturalized epistemology. Quine seemed to advocate for a replacement of traditional normative epistemology by descriptive cognitive science — by a purely empirical account of knowledge acquisition that abandons normative questions altogether. Critics immediately objected that this is not epistemology at all: it trades the normative question of how we ought to form beliefs for the purely descriptive question of how we do in fact form them. Any appeal to the results of cognitive science must itself rely on epistemic norms to evaluate the evidence — the scientists conducting the cognitive science must be justified in their methods and conclusions — and so the normative dimension cannot be simply eliminated without circularity.

Defenders of naturalized epistemology mostly accept this criticism and advocate for revision rather than replacement. A more modest naturalism holds that epistemological inquiry should be continuous with and informed by the natural sciences without being absorbed by them. The question of what constitutes good epistemic practice — what makes beliefs justified or what makes reasoning reliable — is informed by empirical findings about cognition, evolution, and social factors, but it remains an irreducibly normative question. The natural sciences do not provide by themselves the norms for evaluating epistemic practices; they provide information relevant to evaluating which cognitive strategies tend to achieve epistemic goals, but the choice and weighting of those goals remains a philosophical matter.

Evolutionary epistemology is a related program that draws on evolutionary biology to understand the origins and character of human cognitive faculties. One approach, associated with Konrad Lorenz and Karl Popper among others, treats our cognitive capacities — particularly our basic perceptual faculties and logical intuitions — as adaptations shaped by natural selection to track features of the environment. If our perceptual systems evolved because accurate perception was fitness-enhancing, then there is an evolutionary explanation of why they are generally reliable. But critics point out that natural selection is not a truth-tracking mechanism in the strict sense: it selects for reproductive fitness, not for epistemic accuracy. Faculties that systematically misrepresent the world might be fitness-enhancing if they reliably produce adaptive behavior, which means evolutionary success does not straightforwardly vindicate epistemic reliability.

The cognitive biases literature, emerging from experimental psychology and behavioral economics, presents both a challenge and an opportunity for naturalized epistemology. Research by Kahneman, Tversky, and many others has revealed systematic patterns of reasoning error in human cognition — availability bias, confirmation bias, anchoring, framing effects — that seem to show that human epistemic practice falls significantly short of rational norms. For the naturalist, these findings are relevant to epistemology because they describe the actual epistemic condition of human knowers. But they also raise the question of whether naturalized epistemology can accommodate genuine normativity: if our natural cognitive processes are systematically biased, then a purely descriptive account of how we form beliefs cannot also be a normative account of how we should.

The most sophisticated forms of naturalized epistemology therefore seek a reflective equilibrium between descriptive cognitive science and normative epistemological theory. We begin with intuitions about what constitutes good epistemic practice, test these intuitions against systematic cognitive and evolutionary findings, revise our intuitions in light of those findings, and revise our interpretation of the findings in light of revised intuitions. This process of mutual adjustment does not yield an Archimedean standpoint outside both science and epistemology, but it offers a principled way of integrating empirical and normative inquiry that is more adequate to the actual complexity of human knowledge than either purely a priori epistemology or purely empirical cognitive science.
