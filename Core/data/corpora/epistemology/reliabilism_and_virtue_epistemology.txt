Reliabilism is an externalist theory of epistemic justification that grounds the justification of beliefs not in the subject's internal perspective or the reasons accessible to reflection but in the objective reliability of the cognitive processes that produced the beliefs. The theory was developed most fully by Alvin Goldman in a series of papers and books beginning in the late 1970s. Its core thesis is simple and powerful: a belief is justified if and only if it is produced by a reliable belief-forming process, where reliability means a tendency to produce a high proportion of true beliefs. Perception, memory, deductive inference, and inductive reasoning are generally reliable processes; wishful thinking, self-deception, and astrology are not. Justification, on this account, is a natural property that depends on how the world actually is, not on what the believer can recognize or articulate from inside their epistemic situation.

The appeal of reliabilism lies partly in its naturalistic orientation. By tying justification to reliable processes, the account connects epistemology to the cognitive sciences: the study of which belief-forming processes are reliable is partly an empirical question, one that cognitive psychology, neuroscience, and evolutionary biology can inform. If our perceptual systems evolved under selection pressure to accurately track relevant features of the environment, they are reliable in the relevant sense, and the beliefs they produce are thereby justified. Reliabilism does not require that the subject know or be able to articulate why a process is reliable; it only requires that the process in fact be reliable. This externalism about justification is both its strength and the source of its most powerful objections.

The generality problem is the most frequently discussed objection to process reliabilism. When we ask whether a belief is justified by asking whether the process that produced it is reliable, we face the question of how to individuate the process in question. Any actual belief is produced by a process that can be described at many different levels of generality: visual perception in general, visual perception in good lighting, visual perception of red things in good lighting, visual perception of red things in this particular situation. Different levels of generality correspond to different reliability rates, and the theory as stated does not specify which level of description is the relevant one. Without a principled answer to this question, the theory cannot make determinate predictions about which beliefs are justified.

Virtue epistemology is a broader framework that grew partly out of reliabilism and partly out of the application of virtue ethics to epistemological questions. The central move is to shift focus from individual beliefs and belief-forming processes to the epistemic agent as a whole — to the intellectual character traits, dispositions, and faculties that characterize the good epistemic agent. Epistemic virtues include such traits as intellectual humility, open-mindedness, intellectual courage, thoroughness, and intellectual honesty. Vice includes intellectual arrogance, closed-mindedness, and epistemic cowardice. Knowledge, on virtue-theoretic accounts, is a kind of intellectual achievement that reflects well on the agent who attains it.

Ernest Sosa is the most prominent developer of virtue epistemology. Sosa distinguishes between two kinds of epistemic evaluation: animal knowledge and reflective knowledge. Animal knowledge is knowledge that results from the reliable exercise of cognitive faculties — the kind of knowledge that a thermometer has in the sense that it reliably tracks temperature, or that a perceiver has when they form accurate perceptual beliefs through functioning sense organs. Reflective knowledge adds a further condition: the subject must not only have a reliable belief but must be able to situate that reliability within a coherent self-understanding of their epistemic position. Reflective knowledge requires that the subject have a view about why their belief is reliable — that they have a kind of epistemic self-knowledge that goes beyond mere first-order reliability.

Linda Zagzebski develops virtue epistemology in a more robustly normative direction, arguing that epistemic virtues are more than just reliable faculties but are genuine character traits that involve a motivational component. An intellectually virtuous person is not merely reliable but cares about truth and is motivated by the desire to know. On Zagzebski's account, epistemic virtues are analogous to moral virtues: they are stable, deeply ingrained dispositions that characterize a person's way of engaging with the world, and they are admirable because of the good motivations they express rather than merely because of their reliability.

Both reliabilism and virtue epistemology have been tested against cases involving testimonial knowledge — knowledge that depends on what others tell us. When you come to believe something on the basis of someone else's testimony, what makes your resulting belief justified or a case of knowledge? A simple reliabilist account says that your belief is justified if your process of forming beliefs from testimony is generally reliable. But this seems to leave something out: it matters not just whether testimony generally leads to true beliefs but whether the particular testifier is trustworthy and whether you have reason to regard them as such. Virtue epistemologists often add that knowledge from testimony requires that you have exercised appropriate intellectual virtues — that you have been attentive, critical, and appropriately trusting or skeptical given the circumstances.

The debate between internalism and externalism in epistemology runs through discussions of reliabilism and virtue epistemology. Internalists argue that justification supervenes on the subject's internal states — on what is accessible to reflection. If two subjects are in identical internal states, they must have the same justification for their beliefs. Externalists deny this: what matters is not just the internal perspective but the objective facts about the cognitive processes or virtues involved. Reliabilism is paradigmatically externalist in this sense, and one motivation for virtue epistemology's focus on the whole epistemic agent is partly to capture some of the internalist's insight — that there is something normatively significant about the agent's perspective and character — while preserving the externalist's insight that objective reliability matters for epistemic assessment.
