Skepticism in epistemology is not merely doubt but a systematic philosophical position that challenges the possibility of knowledge or justified belief in some or all domains. The most radical form of global skepticism holds that we cannot know anything at all about the external world; more moderate local skepticisms challenge knowledge in specific domains such as other minds, the past, or the external physical world. What makes skepticism philosophically serious — rather than merely perverse — is that skeptical arguments are often formally valid, proceeding from premises that seem highly plausible to conclusions that threaten to undermine the entire enterprise of knowledge. Understanding why skepticism seems compelling and how it can be resisted is one of the central tasks of epistemology.

Descartes gave the most influential formulation of modern epistemological skepticism in his Meditations on First Philosophy, published in 1641. Descartes was not himself a skeptic but used skeptical arguments as a method to find beliefs that were so secure that no conceivable doubt could undermine them. He introduced two increasingly powerful skeptical scenarios. The first is the evil demon hypothesis: an omnipotent evil demon, intent on deceiving Descartes, causes all his perceptual experiences by manipulating his senses without any corresponding external reality. In this scenario, all of Descartes' beliefs about the external world would be false even though they feel just as they do in the actual world. The second, implicit in the demon scenario, is the dreaming argument: for all Descartes can tell from inside his experience, he might be dreaming, and dreams are known to be internally indistinguishable from waking experience.

The contemporary equivalent of the evil demon scenario is the brain-in-a-vat hypothesis, popularized by Hilary Putnam. Imagine that you are a disembodied brain kept alive in a laboratory, connected to a supercomputer that generates all your perceptual experiences exactly as they actually are. Since the brain-in-a-vat's experiences would be subjectively indistinguishable from your own, you cannot determine from inside your experience whether you are a normal person or a brain in a vat. The skeptic argues that since you cannot rule out this possibility, and since your beliefs about the external world would be radically false if you were a brain in a vat, you do not know those beliefs to be true.

The structure of the skeptical argument relies on the principle that knowledge requires the ability to rule out alternatives to what one believes. This closure principle states that if you know P, and you know that P entails Q, then you know Q. Combined with the observation that ordinary empirical knowledge entails the falsity of skeptical hypotheses — if I know that I have hands, then I know that I am not a handless brain in a vat — the closure principle implies that if I cannot rule out the skeptical scenario, I cannot know the ordinary claim either.

Contextualism is one of the most influential responses to skepticism. On contextualist accounts, developed by philosophers like David Lewis and Stewart Cohen, the word "know" is context-sensitive: what counts as knowledge depends on the epistemic standards operative in the conversational context. In ordinary practical contexts, where skeptical scenarios are not under consideration, our ordinary perceptual beliefs count as knowledge because they meet the standards relevant in those contexts. In philosophical contexts, where skeptical scenarios are being raised and the standards are correspondingly high, the same beliefs may not count as knowledge. Skepticism is therefore correct that we do not know things in the demanding philosophical sense, but it does not follow that we do not know things in the ordinary, practically relevant sense.

Epistemic contextualism preserves both the validity of the skeptic's arguments within philosophical contexts and the commonsense view that we know many things in ordinary life. But critics argue that it is epistemically unsatisfying: it seems to say that whether I know something depends on who happens to be talking about it, which conflicts with the intuition that knowledge is an objective, subject-centered relation that does not vary with conversational context.

G.E. Moore's famous response to skepticism was to simply assert the obviousness of ordinary knowledge claims and use them as premises against skeptical conclusions. Moore claimed to know with certainty that he had two hands, and since this knowledge is more certain than any philosophical premise that might undermine it, we should accept the hands and reject any argument that leads to their denial. This Moorean strategy inverts the skeptic's argument: instead of arguing from the undeniability of skeptical possibilities to the denial of ordinary knowledge, we argue from the certainty of ordinary knowledge to the denial that skeptical hypotheses are genuinely possible or threatening.

Wittgenstein developed a related response in On Certainty. He argued that certain propositions — such as that the earth has existed for more than five minutes — function as hinges rather than genuine claims. They are not themselves subject to epistemic justification or doubt; they are the background framework within which doubt and justification make sense. To doubt that I have hands requires a context in which such doubt could have practical significance, and in normal practical life no such context exists. The skeptic's doubt is not genuine doubt at all but a philosophical construct that mimics the surface grammar of doubt without its practical substance.

Nozick's sensitivity account attempts to explain what is wrong with skeptical knowledge claims by analyzing knowledge as tracking truth across possible worlds. On this account, S knows that P if S's belief that P is sensitive to the truth of P: in the nearest possible world in which P is false, S would not believe P. Ordinary perceptual beliefs are sensitive in this way — if I did not have hands, my perceptual systems would not present me with the appearance of having hands — but skeptical hypotheses are not ruled out because the brain-in-a-vat scenario is too distant from actuality to be relevant to the sensitivity relation in ordinary cases. The account blocks the closure principle that drives the skeptical argument, but the denial of closure has its own controversial implications.
