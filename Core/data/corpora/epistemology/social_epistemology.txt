Social epistemology is the branch of epistemology that investigates the epistemic significance of social interactions, institutions, and practices — the ways in which knowledge, belief, and justified credence are generated, transmitted, evaluated, and distorted through social processes. Traditional epistemology often proceeded by considering an idealized individual knower in isolation, asking what one person could know on the basis of their own observations and reasoning. Social epistemology challenges this individualism and insists that knowing is fundamentally a social activity: most of what we know, we know because of what others have told us, what institutions have certified, or what social practices have made accessible.

The epistemology of testimony is one of the central topics in social epistemology. Testimony is the transmission of knowledge or belief through assertion — when you tell me something and I come to believe it on the basis of your telling me. The scale of our dependence on testimony is enormous. The vast majority of our beliefs about history, science, geography, and current events are based on testimony from teachers, books, news sources, and various authorities. We take it on faith that the earth orbits the sun, that antibiotics kill bacteria, that Julius Caesar existed — not because we have personally verified these claims but because reliable sources have told us they are true.

Two broad positions define the debate about the epistemology of testimony. Reductionism, associated with David Hume and more recently with Elizabeth Fricker, holds that testimony does not constitute an independent source of justification. To be justified in accepting what someone tells you, you must have positive reasons for thinking that this particular person or kind of person is reliable. Your justification for testimony-based beliefs is reducible to your justification for thinking that the testifier is trustworthy, which in turn depends on your own observations and inductive reasoning about the reliability of testimony. Anti-reductionism, associated with C.A.J. Coady, holds that testimony is a basic source of epistemic justification, analogous to perception and memory, and that we are prima facie justified in accepting what we are told without positive evidence of the testifier's reliability.

The problem of peer disagreement is another central topic. When two people who seem equally well-informed and intellectually capable arrive at different conclusions about the same question, what should each of them believe? The conciliatory view, developed by philosophers like David Christensen and Adam Elga, holds that discovering a peer's disagreement is strong evidence that you may have made an error, and that you should give significant weight to your peer's view, moving your credence toward theirs. The steadfast view, defended by others like Jennifer Lackey and Richard Feldman in different versions, holds that you are sometimes justified in maintaining your original view even in the face of peer disagreement, particularly if you have done your epistemic work carefully and have good reasons for your belief.

Collective knowledge — whether groups can literally know things — raises questions about the relationship between individual and social epistemic states. Groups clearly behave as if they know things: scientific communities accept theories, juries reach verdicts, corporations form beliefs. But can a group know something that no individual member knows? Margaret Gilbert's account of joint commitment suggests that groups can form beliefs through processes of collective endorsement that are not simply reducible to the beliefs of individual members. Philip Kitcher and others have argued that the division of epistemic labor in scientific communities — where different researchers pursue different hypotheses — can be epistemically rational for the community as a whole even when it would not be rational for any individual.

Epistemic injustice is a more recent contribution to social epistemology, developed by Miranda Fricker. Fricker identifies two forms of epistemic injustice — harms done to someone specifically in their capacity as a knower. Testimonial injustice occurs when a speaker's credibility is deflated due to identity prejudice: a jury may discount the testimony of a Black witness because of racial prejudice, for instance, or a woman's professional opinion may be discounted because of sexist assumptions about her competence. Hermeneutical injustice occurs when someone is unable to make sense of their own experience because the conceptual resources are unavailable — when, for example, victims of workplace sexual harassment lacked the concept and vocabulary to understand and articulate what was happening to them before the concept entered common currency.

The internet and digital media have transformed the social conditions for knowledge formation in ways that social epistemology is still working to understand. The concentration of information in a small number of platforms, the algorithmic curation of content, the phenomenon of echo chambers and filter bubbles, the spread of misinformation through social networks — all of these are developments with profound implications for the social epistemology of public discourse. Some researchers argue that social media fundamentally degrades the epistemic environment by selecting for engagement-maximizing content regardless of truth, while others argue that it expands access to diverse information and expertise that was previously unavailable to many people.

The relationship between testimony and expertise is particularly pressing in an era of specialist knowledge. In modern societies, individuals are deeply dependent on testimony from experts whose domains of knowledge they cannot independently evaluate. How should non-experts navigate expert disagreement, evaluate the reliability of expert testimony, and decide which authorities to trust? John Hardwig argued in an influential paper that this epistemic dependence on experts is not epistemically shameful but is a necessary feature of any society in which knowledge is genuinely complex and specialized. The challenge is to develop the social institutions and individual epistemic practices that make reasonable trust in experts possible while maintaining appropriate critical oversight.
