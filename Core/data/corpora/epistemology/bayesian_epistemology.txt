Bayesian epistemology is a formal approach to the theory of rational belief that models degrees of belief — credences — using the probability calculus and analyzes rational belief change through Bayesian conditionalization. Named after the eighteenth-century mathematician and clergyman Thomas Bayes, the framework has become one of the dominant approaches in formal epistemology and has had enormous influence on statistics, cognitive science, decision theory, and the philosophy of science. Its core claim is that rationality in belief is not primarily about which propositions one believes all-or-nothing but about how one distributes numerical degrees of confidence across propositions, and how those degrees should be updated in response to new evidence.

The fundamental representation in Bayesian epistemology is the credence function, a probability distribution over propositions or possible worlds. A rational agent assigns each proposition a credence — a real number between zero and one representing their degree of belief in its truth — subject to the constraint that these credences form a coherent probability distribution. A credence of one represents certainty that the proposition is true, a credence of zero represents certainty that it is false, and intermediate values represent various degrees of uncertainty. The axioms of probability — non-negativity, normalization, and additivity — constrain the structure of rational credences.

The Dutch book argument is the most famous justification for requiring credences to satisfy the probability axioms. A Dutch book is a set of bets that, taken together, guarantee a loss regardless of how the world turns out. Bruno de Finetti proved that an agent's credences are coherent — satisfy the probability axioms — if and only if there is no Dutch book against them. If your credences violate the probability axioms, a clever bookmaker can offer you a set of individually attractive bets that collectively guarantee your loss. This argument makes the rationality of probabilistic credences a matter of avoiding pragmatic incoherence rather than tracking some external standard of truth.

Bayesian conditionalization is the rule for updating credences in response to evidence. When an agent learns that a proposition E is true, they should update their credence in any hypothesis H from P(H) — the prior credence — to P(H|E) — the posterior credence, calculated using Bayes' theorem: P(H|E) = P(E|H) * P(H) / P(E). The likelihood P(E|H) measures how much more or less probable the evidence is given the hypothesis than on average. Evidence E confirms hypothesis H to the degree that P(H|E) > P(H), and disconfirms it to the degree that P(H|E) < P(H). This provides a precise account of evidential reasoning and how hypotheses are tested against data.

The problem of the priors is the most fundamental challenge for Bayesian epistemology. Conditionalization tells us how to update credences in response to evidence, but it does not tell us what initial credences to start with. Different agents with different priors will, even after observing the same evidence, end up with different posterior credences. As long as agents do not assign prior credences of exactly zero or one to any possibility, their credences will converge in the limit as they accumulate evidence — this is the result known as Bayesian convergence or the washing-out of priors. But in practice, the convergence may be extremely slow, and for many of the most interesting scientific and philosophical questions, we do not have access to enough evidence to wash out even quite different starting credences.

Various principles have been proposed to constrain prior credences. The principle of indifference or the principle of maximum entropy holds that in the absence of evidence, we should distribute credences equally across possibilities or, more generally, choose the probability distribution with the highest entropy compatible with what we know. This principle has intuitive appeal but generates paradoxes when different but equivalent ways of setting up a problem yield conflicting uniform distributions. Objective Bayesian epistemologists attempt to develop principled constraints on priors; subjective Bayesians argue that the choice of priors is genuinely up to the individual agent, provided they satisfy coherence.

Bayesian conditionalization assumes that belief update happens only by learning the truth of some proposition with certainty — that one learns E and then conditionalizes on it. But in practice, much learning is softer: we receive uncertain evidence, impressions, partial indicators. Jeffrey conditionalization generalizes standard conditionalization to handle cases in which evidence shifts the probability of a partition of possibilities without fixing any member of the partition to certainty. If I observe something through uncertain conditions and come away with revised credences in the possibilities, I can use Jeffrey conditionalization to update my credences in other propositions consistently with this probabilistic evidence.

The relationship between Bayesian credences and ordinary all-or-nothing belief has been a subject of considerable discussion. The Lockean thesis holds that one believes P if and only if one's credence in P exceeds some threshold. But the Lottery Paradox, due to Henry Kyburg, shows that the Lockean thesis combined with a principle of agglomeration leads to contradiction: if one believes every lottery ticket will lose given a high enough threshold of credence, and the lottery is guaranteed to have a winner, one believes a contradiction. Some epistemologists accept this result as showing that ordinary belief talk is simply imprecise and should be replaced by credence talk; others argue that belief and credence track different phenomena and both have important roles to play in epistemic life.

Bayesian epistemology has proven especially powerful in the philosophy of science, where it provides a precise model of how scientific theories are confirmed or disconfirmed by evidence, how the probability of a theory should change in response to experimental results, and how to understand the predictive success of theories as evidence for their truth. The Bayesian framework handles the problem of old evidence — the observation that evidence already known before a theory was formulated cannot confirm it in the standard updating story — and the underdetermination of theory by evidence, though both require careful and sometimes controversial handling within the framework.
