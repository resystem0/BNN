The epistemology of science investigates how scientific inquiry generates knowledge, what distinguishes genuine science from pseudo-science, how scientific theories relate to the evidence that supports them, and whether the history of radical scientific change should temper our confidence in current science. These questions have been central to philosophy of science since at least the early twentieth century and remain deeply contested, reflecting both the difficulty of the underlying issues and their significance for how we understand one of humanity's most successful epistemic enterprises.

Karl Popper's falsificationism was the most influential twentieth-century attempt to demarcate science from non-science and to characterize the logical structure of scientific reasoning. Popper observed that no finite body of empirical evidence can conclusively verify a universal scientific claim — no number of observed black ravens proves that all ravens are black — but a single counterinstance can conclusively falsify it. One non-black raven is sufficient to refute the universal claim. Popper concluded that the proper attitude of scientific reasoning is not the accumulation of confirmatory evidence but the search for falsifying evidence. A theory is scientific if and only if it is falsifiable — if there are possible observations that would, if they occurred, refute it. Astrology, psychoanalysis, and Marxism fail this test, Popper argued, because their proponents find ways to accommodate any putative counterevidence by auxiliary modifications.

The problem of auxiliary hypotheses, known as the Duhem-Quine problem, complicates the simple falsificationist picture. A scientific theory is never tested in isolation but always in conjunction with auxiliary assumptions about initial conditions, measuring instruments, background conditions, and the reliability of experimental procedures. When an experiment produces an unexpected result, it is logically possible to save the main theory by revising any of these auxiliaries. The empirical refutation is not of the theory alone but of the conjunction of the theory and its auxiliaries, and logic alone does not determine which component to reject. This means that in practice, falsification is never as clean as the simple logical analysis suggests.

Thomas Kuhn's Structure of Scientific Revolutions, published in 1962, transformed the epistemology of science by bringing historical and sociological considerations to bear on questions that had been treated as purely logical. Kuhn argued that science does not proceed by the continuous accumulation of knowledge through falsification but through discrete episodes of revolutionary change interrupted by long periods of what he called normal science. During normal science, scientists work within a paradigm — a shared framework of assumptions, methods, exemplary problems, and standards of solution — that guides research without being subjected to fundamental questioning. The paradigm defines what counts as a legitimate problem and a satisfactory solution, and most scientific work consists in extending and applying the paradigm rather than testing it.

Anomalies — results that cannot be accommodated within the existing paradigm — accumulate over time, eventually creating a crisis that can only be resolved by a paradigm shift: a revolutionary replacement of the old paradigm by a new one. Kuhn argued controversially that competing paradigms are incommensurable — that they do not share enough common standards and concepts to be directly compared or rationally adjudicated. This incommensurability thesis seemed to many to threaten scientific rationality, implying that paradigm changes are driven by social, psychological, and even aesthetic factors rather than purely rational considerations.

Imre Lakatos attempted to preserve scientific rationality against Kuhnian relativism through his methodology of scientific research programs. A research program consists of a hard core of theoretical commitments protected by a protective belt of auxiliary hypotheses. Scientists in a research program do not abandon the hard core when it faces empirical difficulties but instead modify the protective belt to accommodate the anomalies. What distinguishes progressive from degenerating research programs is whether the modifications of the protective belt generate novel predictions that are confirmed — whether the program is advancing knowledge — or whether they are merely ad hoc adjustments that explain anomalies after the fact without generating new testable claims.

The underdetermination of theory by evidence is a persistent epistemological challenge. Any body of evidence is compatible with multiple incompatible theories: the same data can be explained by different theoretical frameworks, and there is no guarantee that the evidence available determines a unique correct theory. Pierre Duhem argued that because theories are tested in conjunction with auxiliaries, any theory can be made compatible with any evidence by suitable modification of the auxiliaries. Quine generalized this to the radical claim that the whole of science faces experience as a corporate body and that any statement can be retained in the face of recalcitrant experience if we make sufficiently drastic revisions elsewhere.

The question of scientific realism — whether our best scientific theories are literally true of an unobservable reality — has been one of the most contested in philosophy of science. Scientific realists argue that the predictive success of mature science would be miraculous if our theories were not at least approximately true: the best explanation of why our theories work so well is that they accurately describe the world. Anti-realists, following instrumentalism or van Fraassen's constructive empiricism, argue that we need only accept that our theories are empirically adequate — that they correctly describe observable phenomena — and that we have no grounds for inference to the reality of unobservable theoretical entities like quarks or fields. The meta-inductive pessimism of Laudan and others adds a historical argument: since the theories of the past that were most successful in their time have subsequently been abandoned as false, induction over the history of science gives us reason to expect that our current successful theories are also false, despite their predictive success.
