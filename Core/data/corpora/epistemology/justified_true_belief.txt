The analysis of knowledge as justified true belief is one of the most venerable and debated proposals in epistemology. Its origins are often traced to Plato's dialogue Meno, where Socrates distinguishes between knowledge and mere true opinion by noting that true opinions are like the statues of Daedalus — fine things, but likely to wander away unless they are tethered. What tethers a true belief and converts it into knowledge, Plato suggests, is a kind of account or justification, an aitia logismos, a reasoned explanation of why the belief is true. This ancient insight was refined and systematized over centuries of epistemological reflection into what became known in the twentieth century as the traditional analysis of knowledge, or the justified true belief (JTB) account.

On the JTB account, a subject S knows that P if and only if three conditions are met: P is true, S believes that P, and S is justified in believing that P. Each condition seems necessary. The truth condition is required because knowledge is factive: one cannot know what is false. If it turns out that I was wrong about something, I did not know it, however firmly I believed it. The belief condition is required because knowledge involves a mental state: one must actually accept or be committed to P, not merely be able to derive it or have access to evidence for it. The justification condition is required to distinguish lucky true guesses from genuine knowledge: if I believe on no basis at all that it will rain tomorrow and it does, I was right but I did not know. What distinguishes knowledge from mere true belief is that the belief is supported by evidence, reasoning, or reliable cognitive processes.

This analysis faced what appeared to be a decisive objection in 1963, when Edmund Gettier published a short but enormously influential paper demonstrating that justified true belief is not sufficient for knowledge. Gettier constructed cases in which all three conditions are satisfied but knowledge is intuitively absent. In the most discussed type of Gettier case, a subject forms a belief on the basis of good evidence that happens to be false, but the belief turns out to be true for reasons unconnected to the evidence. Suppose I look at what I believe is a reliable clock and it reads two o'clock, and I form the justified belief that it is two o'clock. Unknown to me, the clock stopped exactly twelve hours ago and is showing the wrong time — but by coincidence it actually is two o'clock. My belief is justified, it is true, but surely I do not know that it is two o'clock.

The Gettier problem launched an extensive research program aimed at either strengthening the justification condition to rule out Gettier cases or adding a fourth condition to the analysis of knowledge. One response adds a no-false-lemmas condition: knowledge requires that the belief not be derived from any false premises. This blocks many Gettier cases but faces counterexamples in which the knowledge seems to be undermined by something other than a false intermediate premise. Another response appeals to a defeasibility condition: S knows that P only if the justification for S's belief would survive the addition of any true propositions to S's epistemic situation. If there is a true proposition whose addition would defeat S's justification, then S does not know, even if the three standard conditions are met.

Reliabilist responses to the Gettier problem take a different strategy, arguing that the problem stems from a deficiency in our conception of justification rather than in the conditions of knowledge. On reliabilist accounts, a belief is justified if it is produced by a reliable cognitive process — one that tends to produce true beliefs. In Gettier cases, the reliability of the underlying process is undermined even if the subject believes justifiably by the lights of their evidence. Alvin Goldman's process reliabilism proposes that knowledge requires not just justified belief but belief produced by a generally reliable cognitive process. This approach shifts the focus from the subject's internal epistemic situation to the objective reliability of the processes generating beliefs.

The belief condition in the JTB analysis has also received attention. There are cases in which a subject seems to know something without fully or explicitly believing it — cases of tacit or dispositional knowledge that falls below the threshold of conscious endorsement. A skilled tennis player knows where to place her shot in a way that is reflected in her skilled action but may not involve any explicit propositional belief. This connects to debates about the distinction between propositional knowledge (knowing that P) and procedural knowledge (knowing how to do something), the latter of which may resist assimilation to the JTB framework entirely.

The truth condition, though seemingly the least controversial, also raises questions when combined with uncertainty about what truth itself amounts to. Different theories of truth — correspondence, coherence, deflationary, pragmatist — have different implications for what it means for a belief to be true, and these implications feed into the analysis of knowledge. If truth is understood pragmatically as what works or as what is useful for inquiry, then the relationship between truth and justification becomes less sharp, and the traditional three-condition analysis may need revision.

Plato's original insight — that knowledge requires not just belief but some kind of anchor to reality — remains compelling despite the difficulties with the specific JTB formulation. The post-Gettier literature has clarified that the anchor must be more than the subject's internal epistemic situation: knowledge requires not just that the subject have good grounds but that those grounds be appropriately connected to the truth of what is believed. Whether this appropriate connection is best understood in terms of reliability, causal connection, safety, sensitivity, or some other property is the question around which much contemporary epistemological theory revolves.
