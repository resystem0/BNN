One of the most profound and surprising results in all of information theory is Shannon's channel capacity theorem, which establishes that reliable communication over a noisy channel is possible at any rate below a specific maximum, called the channel capacity, and impossible at any rate above it. This result, published in 1948, was revolutionary because it contradicted the prevailing engineering intuition that noise was an unavoidable enemy that ultimately forced a tradeoff between speed and reliability. Shannon showed that, in principle, noise is not an obstacle to reliable communication — it is merely a constraint on the rate.

A communication channel in the information-theoretic sense is a probabilistic mapping from an input alphabet to an output alphabet. The sender chooses an input symbol, and the channel produces an output symbol according to a probability distribution that depends on the input. In the binary symmetric channel, one of the simplest and most studied models, the input and output are each a single bit, and the channel flips the bit with probability p and leaves it unchanged with probability one minus p. When p equals zero, the channel is noiseless. When p equals one half, the output is independent of the input and the channel is completely useless. For intermediate p, the channel is noisy but not totally unreliable.

The capacity of a channel is defined as the maximum of the mutual information between input and output over all possible input distributions. Mutual information, denoted I(X;Y), measures how much information the output Y conveys about the input X. It is defined as the entropy of Y minus the conditional entropy of Y given X, or equivalently as the entropy of X minus the conditional entropy of X given Y. The mutual information is zero when X and Y are independent — the output tells us nothing about the input — and equals the entropy of X when Y determines X completely. Maximizing mutual information over the input distribution finds the distribution that makes the best use of the channel.

For the binary symmetric channel with crossover probability p, the capacity is one minus H(p), where H(p) is the binary entropy function: minus p log p minus (one minus p) log(one minus p). At p equals zero (noiseless), the capacity is one bit per channel use. At p equals one half (completely noisy), the capacity is zero. For intermediate noise, the capacity lies between zero and one. The input distribution that achieves the capacity is the uniform distribution — sending each bit with equal probability.

For the additive white Gaussian noise channel, a model of fundamental importance for wireless and wired communication, the input is a real number constrained to have average power P, and the output is the input plus a Gaussian noise term with variance N. Shannon showed that the capacity of this channel is one half times the log base 2 of one plus P over N, measured in bits per channel use. This is the celebrated Shannon-Hartley theorem, and the quantity P over N is the signal-to-noise ratio. The capacity grows logarithmically with signal-to-noise ratio, which means that doubling the signal power increases capacity by only one bit per channel use. Bandwidth — the range of frequencies over which the channel operates — multiplies the capacity directly, making bandwidth a more valuable resource than power for achieving high data rates.

Shannon's channel coding theorem, also called the noisy channel coding theorem, states that for any rate R less than the channel capacity C, there exists a family of codes such that the probability of error in decoding can be made arbitrarily small as the block length of the code grows. Conversely, for any rate R greater than C, the error probability is bounded away from zero regardless of the code used. This dichotomy between rates below and above capacity is fundamental: capacity is a sharp threshold, not a gradual degradation.

The proof of the channel coding theorem uses a probabilistic argument called random coding. Shannon showed that if codewords are chosen randomly from the input alphabet according to an appropriately chosen probability distribution, and if the block length is large enough, then with high probability the resulting code achieves the promised low error rate. The argument does not construct specific good codes — it merely proves they exist. Finding constructive codes that approach capacity efficiently was a major engineering challenge that was only fully addressed with the development of turbo codes in 1993 and LDPC codes, originally invented by Gallager in 1960 and rediscovered in the 1990s. Both families of codes, combined with iterative belief-propagation decoding algorithms, can achieve performance very close to the Shannon limit with manageable complexity.

The concept of channel capacity extends naturally to networks of channels, where messages pass through multiple nodes and links. Network information theory studies the capacities of multi-user channels including the broadcast channel (one sender, many receivers), the multiple-access channel (many senders, one receiver), and the interference channel (multiple sender-receiver pairs sharing the same medium). The capacity of these network channels is often much harder to determine than for point-to-point channels, and for the interference channel it remains unknown in general after decades of research.

The distinction between channel capacity and source entropy is the fundamental separation theorem of information theory. Source coding (compression) and channel coding (error correction) can be performed independently and optimally: compress the source to its entropy rate and then encode at the channel capacity to achieve reliable communication. The system is optimal as long as the entropy rate of the source does not exceed the channel capacity. This elegant separation simplifies system design enormously and underlies the architecture of all modern digital communication systems.
