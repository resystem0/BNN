Information theory, founded by Claude Shannon with his landmark 1948 paper "A Mathematical Theory of Communication," provides a rigorous mathematical framework for quantifying information, measuring uncertainty, and analyzing communication systems. At the center of this framework is a single concept — Shannon entropy — that measures the amount of uncertainty in a random variable or, equivalently, the average information content of a message from a source.

Shannon's insight was to ask: how much information do we receive when we learn the outcome of a random event? The answer should depend on how surprising or unexpected the outcome is. If a coin is heavily biased and almost always lands heads, then learning that it landed heads tells us very little — we already expected it. But if the coin is fair and we learn that it landed tails, we gain more information. Shannon formalized this intuition by defining the self-information or surprisal of an event with probability p as the negative logarithm of p. Conventionally the logarithm is taken base 2, in which case the unit is the bit. An event with probability one half has self-information of one bit. An event with probability one quarter has self-information of two bits. An event with probability one has self-information of zero — learning that a certain event occurred gives us no information.

The Shannon entropy of a discrete random variable X that takes values in a finite set is the expected value of the self-information, which equals negative the sum over all possible values x of P(X equals x) times the logarithm base 2 of P(X equals x). This is written as H(X) equals negative the sum of P(x) log P(x). The entropy measures the average uncertainty in X before we observe it, or equivalently, the average information we gain from observing it. For a fair coin, each outcome has probability one half, and the entropy is one bit. For a fair six-sided die, the entropy is log base 2 of 6, approximately 2.58 bits.

The entropy is maximized when all outcomes are equally probable, which is when the uncertainty is greatest. For a random variable with n possible values, the maximum entropy is log base 2 of n bits, achieved by the uniform distribution. This makes intuitive sense: we are most uncertain when all outcomes are equally likely. The entropy is minimized at zero when one outcome has probability one — there is no uncertainty at all. Any bias or skew in the distribution reduces entropy below the maximum.

Shannon entropy has several important properties that any reasonable measure of information should satisfy, and it is in fact the unique measure satisfying certain natural axioms. It is continuous in the probabilities — small changes in probabilities should produce small changes in entropy. It is maximized by the uniform distribution for a given number of outcomes. It is additive for independent random variables: the entropy of two independent random variables observed together equals the sum of their individual entropies. These properties were Shannon's axiomatic motivation for the formula, and he proved that the formula is the unique family (up to a constant multiplicative factor, which corresponds to the choice of logarithm base) satisfying these axioms.

The connection between Shannon entropy and thermodynamic entropy is not merely formal. Shannon himself noted the similarity of his formula to Boltzmann's entropy formula in statistical mechanics, and John von Neumann reportedly suggested to Shannon that he call his quantity entropy both because of this formal similarity and because "nobody knows what entropy really is, so in any debate you will always have the advantage." The connection is deeper than formality: Boltzmann's entropy S equals k times the natural log of W can be rewritten in terms of probabilities for the microstate distribution, and then it becomes formally identical to Shannon's entropy (with a different constant and base). The thermodynamic interpretation of Shannon entropy was made precise by Rolf Landauer and Charles Bennett in their work on the thermodynamics of computation.

Conditional entropy generalizes the entropy concept to situations where we have partial information. The conditional entropy H(X given Y) is the average entropy of X after Y has been observed, which represents the residual uncertainty in X given knowledge of Y. It satisfies the chain rule: the entropy of the joint variable (X, Y) equals the entropy of Y plus the conditional entropy of X given Y. This mirrors the probability chain rule and has a clear information-theoretic interpretation: the total uncertainty in two variables is the uncertainty in one plus the remaining uncertainty in the other given the first.

Differential entropy extends the Shannon entropy concept to continuous random variables, replacing sums with integrals. However, continuous entropy has some counterintuitive properties that discrete entropy does not: it can be negative, it is not invariant under coordinate changes, and it does not have the same direct interpretation as bits of information. The most natural and useful continuous entropy concept is the relative entropy or Kullback-Leibler divergence, which measures how much a distribution P differs from a reference distribution Q and is always non-negative.

The source coding theorem, one of Shannon's fundamental results, connects entropy to data compression. Shannon proved that a source with entropy H bits per symbol can be encoded with on average as close to H bits per symbol as desired, but not with fewer than H bits per symbol on average, without loss of information. The entropy is therefore the fundamental limit on lossless compression — the minimum average number of bits needed to represent symbols from the source. No compression algorithm can do better than the entropy rate, and good compression algorithms approach it. This gives entropy a direct operational meaning as a compression limit, independent of any interpretive appeal to uncertainty or information.
