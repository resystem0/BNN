Mutual information is a measure of the statistical dependence between two random variables, capturing how much knowing one variable reduces uncertainty about the other. It is one of the most versatile and important quantities in information theory, serving as the key quantity in the definition of channel capacity, appearing in bounds on learning and inference, and providing a model-free measure of association that detects nonlinear dependencies invisible to correlation coefficients.

The mutual information between two random variables X and Y is defined as I(X; Y) equals the entropy of X minus the conditional entropy of X given Y. Equivalently, it equals the entropy of Y minus the conditional entropy of Y given X, and also the sum of the entropies of X and Y minus the joint entropy of the pair (X, Y). All these expressions yield the same value, reflecting the symmetric nature of mutual information: I(X; Y) equals I(Y; X). The symmetry is conceptually important — the amount of information X tells us about Y is the same as the amount Y tells us about X.

Mutual information is always non-negative, and it equals zero if and only if X and Y are statistically independent. When X and Y are independent, knowing Y tells us nothing about X, so the conditional entropy of X given Y equals the entropy of X, and their difference is zero. The maximum possible mutual information is limited by the entropies of the individual variables: I(X; Y) cannot exceed H(X) or H(Y). It equals H(X) when Y determines X completely — knowing Y removes all uncertainty about X.

The relationship between mutual information and the Kullback-Leibler divergence illuminates its structure. Mutual information equals the Kullback-Leibler divergence from the joint distribution P(X, Y) to the product of the marginal distributions P(X) times P(Y). The KL divergence is a measure of how different two probability distributions are, always non-negative and zero only when the distributions are identical. Mutual information is zero only when the joint distribution equals the product of the marginals — precisely the condition for independence. This connection shows that mutual information measures how far the joint distribution deviates from what it would be if the variables were independent.

In the context of channel coding, mutual information plays a central role as the quantity that is maximized to find the channel capacity. The capacity of a channel is the maximum mutual information between the input X and the output Y over all possible input distributions. This maximum measures the best-case amount of information the output reveals about the input — the highest rate at which the channel can reliably convey information. For fixed channel transition probabilities, the mutual information as a function of the input distribution is a concave function, so the maximum is uniquely achieved (though potentially by multiple input distributions if the capacity-achieving distribution is not unique).

The data processing inequality is one of the most fundamental results involving mutual information. It states that for a Markov chain X to Y to Z — where Z depends on the past only through Y, not directly through X — the mutual information I(X; Z) is at most I(X; Y). In other words, processing the data can never increase the information it contains about the original variable X. If Y is a noisy version of X and Z is a function of Y, then Z cannot contain more information about X than Y does. This result formalizes the intuition that information can be lost but not created by processing: every step of lossy processing reduces or at best preserves mutual information.

The data processing inequality has profound implications. In any communication system or inference pipeline, information about the source cannot increase as signals are processed. The bottleneck is always the weakest link — the stage with the lowest mutual information between its input and the original source. This motivates the information bottleneck method, developed by Tishby, Pereira, and Bialek, which provides a principled framework for finding compressed representations of X that retain maximum information about a relevant variable Y. The method seeks the representation that minimizes information about X while maximizing information about Y, tracing an information bottleneck curve that balances compression and relevance.

Conditional mutual information I(X; Y given Z) measures how much information X and Y share beyond what is already explained by Z. It is defined as the entropy of X given Z minus the conditional entropy of X given both Y and Z. The chain rule for mutual information states that I(X; Y, Z) equals I(X; Y) plus I(X; Z given Y), expressing the total information X shares with the pair (Y, Z) as the information shared with Y alone plus the additional information shared with Z beyond what Y already provides.

For continuous random variables, mutual information is defined using differential entropies. Unlike differential entropy, which can be negative and is not invariant under coordinate transformations, mutual information for continuous variables retains its non-negativity and invariance under one-to-one transformations. For jointly Gaussian random variables, the mutual information is one half times the log of one divided by one minus the squared correlation coefficient, connecting mutual information to the familiar correlation. For non-Gaussian variables, mutual information detects dependencies that correlation misses, including nonlinear relationships and higher-order dependencies, making it a more general measure of statistical association.

Mutual information has found applications well beyond classical information theory. In machine learning, it appears in feature selection (selecting features that have high mutual information with the target variable), in the analysis of neural networks (the information bottleneck interpretation of deep learning), and in generative models. In neuroscience, it is used to measure how much information a neural spike train carries about a stimulus. In genetics, it measures the co-evolution of amino acid pairs in protein sequences. In all these applications, mutual information's ability to quantify statistical dependence without assumptions about linearity or distributional form makes it a powerful and flexible tool.
