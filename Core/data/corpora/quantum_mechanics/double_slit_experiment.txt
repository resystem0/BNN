The Double-Slit Experiment

The double-slit experiment is one of the most celebrated and conceptually revealing experiments in the history of physics. First performed with light by Thomas Young in 1801 to demonstrate the wave nature of light, and later reimagined and extended into the quantum domain, the double-slit experiment reveals the core mystery of quantum mechanics: that particles exhibit wave-like interference when not observed, yet behave as discrete particles when a measurement determines which path they travel. Richard Feynman declared the double-slit experiment to contain "the only mystery" of quantum mechanics — every other quantum phenomenon is some elaboration of the same underlying puzzle.

In the classical wave version of the experiment, a coherent light source illuminates a barrier with two narrow slits separated by a distance d. The light passing through each slit diffracts and the two sets of waves overlap in the region beyond the barrier. Where crests from one slit arrive simultaneously with crests from the other, constructive interference produces bright fringes. Where crests arrive simultaneously with troughs, destructive interference produces dark fringes. The result on a distant screen is a characteristic alternating pattern of bright and dark stripes. The fringe spacing Δy ≈ λL/d, where λ is the wavelength and L is the distance to the screen, provides a precise measurement of the wavelength. Young's original experiment yielded the first reliable measurements of visible light wavelengths and conclusively established the wave theory of light against the Newtonian corpuscular theory.

The quantum version of the experiment, performed with single electrons, reveals something far stranger. An electron source emits single electrons one at a time toward the double-slit barrier. Each electron is detected as a localized flash at a single point on a detector screen — a perfectly particle-like arrival. After many such detections, the accumulated pattern of impact points is not what one would expect from classical particles passing through two slits, which would produce two bright bands directly behind each slit. Instead, the accumulated pattern is the same alternating interference fringe pattern seen with waves. Each individual electron somehow "interferes with itself," passing through both slits simultaneously in a quantum superposition, and the probability of detection at any given point is governed by the square of the wave function amplitude, which exhibits the wave interference pattern.

This result was first achieved in practice much later than Young's light experiment. Davisson and Germer demonstrated electron diffraction from a crystal in 1927, establishing the wave nature of electrons in accord with de Broglie's 1924 hypothesis. The first true double-slit experiment with single electrons was performed by Claus Jönsson in 1961 in Tübingen. Later, in 1989, Akira Tonomura and colleagues in Japan performed a definitive version with single electrons detected one by one, making clear that the interference pattern builds up gradually and cannot be attributed to any kind of classical interaction between multiple electrons. The experiment has since been repeated with single neutrons, single atoms, and large molecules including C₆₀ fullerene (performed by Anton Zeilinger's group in Vienna in 1999), C₇₀, and molecules containing over 2000 atoms. In each case, the same interference pattern emerges, and the de Broglie wavelength λ = h/p accounts for the fringe spacing, with p the momentum of the particle.

The most profound aspect of the double-slit experiment concerns what happens when one attempts to determine which slit the particle passes through. If a detector is placed at one or both slits to register which path the particle takes, the interference pattern vanishes and is replaced by the two-band classical pattern. This occurs regardless of the details of the measurement — the mere possibility of knowing which-path information is sufficient to destroy interference. This is not a mechanical effect of the detector disturbing the particle; in some experimental arrangements, the which-path information is recorded in the environment and then erased before the particle reaches the detection screen, yet the interference pattern can be recovered. This quantum eraser experiment, first proposed by Marlan Scully and Kai Drühl in 1982 and demonstrated experimentally several times thereafter, shows that the presence or absence of interference depends on whether which-path information exists anywhere in the universe, not merely on whether a human observer reads the detector.

The mathematical description of the double-slit experiment follows directly from the superposition principle. Let the amplitude for the particle to reach a point x on the screen via slit 1 be ψ₁(x) and via slit 2 be ψ₂(x). When both slits are open and which-path information is absent, the total amplitude is ψ(x) = ψ₁(x) + ψ₂(x), and the probability is P(x) = |ψ₁(x) + ψ₂(x)|² = |ψ₁|² + |ψ₂|² + 2Re(ψ₁*ψ₂). The last term, the interference term 2Re(ψ₁*ψ₂), is responsible for the fringe pattern. When a which-path measurement is made, the particle's state becomes entangled with the detector state, and tracing over the detector degree of freedom eliminates the interference term, leaving P(x) = |ψ₁|² + |ψ₂|². The transition from quantum to classical behavior here is understood as a consequence of decoherence: entanglement with the environment destroys the phase coherence between the two paths.

The double-slit experiment also illustrates the complementarity principle articulated by Niels Bohr: wave behavior and particle behavior are mutually exclusive manifestations that cannot be simultaneously observed. Quantitative formulations of wave-particle duality, developed by Berthold-Georg Englert in 1996, establish an inequality relating the fringe visibility V (which measures the sharpness of interference) and the which-path distinguishability D: V² + D² ≤ 1. When D = 1 (complete which-path information), V = 0 (no interference). When V = 1 (perfect interference), D = 0 (no which-path information). Intermediate cases with partial which-path information produce correspondingly reduced fringe visibility, a prediction confirmed in many laboratory experiments using entangled photon pairs and atom interferometers.

Variations of the double-slit experiment have tested quantum mechanics in increasingly extreme regimes. Neutron interferometry, using macroscopic crystal interferometers where the two paths can be separated by centimeters, has verified the phase shifts induced by gravity (showing that gravitational potential affects the phase of a quantum wave function) and the famous 4π spinor rotation symmetry of spin-1/2 particles (demonstrating that a neutron must rotate through 720 degrees, not 360, to return to its original spin state). Atom interferometry has achieved extraordinary precision in measuring gravitational acceleration, the fine structure constant, and gravitational wave strain, making quantum interference one of the most precise measurement tools in modern science.
