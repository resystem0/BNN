Spin and Angular Momentum

Angular momentum in quantum mechanics encompasses both the familiar orbital angular momentum arising from a particle's motion through space and a purely quantum mechanical intrinsic property called spin. Spin has no classical analogue: it is not the result of a particle physically rotating about its axis, yet it behaves mathematically like an angular momentum and produces measurable magnetic moments and mechanical effects. The discovery and theoretical understanding of spin transformed atomic physics, explained the periodic table's fine structure, provided the basis for nuclear magnetic resonance, and laid the foundation for quantum information theory through the concept of the qubit.

In classical mechanics, the orbital angular momentum of a particle is L = r × p, the cross product of its position and momentum vectors. In quantum mechanics, this becomes an operator L̂ = r̂ × p̂, and its components satisfy the commutation relations [L̂_x, L̂_y] = iℏL̂_z, [L̂_y, L̂_z] = iℏL̂_x, and [L̂_z, L̂_x] = iℏL̂_y. These commutation relations, not any particular spatial picture, are taken as the defining algebraic structure of angular momentum in quantum mechanics. From this algebra alone, one can derive all the eigenvalue structure: the squared magnitude L² = L̂_x² + L̂_y² + L̂_z² commutes with all components and has eigenvalues ℓ(ℓ+1)ℏ², while L̂_z has eigenvalues mℏ, where ℓ is a non-negative integer and m ranges from -ℓ to +ℓ in integer steps. The quantum number ℓ characterizes the orbital angular momentum state, and m is its projection along a chosen quantization axis.

The existence of spin was first indicated experimentally by Otto Stern and Walther Gerlach in 1922. In their experiment, a beam of silver atoms was passed through an inhomogeneous magnetic field. Classically, one would expect the beam to spread continuously into a smear, reflecting a continuous distribution of magnetic moment orientations. Instead, the beam split into exactly two discrete components, corresponding to only two values of the magnetic moment along the field direction. Since the outermost electron of silver has zero orbital angular momentum (it is in an s-state with ℓ = 0), the splitting could not be explained by orbital angular momentum and pointed to an intrinsic angular momentum of the electron itself. The two-valued splitting indicated a half-integer angular momentum quantum number.

In 1925, Samuel Goudsmit and George Uhlenbeck proposed that the electron carries an intrinsic angular momentum of magnitude ½ℏ, which they pictured as the electron spinning about its own axis. While this classical picture is known to be incorrect (an electron is a point particle, and a literal spinning sphere would require its surface to move faster than light for any reasonable size estimate), the terminology "spin" has persisted. The quantum mechanical treatment, formalized by Wolfgang Pauli using 2×2 matrices (the Pauli matrices), describes spin-1/2 particles with a two-dimensional Hilbert space. The spin operators are Ŝ_x = (ℏ/2)σ_x, Ŝ_y = (ℏ/2)σ_y, and Ŝ_z = (ℏ/2)σ_z, where σ_x, σ_y, σ_z are the Pauli matrices: σ_x = [[0,1],[1,0]], σ_y = [[0,-i],[i,0]], σ_z = [[1,0],[0,-1]]. These operators satisfy the same commutation relations as orbital angular momentum, confirming that spin is a bona fide angular momentum.

The relativistic quantum mechanical treatment of spin came with Paul Dirac's 1928 equation. Dirac sought a relativistic wave equation linear in both time and space derivatives, and found that the electron wave function necessarily had four components. Two components correspond to spin-up and spin-down states of the electron; the other two, initially puzzling, were later identified as the positron (the electron's antiparticle) states. The Dirac equation thus predicted the g-factor of the electron spin magnetic moment: the gyromagnetic ratio relating spin angular momentum to magnetic moment. At tree level, the Dirac equation predicts g = 2 for the electron, whereas for orbital angular momentum g = 1. The small deviation from g = 2 (the "anomalous magnetic moment," with g ≈ 2.00231930436256) arises from quantum electrodynamic corrections and is one of the most precisely calculated and measured quantities in physics.

General angular momentum in quantum mechanics is described by quantum numbers j and m, where j can be any non-negative integer or half-integer. Integer values of j correspond to orbital angular momentum (or spin of bosons like photons), while half-integer values correspond to spin of fermions. Particles with half-integer spin — electrons, protons, neutrons, quarks, and their partners — are collectively called fermions and obey Fermi-Dirac statistics, which includes the Pauli exclusion principle: no two identical fermions can occupy the same quantum state simultaneously. Particles with integer spin — photons, pions, W and Z bosons, Higgs bosons — are bosons and obey Bose-Einstein statistics, which allows (and in some cases encourages) multiple particles to occupy the same quantum state.

The Pauli exclusion principle, which follows directly from the antisymmetry requirement on fermionic wave functions, is responsible for the shell structure of atoms, the diversity of chemical elements, and the stability of matter. Without it, all electrons in an atom would occupy the lowest-energy orbital, and the rich chemical properties of the elements would not exist. The Pauli principle also underlies the stability of neutron stars: neutron degeneracy pressure, arising from Pauli exclusion among neutrons, supports stars of up to about two solar masses against gravitational collapse.

The addition of angular momenta is a fundamental operation in atomic and nuclear physics. When two angular momenta J₁ and J₂ are combined, the total angular momentum J = J₁ + J₂ can take values from |j₁ - j₂| to j₁ + j₂ in integer steps. The Clebsch-Gordan coefficients give the coefficients for expressing the combined states in terms of the individual states and are tabulated for all combinations of angular momenta up to moderately large values. Spin-orbit coupling — the interaction between an electron's orbital angular momentum and its spin, arising from the relativistic transformation of the electric field of the nucleus into a magnetic field in the electron's rest frame — is responsible for the fine structure of atomic spectra, splitting energy levels that would otherwise be degenerate into multiplets. Nuclear spin, arising from the coupling of proton and neutron spins and orbital motions within the nucleus, underlies nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), technologies of enormous scientific and medical importance.
