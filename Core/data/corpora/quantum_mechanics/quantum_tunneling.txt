Quantum Tunneling

Quantum tunneling is a phenomenon by which a quantum particle passes through a potential energy barrier that it classically could not surmount. In classical mechanics, if a particle does not have enough kinetic energy to overcome a potential energy barrier, it is simply reflected; it can never appear on the other side. In quantum mechanics, the wave-like nature of particles means that the wave function does not abruptly vanish at the barrier but penetrates and decays exponentially into the classically forbidden region. If the barrier is finite in width, the wave function can emerge on the far side with nonzero amplitude, giving a finite probability for the particle to be transmitted through the barrier. This classically impossible process is not a curiosity or an approximation — it is a precise and experimentally verified prediction of quantum mechanics with consequences that span nuclear physics, chemistry, astrophysics, and modern technology.

The quantum mechanical treatment of tunneling begins with the one-dimensional rectangular potential barrier. Consider a particle of mass m and total energy E approaching a barrier of height V₀ > E and width a. In the classically allowed regions on either side of the barrier, the wave function oscillates as a plane wave. Inside the barrier, where the kinetic energy would be negative classically, the Schrödinger equation yields a decaying exponential solution: ψ(x) ~ e^(-κx), where κ = √(2m(V₀ - E))/ℏ is the decay constant. The transmission coefficient T — the ratio of the transmitted probability flux to the incident flux — depends sensitively on both the height and width of the barrier. For a thick or tall barrier, T ≈ e^(-2κa), an exponentially small but nonzero quantity. This exponential dependence on barrier width and height-energy difference is the hallmark of tunneling and explains why tunneling rates can vary over many orders of magnitude for seemingly small changes in parameters.

Alpha decay, the process by which heavy atomic nuclei emit alpha particles (helium-4 nuclei), was the first phenomenon to be explained by quantum tunneling. In 1928, George Gamow and, independently, Ronald Gurney and Edward Condon applied quantum tunneling theory to the nucleus. An alpha particle inside a nucleus is bound by the attractive strong nuclear force, but this is surrounded by a repulsive Coulomb barrier due to the electromagnetic repulsion between the positively charged alpha particle and the residual nucleus. The alpha particle lacks sufficient energy to classically surmount this Coulomb barrier, yet alpha decay does occur — at rates that span over twenty orders of magnitude across different isotopes. The Gamow theory, which models the Coulomb barrier as a smoothly varying potential and integrates the WKB approximation over the full barrier, reproduces the observed half-lives with remarkable accuracy and explains the enormous variation in decay rates through the exponential sensitivity of tunneling to the barrier integral.

The WKB approximation (named for Wentzel, Kramers, and Brillouin) provides a general semiclassical method for calculating tunneling probabilities through non-rectangular barriers. The transmission probability is given by T ~ exp(-2 ∫√(2m(V(x)-E))/ℏ dx), where the integral is taken over the classically forbidden region. This formula makes explicit how the tunneling probability depends on the integral of the "imaginary momentum" throughout the barrier and provides the mathematical basis for the Gamow factor in alpha decay, field emission theory, and many other applications.

Nuclear fusion in stars depends fundamentally on quantum tunneling. The thermonuclear fusion reactions that power the sun and other stars require protons to overcome the Coulomb repulsion and approach each other closely enough for the strong nuclear force to initiate fusion. At the temperatures in stellar cores — roughly 15 million Kelvin — the thermal energies of protons are far below the classical Coulomb barrier. Fusion rates in the sun are dominated by protons tunneling through this barrier, a process that proceeds at rates so low that individual protons in the solar core take billions of years on average to fuse. The Gamow window, a narrow range of energies near the most probable tunneling contribution, determines which nuclear reaction rates are astrophysically relevant and underlies the theory of stellar nucleosynthesis.

Chemical reaction rates are significantly influenced by tunneling, particularly for reactions involving the transfer of light atoms or protons. Proton-coupled electron transfer, hydrogen atom tunneling, and enzyme-catalyzed hydrogen transfers are areas of active research at the interface of quantum mechanics and biochemistry. Reactions that would be extremely slow by classical transition-state theory are enormously accelerated by tunneling, especially at low temperatures where classical over-barrier processes are frozen out. This has implications for the mechanisms of enzyme catalysis, atmospheric chemistry, and the behavior of chemical reactions on cold interstellar dust grains.

The scanning tunneling microscope (STM), invented by Gerd Binnig and Heinrich Rohrer at IBM Zürich in 1981 (for which they received the 1986 Nobel Prize in Physics), is a direct practical application of quantum tunneling. In the STM, a sharp metallic tip is brought within a few angstroms of a conducting surface. When a bias voltage is applied, electrons tunnel through the vacuum gap between tip and surface. The tunneling current is extraordinarily sensitive to the tip-surface separation because of the exponential dependence of T on gap width: a change of just one angstrom in separation changes the current by roughly an order of magnitude. By scanning the tip across the surface and maintaining constant current through a feedback loop, the STM maps the surface topography with atomic resolution. Binnig and Rohrer's first STM images revealed the atomic reconstruction of the silicon (111) surface in unprecedented detail, inaugurating the field of nanoscale surface science.

Tunneling is also central to modern electronic devices. In tunnel diodes (Esaki diodes), invented by Leo Esaki in 1957, heavy doping of a p-n junction semiconductor makes the depletion region thin enough for electrons to tunnel between the conduction band of the n-type region and the valence band of the p-type region, producing a region of negative differential resistance used in high-frequency oscillators. Flash memory cells rely on Fowler-Nordheim tunneling of electrons through thin oxide layers to write and erase stored charge. Josephson junctions — thin insulating barriers between two superconductors — carry a supercurrent via Cooper pair tunneling and are the operating principle behind SQUIDs (superconducting quantum interference devices) used in ultrasensitive magnetometry, and are a key component in superconducting qubit architectures for quantum computing.
