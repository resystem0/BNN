The Many-Worlds Interpretation

The many-worlds interpretation (MWI) of quantum mechanics, also called the Everett interpretation or the relative-state interpretation, proposes that the wave function never collapses and that quantum mechanics applies universally to all physical systems without exception, including measuring apparatuses, observers, and the universe as a whole. As a consequence, every quantum measurement causes the universe to branch into multiple non-communicating copies, one for each possible measurement outcome, with each outcome being realized in some branch. What we experience as the random result of a quantum measurement is, on this view, only our local experience of one branch; other versions of us in other branches experience other outcomes. The many-worlds interpretation was proposed by Hugh Everett III in his 1957 Princeton doctoral dissertation, developed with the encouragement of his advisor John Archibald Wheeler, and has gained substantial support among theoretical physicists and philosophers of physics in subsequent decades.

Everett's starting point was dissatisfaction with the Copenhagen interpretation's postulation of wave function collapse as a fundamental, unanalyzable process. In Copenhagen, the Schrödinger equation governs the evolution of isolated quantum systems, but a separate and physically unexplained collapse postulate governs what happens during measurement. Everett asked: what if collapse is an illusion, a consequence of the observer becoming entangled with the system, and the Schrödinger equation holds absolutely everywhere? He formalized this question by treating the observer's memory states as quantum variables and analyzing the full quantum state of the combined observer-system.

In Everett's formalism, consider a spin-1/2 particle in the superposition |Ψ_particle⟩ = α|↑⟩ + β|↓⟩ being measured by an observer whose memory can record either "spin-up" or "spin-down." Before measurement, the observer is in a neutral ready state |ready⟩. The Schrödinger equation evolves the combined state to α|↑⟩|observer recorded ↑⟩ + β|↓⟩|observer recorded ↓⟩. There is no collapse; instead, the observer is now in a superposition of two "branches," one in which they recorded spin-up and one in which they recorded spin-down. Each branch contains a well-defined observer with a definite memory of a definite outcome. The global quantum state is a superposition of many such branches, and within each branch everything appears as if a single definite outcome occurred. This, Everett argued, explains why observers always report single definite outcomes even though the wave function contains all outcomes simultaneously.

The branching process in the many-worlds interpretation is not a sudden, discrete splitting event. It occurs continuously as quantum systems become entangled with their environments and with measuring devices. Modern treatments of the MWI incorporate decoherence theory to explain how branches become effectively non-interfering: the entanglement of the system with environmental degrees of freedom rapidly makes the off-diagonal terms in the reduced density matrix negligible in the preferred pointer basis, so different branches become dynamically isolated from one another. The branches are real in the sense that they all exist in the universal wave function, but they cannot interact with each other once decoherence has occurred, making them empirically inaccessible from within any single branch.

One of the deepest challenges for the many-worlds interpretation is the probability problem: if all outcomes occur, what justifies assigning Born rule probabilities to them? In what sense can it be meaningful to say that a spin-up outcome has probability |α|² if both outcomes occur? Several responses have been proposed. Everett himself attempted to derive the Born rule from a frequency-counting argument applied to infinitely many repeated measurements. David Deutsch and, more rigorously, David Wallace have argued that the Born rule can be derived from decision-theoretic axioms: a rational agent within a branching universe, who knows the MWI is correct and who obeys certain rationality axioms analogous to those of classical decision theory (but adapted for branching), will act as if assigning Born rule probabilities to the different branches. This derivation is controversial; critics argue that the rationality axioms already implicitly contain the Born rule or require justification that presupposes the very probability weights they claim to derive.

A related problem is the preferred basis problem: what determines the branching basis? The universal wave function can be expressed in infinitely many different bases, and without an additional principle, there is no fact about how it branches. The MWI's answer invokes environmental decoherence: the preferred basis is the pointer basis selected by the structure of environmental interactions. The branches are the ones that are stable against environmental monitoring, and they correspond to the classical properties we observe. This is widely regarded as a satisfactory response in principle, though working out the details for realistic environments and showing that the "branches" are well-defined with probability close to one requires technical machinery and certain approximations.

The ontology of the many-worlds interpretation is genuinely radical. On the most straightforward reading, the MWI posits a literally vast — perhaps infinitely vast — proliferation of simultaneously real universes. Each quantum event with n possible outcomes multiplies the number of branches by n. Across cosmic history, the number of branches is incomprehensibly large. Some proponents of the MWI regard this proliferation as a virtue — a purely mechanical consequence of taking the simplest possible fundamental laws seriously — while critics regard it as a reductio ad absurdum and suggest that Occam's razor should favor interpretations that do not multiply universes without necessity.

Defenders of the MWI often argue that the wave function itself is the most economical ontology: the MWI takes the wave function at face value as a real physical object and adds nothing to it. By contrast, collapse interpretations add an unspecified physical process (collapse) that has never been observed and that introduces a preferred class of events (measurements) requiring special treatment. David Bohm's pilot-wave theory adds hidden variables in addition to the wave function. Objective collapse theories like GRW (Ghirardi-Rimini-Weber) add stochastic modification terms to the Schrödinger equation. The many-worlds interpretation claims to be the "bare bones" of quantum mechanics with nothing added and nothing subtracted.

The many-worlds interpretation has implications for quantum cosmology and the problem of the wave function of the universe. In standard Copenhagen quantum mechanics, measurement requires a classical observer outside the system, but for the universe as a whole, there is no external observer. The Everett interpretation naturally accommodates quantum cosmology by allowing the universal wave function to evolve unitarily without any external collapse trigger. For this reason, Everett's framework has been adopted by many quantum cosmologists, including Stephen Hawking and James Hartle, who developed the no-boundary proposal for the wave function of the universe within an Everettian framework. The many-worlds interpretation remains a major position in the foundations of quantum mechanics and is taken seriously as a research program by a substantial and growing community of physicists and philosophers.
