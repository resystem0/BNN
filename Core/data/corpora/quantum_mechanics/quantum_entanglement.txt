Quantum Entanglement

Quantum entanglement is a phenomenon in which two or more particles become correlated in such a way that the quantum state of each particle cannot be described independently of the others, regardless of the distance separating them. Entanglement is not a classical correlation arising from shared history or hidden common causes; it is a distinctly quantum mechanical property with no analogue in classical physics. Albert Einstein, Boris Podolsky, and Nathan Rosen first analyzed entanglement in their celebrated 1935 EPR paper, where they argued that quantum mechanics was either incomplete or implied a troubling form of "spooky action at a distance." This argument set the stage for decades of foundational research culminating in John Bell's 1964 theorem and the experimental tests that followed.

Mathematically, entanglement arises when the joint quantum state of a multi-particle system cannot be written as a tensor product of individual particle states. For two qubits, a product state would look like |Ψ⟩ = |ψ₁⟩ ⊗ |ψ₂⟩, where each particle has its own well-defined quantum state. An entangled state, by contrast, cannot be so factored. The four maximally entangled two-qubit states, known as the Bell states, are canonical examples. The Bell state |Φ⁺⟩ = (1/√2)(|00⟩ + |11⟩) represents a superposition in which both qubits are simultaneously in the |0⟩ state and both in the |1⟩ state, but neither qubit has a definite state on its own. If one qubit is measured and found to be |0⟩, the other qubit is instantly determined to be |0⟩ as well, no matter how far apart the two qubits are. The other Bell states, |Φ⁻⟩, |Ψ⁺⟩, and |Ψ⁻⟩, encode anti-correlations or phase relations that are equally non-classical.

The EPR argument proceeded as follows. Suppose two particles are prepared in an entangled state and then separated by a large distance. An observer, Alice, measures the position of particle 1 with arbitrary precision. Because of the entanglement, she can then predict with certainty the position of particle 2 without disturbing it in any way. Alternatively, Alice could have chosen to measure the momentum of particle 1, allowing her to predict the momentum of particle 2 with certainty. EPR concluded that since the outcome can be predicted without any disturbance to particle 2, and since the state of particle 2 could not have been altered by Alice's choice of measurement on particle 1 (otherwise information would travel faster than light), both the position and momentum of particle 2 must have definite values simultaneously — in contradiction with the uncertainty principle. EPR therefore concluded that quantum mechanics is incomplete, and that a more complete theory with "hidden variables" must exist.

John Bell's 1964 theorem fundamentally changed this debate. Bell showed that any local hidden-variable theory — one in which measurement outcomes are determined by pre-existing properties of the particles and no influence can travel faster than light — must satisfy a set of inequalities now called Bell inequalities. Quantum mechanics, he proved, predicts correlations that violate these inequalities for certain entangled states and certain measurement settings. This was not merely a philosophical argument; it was a concrete, testable prediction distinguishing quantum mechanics from any local realistic theory.

The first experimental tests of Bell inequalities were performed by Stuart Freedman and John Clauser in 1972 and by Alain Aspect and collaborators in a series of pivotal experiments in 1981-82. Aspect's experiments used entangled photons produced by atomic cascade and measured their polarization correlations at two well-separated detectors. The results violated the Bell-CHSH inequality (a generalized form of Bell's original inequality derived by Clauser, Horne, Shimony, and Holt) by many standard deviations and agreed with quantum mechanical predictions. Subsequent loophole-free Bell tests, closing both the detection loophole and the locality loophole simultaneously, were performed in 2015 by groups in Delft, Vienna, and Boulder. These experiments conclusively ruled out local hidden-variable theories as descriptions of nature. John Clauser, Alain Aspect, and Anton Zeilinger were awarded the 2022 Nobel Prize in Physics for their experimental work on entanglement and Bell inequality tests.

The non-local nature of entanglement does not, however, permit faster-than-light communication. Alice's measurement result is random, and she has no control over which outcome she obtains. Bob, at a distant location, also obtains a random result when he measures his particle. Only when Alice and Bob compare their results through a classical channel do they discover the correlation. The quantum correlations are therefore non-local in the sense that they cannot be explained by local hidden variables, but they are non-signaling: they cannot be used to transmit information superluminally. This is ensured by the no-communication theorem, a rigorous result in quantum information theory.

Entanglement plays a central role in quantum information and quantum technology. Quantum teleportation, proposed by Bennett, Brassard, Crépeau, Jozsa, Peres, and Wootters in 1993 and first demonstrated experimentally shortly thereafter, uses entanglement as a resource to transfer the complete quantum state of a particle from one location to another without physically transmitting the particle itself. The process requires both a shared entangled pair and the transmission of two classical bits, and it does not clone the original state (consistent with the no-cloning theorem). Quantum key distribution protocols such as Ekert's E91 protocol use entanglement to generate cryptographic keys with security guaranteed by the laws of physics — any eavesdropper would disturb the entanglement and be detectable.

In quantum computing, entanglement is an essential ingredient for many quantum algorithms, though its precise role in computational speedup remains a subject of active research. Entangled states enable superdense coding, in which two classical bits of information can be transmitted using a single qubit, given prior sharing of an entangled pair. Entanglement also underlies the power of quantum error correction: logical qubits are encoded across multiple physical qubits in highly entangled states that protect against local errors while preserving quantum information.

The quantification of entanglement is itself a rich field. For pure bipartite states, entanglement is measured by the entropy of entanglement, equal to the von Neumann entropy of the reduced density matrix of either subsystem. For mixed states, multiple inequivalent entanglement measures exist, including entanglement of formation, distillable entanglement, entanglement cost, and logarithmic negativity. Multipartite entanglement — entanglement among three or more particles — is even richer and less fully characterized, with several inequivalent classes of entanglement that cannot be converted into one another by local operations and classical communication. Greenberger-Horne-Zeilinger (GHZ) states and W states are canonical examples of inequivalent three-party entangled states.
