The brain confronts any student of emergence with what may be its hardest and most consequential case. Here is a system composed of roughly eighty-six billion neurons, each a biochemically complex cell capable of firing electrochemical pulses — action potentials — at rates from nearly silent to a few hundred per second. Each neuron connects to thousands of others through synapses whose strengths encode, in some fashion, the history of the organism's experiences. From this dense web of spiking cells arises the whole of mental life: perception, memory, language, reasoning, emotion, and the subjective quality of conscious experience. The emergence of mind from neurons is simultaneously the most familiar and the most mysterious fact about the natural world.

Connectionism, the theoretical framework that treats cognitive processes as patterns of activation distributed across networks of simple processing units, emerged as a scientific research program in the 1980s and provided the first computational models of how neural-like systems could give rise to higher-level cognitive properties. The seminal Parallel Distributed Processing volumes of Rumelhart and McClelland demonstrated that networks of simple threshold units, trained by the backpropagation algorithm, could learn to perform tasks — reading words aloud, learning past tenses, recognizing visual patterns — in ways that exhibited the graceful degradation, prototype effects, and context sensitivity characteristic of human cognition. These properties were not programmed into the network but emerged from learning: the network's internal representations, encoded in the pattern of synaptic weights, spontaneously developed structure appropriate to the task.

The internal representations learned by neural networks are among the most striking examples of emergent structure in artificial systems. When a deep convolutional neural network is trained to classify images, the successive layers of the network develop increasingly abstract representations: the first layer detects edges and gratings, the second layer detects textures and simple shapes, deeper layers detect object parts, and the final layers represent complex categorical abstractions. These representations were not specified by the network's designers; they emerged from the optimization process acting on the raw statistics of the training data. The hierarchy of representations mirrors, in a suggestive way, the hierarchical processing of visual information in the primate visual cortex, from V1 through V4, IT, and PFC. Whether this similarity is superficial or reflects deep principles of efficient visual processing is an active research question.

The relationship between neural activity and conscious experience is the deepest puzzle in the science of mind, and it is precisely where emergence becomes most philosophically loaded. Francis Crick and Christof Koch proposed the concept of neural correlates of consciousness — the minimal neural events jointly sufficient for a given conscious perception — as a scientifically tractable entry point into this problem. The approach sidesteps the metaphysical question of why any physical process should be accompanied by subjective experience and focuses instead on identifying which neural processes are associated with conscious states. Decades of research using functional neuroimaging, single-unit recording, and transcranial magnetic stimulation have identified candidate neural correlates: the ignition of a frontoparietal network in global workspace theory, the information integration across thalamocortical loops in Tononi's integrated information theory, and the resonant activity between perceptual and higher-order areas in higher-order theories.

Tononi's Integrated Information Theory (IIT) attempts to provide a mathematical account of what consciousness is and why it is associated with particular physical substrates. IIT proposes that consciousness is identical to integrated information — denoted Phi — a measure of the extent to which a system generates information as a whole above and beyond the information generated by its parts. The theory predicts that consciousness requires a substrate with high internal integration and irreducibility, explaining why the cerebral cortex supports consciousness while the cerebellum, which has a similar number of neurons, appears to contribute little to conscious experience. IIT has attracted both admiration for its mathematical rigor and criticism for its counterintuitive predictions: certain simple computer architectures could, in principle, have high Phi, while certain biological structures might have low Phi despite their apparent complexity.

Global Workspace Theory, developed by Bernard Baars and extended computationally by Dehaene, offers a different account of consciousness as an emergent property of a broadcasting architecture. In this view, the brain contains many specialized unconscious processors operating in parallel, and conscious experience arises when information is broadcast widely across a global workspace — a distributed set of neurons with long-range connections that allow information to be made available to a wide variety of cognitive systems. The theory predicts that consciousness is associated with the ignition of a frontoparietal network and a characteristic pattern of late, widespread neural activity — the "P3" component of the event-related potential — that occurs several hundred milliseconds after stimulus onset. This framework is more amenable to experimental test than IIT and has accumulated substantial empirical support.

The emergence of language from the neural substrate presents a separate but related set of puzzles. Language involves a hierarchical, recursive combinatorial system for encoding and communicating thoughts, and it is associated with left-lateralized perisylvian cortical areas — Broca's area and Wernicke's area — whose damage produces characteristic aphasia syndromes. Yet no part of the brain seems to be dedicated exclusively to language; linguistic processing draws on a broad network that overlaps with regions involved in working memory, attention, motor planning, and social cognition. The emergence of linguistic competence during development, and presumably during evolution, involved the co-option and reorganization of multiple pre-existing systems, suggesting that language is itself an emergent phenomenon at the evolutionary and developmental levels as well as at the level of neural computation.

Recent advances in large-scale neural recording, brain organoids, and deep learning have created new opportunities and new puzzles for the science of neural emergence. Recording thousands of neurons simultaneously has revealed that much of the brain's information is encoded in the geometry of high-dimensional population activity patterns — trajectories through a neural state space — rather than in the firing rates of individual cells. Brain organoids, three-dimensional tissue cultures derived from pluripotent stem cells, have been observed to develop oscillatory electrical activity and rudimentary sensory responses, raising profound questions about what neural complexity suffices for the emergence of experience. And the success of large language models in producing humanlike text has forced researchers to grapple anew with the question of what cognitive properties might emerge from scale and training alone, without explicit cognitive architecture.
