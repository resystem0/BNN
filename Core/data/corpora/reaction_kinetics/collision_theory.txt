Collision theory provides the simplest molecular-level model for the rates of bimolecular gas-phase reactions. The fundamental premise is that a reaction can occur only when two reactant molecules collide, and that not all collisions lead to reaction — only those in which the colliding molecules have sufficient kinetic energy and are oriented correctly relative to each other. The rate of reaction is therefore the product of the rate of collisions and the fraction of those collisions that are reactive. This seemingly simple idea leads to an expression for the rate constant that agrees qualitatively with experiment and introduces the physical parameters that all subsequent, more refined theories must also account for.

The collision frequency, the number of collisions per unit volume per unit time between molecules of type A and molecules of type B, can be calculated from the kinetic molecular theory of gases. For two distinct molecular species with diameters d_A and d_B, molecular masses m_A and m_B, and number densities n_A and n_B, the collision frequency per unit volume is Z_AB = n_A n_B sigma_AB (8 kB T / pi mu)^(1/2), where sigma_AB is the collision cross section, equal to pi times the square of the average collision diameter (d_A + d_B)/2, and mu is the reduced mass m_A m_B/(m_A + m_B). The factor in parentheses is the mean relative speed of the colliding molecules. At room temperature and atmospheric pressure, collision frequencies between typical gas molecules are on the order of 10 to the 28th collisions per cubic meter per second, an enormous number.

If every collision led to reaction, the resulting reaction rate would be many orders of magnitude faster than any observed chemical reaction. The first correction applied in collision theory is to require that the collision provides enough kinetic energy along the line of centers — the line connecting the centers of the two colliding molecules — to overcome the activation energy. Only those collisions with a relative kinetic energy along the line of centers exceeding the activation energy Ea can proceed to products. The fraction of collisions satisfying this criterion is given by the Boltzmann factor exp(-Ea/RT), the same factor appearing in the Arrhenius equation. Multiplying the total collision frequency by this Boltzmann factor gives a predicted rate constant of k = N_A sigma_AB (8 kB T / pi mu)^(1/2) exp(-Ea/RT), where N_A is Avogadro's number.

This expression is the collision theory rate constant, and comparison with the Arrhenius equation k = A exp(-Ea/RT) identifies the pre-exponential factor A with N_A sigma_AB (8 kB T / pi mu)^(1/2), sometimes written as Z_0, the collision frequency per unit concentration of each reactant. The temperature dependence of the pre-exponential factor is weak, varying as the square root of T, compared to the strong exponential temperature dependence of the Boltzmann factor for any reaction with a significant activation energy. This is why the Arrhenius equation accurately describes the temperature dependence of most reactions over moderate temperature ranges: the slowly varying pre-exponential factor contributes only a small correction to the dominant exponential.

The steric factor p is introduced when experimental pre-exponential factors are compared with the values predicted by collision theory. For many reactions, the experimental A factor is smaller than the collision theory prediction by a factor ranging from about 10 to the minus 1 for favorable cases to 10 to the minus 9 for highly constrained reactions. This discrepancy is attributed to steric requirements: the colliding molecules must be oriented correctly at the moment of collision for the reaction to proceed. The steric factor is defined as the ratio of the experimental A factor to the theoretical collision frequency, p = A_exp / Z_0, and it is a dimensionless number between 0 and 1 that captures the probability of the correct orientation. Reactions requiring precise end-to-end alignment or involving large, complex molecules with only a small reactive face have small steric factors.

The Arrhenius equation is the central empirical expression connecting temperature to reaction rate and is ubiquitous in chemical kinetics. Svante Arrhenius proposed it in 1889 based on the observation that the logarithm of the rate constant varies linearly with the reciprocal of temperature. The equation is k = A exp(-Ea/RT), where A is the pre-exponential or frequency factor, Ea is the activation energy, R is the gas constant, and T is the absolute temperature. Taking the natural logarithm gives ln k = ln A - Ea/(RT), which predicts a linear relationship between ln k and 1/T with slope -Ea/R and intercept ln A. This Arrhenius plot is the standard method of determining activation energies experimentally.

The physical interpretation of the Arrhenius equation is that reactant molecules must acquire the activation energy Ea before they can cross the energy barrier separating reactants from products. The fraction of molecules with energy exceeding Ea at temperature T is given by the Boltzmann factor exp(-Ea/RT). As temperature increases, this fraction increases exponentially, so the rate increases strongly. A rule of thumb often cited is that the rate of many biological and chemical reactions roughly doubles for every 10 degree Celsius increase in temperature near room temperature; this corresponds to an activation energy of approximately 50 to 60 kilojoules per mole. The precise Q10 temperature coefficient varies widely with the activation energy, and reactions with very low activation energies show much weaker temperature sensitivity.

Deviations from simple Arrhenius behavior reveal important mechanistic information. If the reaction proceeds through two parallel pathways with different activation energies, the overall rate constant is the sum of two Arrhenius terms, and the Arrhenius plot will be curved with the high-activation-energy pathway dominating at high temperature and the low-activation-energy pathway dominating at low temperature. If the reaction mechanism changes with temperature, as sometimes occurs when a high-temperature concerted pathway gives way to a lower-temperature stepwise mechanism, the Arrhenius plot will show a break. Curved Arrhenius plots at low temperature can also indicate quantum mechanical tunneling, where the reaction bypasses the barrier entirely and the apparent activation energy is lower than the classical barrier height.
