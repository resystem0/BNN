The quantitative study of reaction rates begins with the rate law, a mathematical expression that relates the instantaneous rate of a chemical reaction to the concentrations of reactants raised to certain powers. The rate law cannot be deduced from the stoichiometric equation alone; it must be determined experimentally, because the macroscopic stoichiometry need not reflect the molecular-level mechanism by which the reaction occurs. The rate constant, appearing as the proportionality factor in the rate law, encapsulates the temperature dependence of the rate and the effect of any catalysts, while the exponents on the concentration terms — the reaction orders — reflect the mechanism.

The overall reaction order is the sum of all the individual concentration exponents in the rate law. A first-order reaction has a rate proportional to the concentration of a single reactant raised to the first power. A second-order reaction has a rate proportional either to the concentration of one reactant squared or to the product of the concentrations of two different reactants, each raised to the first power. Zero-order reactions proceed at a rate independent of reactant concentration, which occurs when the reaction rate is limited by some factor other than the availability of the reactant itself, such as the availability of a catalytic surface.

The integrated rate law is obtained by solving the differential rate equation to express concentration as a function of time, and it provides the most direct connection between kinetic theory and experimental measurement. For a first-order reaction with rate law d[A]/dt = -k[A], integration yields ln[A] = ln[A]0 - kt, or equivalently [A] = [A]0 exp(-kt). This exponential decay means that the concentration of A decreases by the same fraction in every successive equal time interval. The half-life of a first-order reaction, the time required for the concentration to fall to half its initial value, is a constant given by t1/2 = ln(2)/k = 0.693/k, independent of the initial concentration. Radioactive decay is a first-order process, and this independence of half-life from initial concentration is why radiometric dating is possible.

For a second-order reaction in which the rate law is d[A]/dt = -k[A]2, integration gives 1/[A] = 1/[A]0 + kt. The half-life for this case is t1/2 = 1/(k[A]0), which depends on the initial concentration: higher initial concentrations lead to shorter half-lives. This contrasts fundamentally with the first-order case. For a zero-order reaction with rate -k, integration gives [A] = [A]0 - kt, a linear decrease in concentration with time until the reactant is exhausted. The half-life is [A]0/(2k), also dependent on initial concentration.

Determining the rate law experimentally requires measuring how the initial rate changes as the initial concentration of each reactant is varied systematically, keeping other concentrations constant. In the method of initial rates, experiments are designed so that the early-time rate, before significant product accumulation or reactant depletion, can be measured for different starting concentrations. If doubling the concentration of reactant A doubles the initial rate, the reaction is first order in A. If doubling the concentration of A quadruples the rate, it is second order in A. If doubling the concentration of A leaves the rate unchanged, it is zero order in A. These relationships allow the reaction order with respect to each reactant to be determined independently.

The isolation method is an alternative experimental strategy in which all reactants except one are present in large excess. Under these conditions, the concentrations of the excess reactants remain essentially constant throughout the reaction — the pseudo-first-order approximation — and the measured rate reflects only the dependence on the one reactant present at lower concentration. The apparent rate constant measured under these conditions is a product of the true rate constant and the concentrations of all the reactants held in excess. By conducting experiments that isolate each reactant in turn, all the individual reaction orders can be determined.

The rate constant k carries units that depend on the overall reaction order, a fact that must be tracked carefully in calculations. For a first-order reaction, k has units of inverse time, typically inverse seconds. For a second-order reaction, k has units of inverse concentration times inverse time, such as liters per mole per second. For zero-order reactions, k has units of concentration per time, such as moles per liter per second. These unit requirements provide a useful consistency check when analyzing kinetic data.

The relationship between the rate law and the elementary steps of the reaction mechanism is central to mechanistic analysis. When a reaction proceeds through several elementary steps, the overall rate law is determined primarily by the slowest step, the rate-determining step. If the rate-determining step is preceded by one or more fast equilibria that establish steady-state concentrations of reactive intermediates, the rate law can be derived by expressing the concentration of intermediates in terms of reactant concentrations using the equilibrium constant expressions for the fast steps. The steady-state approximation provides an alternative approach: it assumes that the concentration of each reactive intermediate reaches a constant value quickly and that the rate of formation equals the rate of consumption, allowing the intermediate concentration to be eliminated algebraically from the rate expression.
