Diffusion is the spontaneous net movement of molecules or particles from regions of higher concentration to regions of lower concentration, driven by the thermodynamic tendency to maximize entropy and homogenize concentration gradients. In the context of chemical kinetics, diffusion becomes the rate-limiting factor when the intrinsic chemical reaction is so fast that the overall rate is governed not by the chemical transformation itself but by how quickly reactant molecules can find each other through random thermal motion. Understanding diffusion and mass transport is essential for analyzing reactions in solution, at interfaces, and within porous catalysts, where geometric and physical constraints slow the delivery of reactants to active sites.

Fick's first law quantitatively relates the diffusive flux, the number of molecules passing through a unit area per unit time, to the concentration gradient. In one dimension, the flux J is equal to minus D times the spatial derivative of concentration with respect to position, J = -D (dc/dx), where D is the diffusion coefficient and the negative sign indicates that flux is directed from high to low concentration. The diffusion coefficient D has units of area per time, typically square meters per second or square centimeters per second, and depends on the size and shape of the diffusing species, the viscosity of the medium, and the temperature. In dilute aqueous solution at room temperature, small molecules like oxygen and glucose have diffusion coefficients on the order of 10 to the minus 9 square meters per second.

Fick's second law describes the time evolution of concentration profiles. It states that the rate of change of concentration at a point is equal to D times the second spatial derivative of concentration: dc/dt = D (d2c/dx2). This partial differential equation, the diffusion equation, has solutions for various boundary and initial conditions that describe how concentration profiles spread and relax toward uniformity. For an initial point source of N particles at the origin, the concentration at position x and time t is a Gaussian function: c(x,t) = N/(4 pi D t)^(1/2) exp(-x2/(4Dt)). The width of this Gaussian, measured by its standard deviation, grows as the square root of time, reflecting the characteristic diffusive spreading behavior. The mean squared displacement of a diffusing particle is related to time by <x2> = 2Dt in one dimension, or 6Dt in three dimensions.

The diffusion coefficient can be related to molecular properties through the Stokes-Einstein equation, derived by combining Einstein's analysis of Brownian motion with the hydrodynamic result for the drag on a spherical particle. The equation D = kBT/(6 pi eta r) states that the diffusion coefficient is directly proportional to the thermal energy kBT and inversely proportional to the dynamic viscosity of the solvent eta and the hydrodynamic radius of the diffusing particle r. This equation correctly predicts that larger molecules diffuse more slowly and that diffusion speeds up at higher temperatures and in less viscous solvents. It also shows why diffusion in biological membranes, which are far more viscous than bulk water, is much slower than in the cytoplasm.

Diffusion-limited reactions occur when the rate of chemical reaction between reactants once they have met is much faster than the rate at which they diffuse together. In this regime, every encounter between reactants leads to reaction, and the overall rate is governed entirely by the encounter rate. Smoluchowski developed the theory of diffusion-limited reaction rates in 1917, showing that the rate constant for a diffusion-limited reaction between two spherical reactants is k = 4 pi N_A D_AB r_AB, where D_AB is the sum of the diffusion coefficients of the two reactants and r_AB is the reaction distance, typically close to the sum of their radii. For typical small molecules in water, this formula predicts rate constants of around 10 to the 9th to 10 to the 10th liters per mole per second, consistent with measured rate constants for proton transfer reactions and radical recombinations.

In heterogeneous catalysis, the effectiveness of a porous catalyst depends on how readily reactants can diffuse from the bulk fluid into the interior pores where active sites are located, and how quickly products can diffuse back out. When the reaction is fast relative to diffusion, reactants are consumed near the outer surface of the catalyst particle and the interior active sites are underutilized. This regime is called internal diffusion limitation. The Thiele modulus, a dimensionless parameter defined as the ratio of the characteristic reaction rate to the characteristic diffusion rate within the particle, determines whether a catalyst operates in the kinetic regime, where all active sites are efficiently used, or the diffusion-limited regime. Large Thiele moduli indicate diffusion limitation; the effectiveness factor, the ratio of observed reaction rate to the rate that would be achieved if the entire internal surface were exposed to bulk reactant concentrations, falls below one and can be much less.

Membrane diffusion is central to biological function. Nutrients, gases, and signaling molecules must cross cell membranes to enter or exit cells, and the rate of this transport determines how rapidly cells can respond to environmental changes and sustain metabolic activity. Small nonpolar molecules like oxygen, carbon dioxide, and steroids can dissolve in the lipid bilayer and diffuse across without the need for specific transport proteins. The flux across the membrane follows Fick's law with the permeability coefficient replacing the diffusion coefficient, and the permeability depends on both the partition coefficient of the molecule between the aqueous phase and the lipid phase and the diffusion coefficient within the bilayer. Larger or charged molecules require facilitated diffusion through protein channels or active transport mechanisms that use metabolic energy.

Turbulent mixing and convection in macroscopic reaction systems introduce mass transport by mechanisms far more efficient than molecular diffusion. In well-stirred reactors, turbulent eddies transport reactants over large distances rapidly, and the concentration gradients within the well-mixed zone are negligible. However, even in stirred systems, a thin stagnant boundary layer persists at catalyst surfaces, electrode surfaces, or phase boundaries through which molecular diffusion remains the only transport mechanism. The boundary layer thickness and the concentration gradient across it determine the flux of reactant to the surface and can limit the overall reaction rate if the surface reaction is fast. Engineering design of reactors must account for this interplay between turbulent convection in the bulk and molecular diffusion in the boundary layer to achieve the desired productivity and selectivity.
