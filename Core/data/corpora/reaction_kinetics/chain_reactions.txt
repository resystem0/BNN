Chain reactions are a class of chemical processes distinguished by the participation of reactive intermediates — typically free radicals or ions — that are consumed in one step and regenerated in subsequent steps, allowing a single initiation event to drive the conversion of many reactant molecules to products. The propagating intermediate is the chain carrier, and its repeated recycling through the propagation cycle amplifies the chemical change far beyond what a single initiation event would accomplish. Chain reactions account for the combustion of hydrocarbons, the polymerization of synthetic polymers, the photochemical reactions in the atmosphere, and under certain conditions, runaway explosive processes.

The chain mechanism is divided into three conceptually distinct phases. Initiation is the step or steps in which chain carriers are first generated from stable molecules, typically by thermal activation, photolysis, or the addition of a small amount of initiator. This step is slow because it requires breaking strong bonds or overcoming a significant activation barrier, but it need only occur once for each chain to begin. Propagation comprises the steps in which the chain carrier reacts with a reactant molecule to form product and regenerate the chain carrier, or a different chain carrier that continues the cycle. Each propagation step individually is fast, and the sequence can repeat many thousands of times before termination. Termination is the step or steps in which chain carriers are consumed without being regenerated, removing them from the cycle and ending the chain.

The chlorination of hydrogen gas provides a clean example that illustrates the key features. Initiation occurs when molecular chlorine is photolyzed by ultraviolet light or thermally dissociated to produce two chlorine atoms: Cl2 plus energy gives two Cl. Each chlorine atom is a chain carrier with an unpaired electron — a radical — that is highly reactive toward hydrogen. In the first propagation step, the chlorine atom abstracts a hydrogen atom from H2 to form HCl and a hydrogen radical: Cl + H2 gives HCl + H. In the second propagation step, the hydrogen radical attacks molecular chlorine to form another HCl molecule and regenerate a chlorine atom: H + Cl2 gives HCl + Cl. These two propagation steps cycle repeatedly, each cycle producing two molecules of HCl while consuming one H2 and one Cl2. Termination occurs when two radicals meet and recombine: Cl + Cl gives Cl2, or H + H gives H2, or H + Cl gives HCl.

The chain length is defined as the number of propagation cycles completed per initiation event, and it determines the amplification factor of the chain process. Long chain lengths mean that a small amount of initiation leads to extensive reaction. Chain lengths in gas-phase reactions can range from tens for poorly propagating chains to millions for highly efficient ones. The chain length is kinetically determined by the relative rates of propagation and termination: anything that speeds propagation relative to termination lengthens the chain and vice versa. Chain inhibitors, also called retarders, work by reacting with chain carriers to form less reactive species, shortening the chain and slowing the overall reaction. This is the mechanism by which antioxidants protect against oxidative degradation.

Free radical polymerization is a chain reaction of enormous industrial importance. Initiation generates a radical initiator fragment, typically by thermal decomposition of a peroxide or azo compound, that adds to a monomer molecule. The resulting radical adds to another monomer, and so on through thousands of addition steps, each one extending the growing polymer chain by one monomer unit. Termination occurs by combination of two growing chains or by disproportionation in which one chain transfers a hydrogen atom to the other, producing one saturated and one unsaturated chain end. The average chain length, and therefore the average degree of polymerization and molecular weight of the product, depends on the ratio of propagation to termination rates. High monomer concentrations and low initiator concentrations favor long chains and high molecular weights.

Branching chain reactions introduce additional steps in which a single chain carrier generates more than one chain carrier, causing the chain carrier population to grow exponentially rather than remaining steady. When branching occurs faster than termination, the chain carrier concentration increases without bound, the reaction rate accelerates uncontrollably, and the system can explode. The hydrogen-oxygen reaction at elevated temperatures exhibits this behavior. Above a certain critical pressure at each temperature — the explosion limit — chain branching overcomes termination, the reaction becomes self-accelerating, and explosion follows. Below the limit, termination keeps pace with branching and a steady, controlled reaction occurs. The existence of these explosion limits and their pressure dependence were key experimental observations that motivated the development of chain reaction theory by Semenov and Hinshelwood.

Tropospheric chemistry involves chain reactions of enormous environmental consequence. In polluted urban air, nitrogen oxides produced by vehicle engines participate in a chain reaction with hydrocarbons and sunlight to produce ozone and photochemical smog. The hydroxyl radical, OH, is the primary chain carrier in atmospheric oxidation, initiating the degradation of almost all volatile organic compounds. OH is generated photochemically from ozone and then reacts with hydrocarbon fuels in propagation steps that regenerate peroxy radicals, which in turn regenerate OH, cycling the chain. The lifetime of atmospheric methane, a potent greenhouse gas, is determined almost entirely by its rate of reaction with the OH radical, and any perturbation to the atmospheric OH concentration has cascading effects on the concentrations of all species that OH oxidizes.

Radical inhibitors and scavengers work by accepting radical chain carriers and converting them to stable molecules or to radicals too unreactive to continue the chain. Phenolic antioxidants like vitamin E work this way in biological systems: the phenolic OH group donates a hydrogen atom to a chain-carrying peroxyl radical, forming a more stable phenoxy radical that does not propagate the lipid peroxidation chain. In the food industry, synthetic antioxidants like BHT serve the same function, intercepting chain carriers in the oxidative degradation of fats. In polymer chemistry, inhibitors and retarders are used to control the rate of polymerization and to prevent premature gelation of monomers during storage and transport.
