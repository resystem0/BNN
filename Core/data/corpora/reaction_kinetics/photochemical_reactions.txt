Photochemical reactions are chemical transformations initiated by the absorption of electromagnetic radiation, typically in the ultraviolet or visible range of the spectrum. They are fundamentally different from thermal reactions in that the energy input does not come from the thermal kinetic energy of molecular collisions but from the quantized absorption of photons by specific molecular chromophores. This distinction has profound consequences for the chemistry that is possible: photochemical reactions can access excited electronic states that are thermally unavailable at reasonable temperatures, enabling transformations with no thermal counterpart and allowing energy-selective excitation of specific molecular components.

The Jablonski diagram is the standard schematic for depicting the electronic and vibrational states of a molecule and the transitions between them following photon absorption. The horizontal lines represent vibrational levels within each electronic state, and the electronic states are arranged vertically in order of energy. The ground state S0 is at the bottom, followed by the first singlet excited state S1, the second singlet excited state S2, and the triplet states T1 and T2. Absorption of a photon promotes the molecule from S0 to a vibrational level of S1 or S2. Internal conversion is the rapid, radiationless relaxation between electronic states of the same spin multiplicity; from S2, internal conversion to S1 is typically complete within picoseconds. Vibrational relaxation within S1 similarly brings the molecule to the lowest vibrational level of S1 within picoseconds.

From the lowest vibrational level of S1, several pathways compete. Fluorescence is radiative decay back to S0, with emission of a photon whose energy is lower than the absorbed photon due to vibrational energy losses — the Stokes shift. Intersystem crossing is the spin-forbidden but nonetheless observable transition from the singlet state S1 to the triplet state T1, facilitated by spin-orbit coupling. The triplet state has a much longer lifetime than the singlet state, because radiative decay from T1 to S0, called phosphorescence, is also spin-forbidden. Internal conversion from S1 to S0 is the radiationless alternative to fluorescence, depositing the electronic energy as heat. Finally, photochemistry can occur from either S1 or T1 if a reactive pathway is available.

Quantum yield is the efficiency parameter that relates photochemical outcome to photon absorption. The quantum yield of a photochemical product is defined as the number of molecules of product formed divided by the number of photons absorbed. A quantum yield of 1 means that every absorbed photon leads to formation of one product molecule. Quantum yields can exceed 1 in chain reactions where a single absorbed photon initiates a sequence of dark reactions, as in the photochlorination of hydrocarbons where a quantum yield of 10 to the 4th or more is observed. Quantum yields can also be less than 1 when competing photophysical processes such as fluorescence and internal conversion depopulate the excited state before chemistry can occur.

The Grotthuss-Draper law states that only light that is absorbed by a substance can cause photochemical change, and the Beer-Lambert law quantifies the extent of absorption as a function of concentration and path length: log(I0/I) = epsilon c l, where epsilon is the molar absorption coefficient at the given wavelength, c is the concentration of the absorber, and l is the path length of light through the sample. This law is the quantitative foundation of spectrophotometry. The wavelength-dependent molar absorption coefficient reflects the electronic structure of the molecule and can be computed from first principles using quantum chemistry, providing a connection between molecular orbital theory and macroscopic photochemistry.

Photosensitization is a technique in which the photoactive molecule, the sensitizer, absorbs the incident light and transfers energy to a second species, the acceptor, which then undergoes the photochemical reaction. Photosensitization is useful when the desired acceptor molecule does not absorb light efficiently at the available wavelengths, or when direct photolysis of the acceptor leads to unwanted side reactions. The sensitizer is typically chosen to be an efficient absorber with a long-lived triplet state, since energy transfer to the acceptor occurs most efficiently from the triplet manifold via the Dexter or Förster mechanisms. Rose bengal, methylene blue, and a variety of porphyrins are commonly used sensitizers. Singlet oxygen generation, essential in photodynamic therapy for cancer treatment, relies on sensitized energy transfer from the triplet state of the sensitizer to ground-state triplet oxygen, producing the reactive singlet oxygen that destroys nearby biomolecules.

Photoisomerization reactions are among the most biologically significant photochemical processes. The vision process in vertebrates depends on the photoisomerization of 11-cis-retinal to all-trans-retinal upon absorption of a visible-light photon within the rhodopsin protein. This isomerization occurs within 200 femtoseconds, among the fastest chemical reactions known, and triggers a conformational change in the protein that ultimately generates a nerve impulse. The quantum yield of this isomerization is approximately 0.67, meaning that two thirds of absorbed photons lead to the isomerization product. The remarkable speed and efficiency of this reaction reflect the careful molecular engineering of the protein environment, which shapes the excited-state potential energy surface to funnel the molecule toward the product geometry.

Photosynthesis harnesses the interaction of light with chlorophyll and accessory pigments to drive the thermodynamically uphill reduction of carbon dioxide to carbohydrates. The primary photochemical events in both photosystem I and photosystem II involve ultrafast electron transfer from a photoexcited chlorophyll dimer to a nearby acceptor molecule, occurring within picoseconds and with near-unit quantum efficiency. The speed of this charge separation is essential to prevent wasteful back-reaction. The subsequent electron transfer events are slower but still highly efficient, and the energy conserved in the form of the transmembrane proton gradient and the reduced cofactors NADPH is ultimately used to fix carbon dioxide. The quantum yield for carbon fixation in photosynthesis under ideal conditions, approximately 0.125 moles of CO2 fixed per mole of photons absorbed, reflects the requirement of eight photons to drive the full set of reactions.
