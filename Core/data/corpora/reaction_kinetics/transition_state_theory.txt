Transition state theory, also known as activated complex theory, is the theoretical framework that provides a molecular-level interpretation of the temperature dependence of reaction rates and a quantitative basis for calculating rate constants from molecular properties. Developed independently by Eyring, Evans, and Polanyi in 1935, the theory postulates that reactants are in quasi-equilibrium with a species at the top of the energy barrier along the reaction coordinate, called the transition state or activated complex, and that the rate of the reaction is determined by the rate at which this species crosses over the energy maximum to form products.

The reaction coordinate is a collective variable that parameterizes the progress of the reaction from reactants to products. For a simple bond-breaking and bond-forming reaction such as the bimolecular hydrogen transfer A + B-C going to A-B + C, the reaction coordinate can be thought of as the antisymmetric stretch of the three-atom system, tracking the breaking B-C distance and the forming A-B distance simultaneously. Along this coordinate, the potential energy surface, which is the Born-Oppenheimer surface giving the electronic energy as a function of all nuclear positions, has a saddle point geometry corresponding to the transition state. The energy at this saddle point relative to the energy of the reactants is the activation energy, Ea, of the forward reaction. The energy at the saddle point relative to the products is the activation energy of the reverse reaction.

The empirical Arrhenius equation, k = A exp(-Ea/RT), expresses the temperature dependence of the rate constant in terms of the activation energy and a pre-exponential factor A. This equation, proposed by Arrhenius in 1889 before its theoretical justification was available, captures the observation that most reaction rates increase exponentially with temperature. A plot of the natural logarithm of k against the inverse of absolute temperature, called an Arrhenius plot, yields a straight line with slope -Ea/R and intercept ln(A), allowing both the activation energy and the pre-exponential factor to be determined from experimental rate constant measurements at a series of temperatures.

Transition state theory provides the theoretical basis for the Arrhenius equation through the Eyring equation. The Eyring equation expresses the rate constant as k = (kB T/h) exp(-DeltaG‡/RT), where kB is the Boltzmann constant, h is Planck's constant, and DeltaG‡ is the Gibbs energy of activation, the standard Gibbs energy difference between the transition state and the reactants. The factor kB T/h, approximately 6.25 times 10 to the 12th per second at 298 K, represents the universal frequency at which an activated complex that has been thermally assembled at the transition state passes over the barrier. The exponential factor exp(-DeltaG‡/RT) represents the Boltzmann probability that a given collision has sufficient energy and proper orientation to form the activated complex.

The Gibbs energy of activation can be decomposed into enthalpic and entropic contributions: DeltaG‡ = DeltaH‡ - T DeltaS‡. The enthalpy of activation DeltaH‡ is closely related to the Arrhenius activation energy; for reactions in solution at constant pressure, the two are related by DeltaH‡ = Ea - RT. The entropy of activation DeltaS‡ determines the pre-exponential factor and reflects the change in the number and freedom of degrees of freedom as the reactants come together to form the transition state. Unimolecular reactions typically have near-zero entropies of activation. Bimolecular reactions require two molecules to come together and form a transition state with restricted geometry, so they have negative entropies of activation. Highly organized transition states with strict geometric requirements, such as those for pericyclic reactions, have large negative entropies of activation that slow the reaction relative to what the enthalpy of activation alone would predict.

The potential energy surface itself can be computed using quantum chemical methods, and modern computational chemistry can locate transition states, compute their geometries and vibrational frequencies, and calculate activation energies with quantitative accuracy for reactions of moderate complexity. The geometry of the transition state can be probed experimentally through structure-activity relationships and linear free energy relationships such as the Hammett equation, which correlates the rates of reactions at substituted positions with substituent constants that reflect the electronic demands of the transition state. A positive Hammett rho value indicates that electron-withdrawing groups accelerate the reaction and that the transition state has developed negative charge relative to the reactants, while a negative rho indicates that the transition state is electron-deficient.

Kinetic isotope effects provide experimental access to the structure of the transition state at the atomic level. When a hydrogen atom involved in bond breaking or forming is replaced by its heavier isotope deuterium, the reaction rate decreases because the zero-point vibrational energy is lower for the heavier isotope, increasing the effective energy barrier. Primary kinetic isotope effects, observed when the isotopically substituted atom is directly transferred in the rate-determining step, typically give rate ratios kH/kD in the range of 2 to 7 at room temperature. Secondary kinetic isotope effects arise from isotopic substitution at an adjacent position and are smaller, typically 1.0 to 1.4. The observation of a large primary kinetic isotope effect is strong evidence that the bond to hydrogen is substantially broken in the transition state.

Quantum mechanical tunneling is a phenomenon in which the transferring atom does not need to reach the classical top of the energy barrier but passes through the barrier by virtue of its wave-like nature. Tunneling is most significant for light atoms, particularly hydrogen and its isotopes, and at low temperatures where classical barrier crossing is improbable. When tunneling contributes significantly, the Arrhenius plot becomes curved at low temperatures rather than linear, and the kinetic isotope effect can greatly exceed the classical limit. Tunneling is increasingly recognized as important in enzyme-catalyzed reactions, where the enzyme active site may be organized to bring donor and acceptor groups into close proximity, reducing the tunneling distance and dramatically enhancing the rate.
