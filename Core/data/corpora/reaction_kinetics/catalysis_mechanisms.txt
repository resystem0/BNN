Catalysis is the acceleration of a chemical reaction by a substance that participates in the reaction mechanism but is regenerated in its original form by the end of the catalytic cycle, so that it is not consumed overall. A catalyst lowers the activation energy of the reaction by providing an alternative pathway with a lower-energy transition state, increasing the rate constant and therefore the rate without affecting the equilibrium position. This last point is fundamental: a catalyst makes equilibrium faster to reach but cannot shift the equilibrium composition, because it must equally accelerate the forward and reverse reactions if it is to be regenerated without net chemical change.

Enzyme catalysis represents the most sophisticated form of catalysis found in nature and has been the subject of intense investigation since the nineteenth century. Enzymes are biological catalysts, typically proteins with precisely shaped active sites that bind substrate molecules with high specificity and position them optimally for chemical transformation. The Michaelis-Menten model, proposed in 1913, provides the foundational mathematical description of enzyme kinetics. In this model, the enzyme E binds reversibly to substrate S to form an enzyme-substrate complex ES, which then proceeds irreversibly to release product P and regenerate the free enzyme. The rate of product formation is given by v = Vmax [S]/(Km + [S]), where Vmax is the maximum rate achieved when all enzyme active sites are saturated with substrate, and Km, the Michaelis constant, is the substrate concentration at which the rate is half-maximal.

The Michaelis constant Km is a composite kinetic parameter equal to (k-1 + k2)/k1, where k1 and k-1 are the rate constants for ES formation and dissociation respectively, and k2 is the rate constant for the catalytic step. In the limiting case where k2 is much smaller than k-1, Km approximates the dissociation constant of the ES complex and is a rough measure of substrate affinity. Low Km values indicate tight binding; high Km values indicate that high substrate concentrations are needed to achieve half-maximal rate. The catalytic efficiency of an enzyme is measured by the quantity kcat/Km, where kcat equals Vmax divided by the total enzyme concentration. This ratio has a theoretical upper limit set by the rate of diffusion of substrate to the enzyme, approximately 10 to the 8th to 10 to the 9th liters per mole per second, and some highly evolved enzymes approach this limit.

Enzymes achieve their remarkable rate enhancements, which can reach factors of 10 to the 17th relative to the uncatalyzed reaction, through a combination of mechanisms. Proximity and orientation effects bring reactants together and position them correctly for reaction, reducing the entropy cost of forming the transition state. Electrostatic stabilization by charged or polar residues in the active site can preferentially stabilize the transition state relative to the ground state. Acid-base catalysis employs amino acid side chains as proton donors or acceptors to facilitate proton transfers that would otherwise be slow. Covalent catalysis forms a transient covalent bond between the enzyme and substrate, creating a new lower-energy pathway through a covalent intermediate. Metal ion catalysis uses bound metal ions to activate substrates, position reactive groups, or provide Lewis acid activation of leaving groups.

Heterogeneous catalysis involves a catalyst in a different phase from the reactants, most commonly a solid catalyst and gaseous or liquid reactants. The catalytic reaction occurs at the surface of the solid, and the overall rate depends on how rapidly reactants can reach and bind to active sites. The mechanism of heterogeneous catalysis typically involves several steps: adsorption of reactants onto the surface, surface diffusion to bring adsorbed species together, surface reaction between adsorbed intermediates, and desorption of products. The Langmuir-Hinshelwood mechanism assumes that both reactants adsorb onto the surface before reacting with each other. The Eley-Rideal mechanism involves reaction between an adsorbed species and a molecule arriving directly from the gas phase without prior adsorption.

The Haber-Bosch synthesis of ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen over an iron catalyst is the prototypical example of industrial heterogeneous catalysis and one of the most economically important chemical processes ever developed. The rate-limiting step is the dissociative chemisorption of nitrogen onto the iron surface, breaking the extremely strong nitrogen-nitrogen triple bond. The iron surface lowers the activation energy for this bond breaking by forming partial bonds to each nitrogen atom, stabilizing the dissociative transition state. Promoters such as potassium and aluminum oxide are added to the catalyst to enhance activity and prevent sintering of the iron particles at the high reaction temperature. The development of this process by Haber and Bosch allowed industrial-scale nitrogen fixation, making synthetic fertilizers available and fundamentally transforming global agriculture.

Acid-base catalysis in organic chemistry encompasses a vast range of reactions including ester hydrolysis, aldol reactions, and hemiacetal formation. In specific acid catalysis, the reaction rate depends on the hydrogen ion concentration and is accelerated by H3O+ but not by undissociated acids. In specific base catalysis, the rate depends on the hydroxide ion concentration. In general acid catalysis, the rate is enhanced by any proton donor, not just hydronium ion, and the catalytic effectiveness of different acids correlates with their acid strengths according to the Brønsted catalysis law, which states that the logarithm of the rate constant is linearly proportional to the pKa of the catalyst with a slope alpha between 0 and 1. The slope alpha reflects the degree to which the proton has been transferred in the transition state.

Autocatalysis is the unusual situation in which one of the products of a reaction is also a catalyst for that reaction. The result is characteristic sigmoidal kinetics: the reaction starts slowly because little catalyst is present, accelerates as product builds up, and eventually slows as reactant is depleted. Autocatalysis plays a role in acid-catalyzed reactions where acid is a product, in nucleation processes, and in some biological signaling cascades. It is also central to certain oscillating chemical systems and to autocatalytic sets proposed as models for the origin of life, in which a network of molecules collectively catalyzes its own reproduction.
